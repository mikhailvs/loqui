## Spacing effect & spaced-repetition scheduling for L2 vocabulary
**🟢 strong** — The spacing/distributed-practice effect is real and one of the most replicated findings in learning science: spaced study beats massed for long-term retention. Cepeda et al. (2006) synthesized 839 assessments across 317 verbal-recall experiments; spaced beat massed in the large majority of comparisons. Mawson & Kang (2025; 22 reports, 31 effect sizes, N>3000, screened from 3000+ articles) found d=0.54 (95% CI [0.31,0.77]) for distributed over massed in CLASSROOM settings. ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT: the often-quoted single Cepeda-2006 Cohen's d could not be confirmed (the paper reports effects jointly as functions of ISI x retention-interval, not one pooled d); the canonical magnitude is better expressed as a ~9-15% absolute retention advantage. The Mawson & Kang CI lower bound (0.31) and the heterogeneity across the 31 effects mean d=0.54 is a central estimate, not a floor. Effect is robust in DIRECTION but moderate and moderator-dependent in size.
- *Effect:* d=0.54, 95% CI [0.31,0.77] (Mawson & Kang 2025 classroom meta); Cepeda 2006 ~9-15% absolute retention gain; single pooled d unconfirmed
- *Sources:* Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer 2006, Psychological Bulletin 132:354-380 (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16719566); Mawson & Kang 2025, Behavioral Sciences 15:771 (mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/6/771, ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12189222)
- *→ Loop:* Keep Review as a first-class, high-priority drive and add the hard INVARIANT that any introduced item MUST be resurfaced spaced (never massed within one session). This is the best-supported lever in the harness. Demote Drill (massed reps) relative to Review. But size the priority weighting conservatively: the classroom effect is d~0.5 with a real lower-bound near 0.3, so spacing is a strong default, not an overwhelming one that should starve other drives.

**🟢 strong** — Optimal inter-study gap scales with target retention interval: longer desired retention requires longer gaps. Cepeda et al. (2008), ~1350 people, gaps up to 3.5 months, tests to 1 year, found the optimal gap-as-proportion-of-test-delay falls from ~20-40% (1-week target) to ~5-10% (1-year target) — a 'temporal ridgeline,' not a single magic interval. ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT: the ridgeline is broad and flat near its peak (retention is fairly forgiving to gap mis-specification within a wide band), and these are fact-learning lab paradigms; the precise ratios should be treated as order-of-magnitude guidance, not exact targets.
- *Effect:* Optimal gap/retention-interval ratio ~5-20% (declining with longer RI); optimal vs short gap roughly doubled retention in some cells; ridgeline is broad/flat near peak
- *Sources:* Cepeda, Vul, Rohrer, Wixted & Pashler 2008, Psychological Science 19:1095-1102 (laplab.ucsd.edu); files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED505660.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Reject fixed-interval schedules. Compute Review urgency from a per-item retention model parameterized by the course-end horizon (longer horizon -> longer gaps). Invariant: gap to next review grows with item stability, within bounds tied to the horizon. Because the ridgeline is flat near peak, do NOT over-engineer exact gap targeting — getting within the right order of magnitude captures nearly all the benefit.

**🟢 strong** — FSRS (DSR: Difficulty, Stability, Retrievability) is the current state-of-the-art open scheduler and beats SM-2 on benchmarks: on 500M+ Anki reviews / 10k-20k+ users it reaches ~90% retention with ~20-30% FEWER reviews and gives better recall predictions for ~99.5% of users; Anki made it default (v23.10, Nov 2023). ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT: these are EFFICIENCY/PREDICTION-ACCURACY benchmarks on observational Anki logs, NOT randomized learning-outcome trials. The benchmark authors note SM-2 was never designed to output recall probabilities, so the prediction-accuracy comparison is somewhat unfair to SM-2; and the '20-30% fewer reviews' figure comes from simulation/community migration reports, not a controlled student RCT. FSRS is the safe engineering default, but its superiority is 'better-calibrated and more efficient,' not 'proven to produce more learning per unit time in an RCT.'
- *Effect:* FSRS ~20-30% fewer reviews for equal retention vs SM-2 (simulation/migration reports); MAE ~4% vs ~14% for SM-2 on log-loss; better for ~99.5% of users — all observational, not RCT
- *Sources:* open-spaced-repetition/srs-benchmark (github.com/open-spaced-repetition/srs-benchmark); fsrs4anki (github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki); Anki FAQ (faqs.ankiweb.net/what-spaced-repetition-algorithm); Expertium benchmark (expertium.github.io/Benchmark.html)
- *→ Loop:* Spine (Q1): use a retrievability-model scheduler (FSRS/DSR-style or HLR-style) as the Review drive's internal mechanism — NOT a phase machine, NOT SM-2/Leitner. Keep the drive-arbiter as top-level decision-maker but let it consult a DSR-style retrievability estimate to score Review and set the 'overdue within N turns' invariant (N from predicted recall dropping below target, e.g. 0.85-0.90). Because the FSRS edge is efficiency not proven-extra-learning, the win is fewer wasted reviews / better calibration, which is exactly what an arbiter that must budget turns across drives needs.

**🟡 moderate** — For L2 vocabulary specifically, expanding gives at best a SMALL advantage over equal spacing (Nakata 2015, 128 learners x 20 word pairs: expanding>equal by only 4.6-8.5% and only on the delayed receptive posttest), whereas the AMOUNT of spacing produced LARGE effects and massed practice was reliably WORST across conditions. So the big lever is 'space at all / space more,' not 'expand vs equal.' ADDED CRITICAL CAVEAT (explicit-knowledge confound): Nakata & Elgort (2021) found spacing facilitated EXPLICIT vocabulary knowledge (recall/recognition) but did NOT beat massing for TACIT/implicit semantic knowledge (semantic priming) — massed was as good as spaced for the implicit measure. Nakata (2023) further shows RELEARNING/amount of re-exposure can override expand-vs-equal differences. Most spacing evidence measures explicit form-meaning mapping, which is NOT the same as communicative/automatized competence.
- *Effect:* Expanding vs equal: +4.6-8.5% (delayed receptive only, small); amount-of-spacing main effect LARGE; massed reliably worst for EXPLICIT knowledge but EQUAL to spaced for tacit/implicit knowledge (Nakata & Elgort 2021)
- *Sources:* Nakata 2015, SSLA 37:677-711 (doi:10.1017/S0272263114000825); Nakata & Elgort 2021, Second Language Research 37:233-260 (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0267658320927764); Nakata 2023, Language Learning 73 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/lang.12553)
- *→ Loop:* Load-bearing vocab invariant: 'never mass an item' (forbid back-to-back elicit/drill of one lexical item in a session) and ensure ENOUGH total spaced re-exposures. Do NOT spend engineering effort perfecting an expanding curve for vocab. CRITICAL: scope the spacing machinery to EXPLICIT item knowledge (introduce/elicit/review/probe of vocab and set forms). Do not assume spaced retrieval success implies communicative/implicit acquisition — keep a separate signal (Chat-based spontaneous use) for that, since spacing's advantage does not clearly transfer to tacit knowledge. Progress drive should not introduce a new word while a recent one is still shaky.

**🟡 moderate** — Trainable, individualized memory models (half-life regression) outperform static heuristic schedulers on PREDICTION. Duolingo HLR (Settles & Meeder 2016, 13M traces) roughly halved recall-prediction error vs a Leitner baseline and indirectly estimates per-item half-life. ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT (engagement vs learning): the headline PRODUCTION A/B result was a 12% increase in DAILY ENGAGEMENT, NOT a controlled measure of durable learning; the '+9.5% practice-session retention' is a within-product secondary metric, not a delayed-posttest learning-outcome trial. The 'halved error' result is an offline prediction-accuracy benchmark on observational data, a weaker evidence class than the controlled Cepeda/Nakata spacing experiments. HLR has also drawn methodological follow-up critique, and whether it remains Duolingo's production model is unconfirmed.
- *Effect:* HLR prediction error ~half of Leitner (offline, observational); production A/B = +12% daily ENGAGEMENT, +9.5% practice-session retention (secondary metric, not a delayed learning-outcome RCT)
- *Sources:* Settles & Meeder 2016, ACL pp.1848-1858 (research.duolingo.com/papers/settles.acl16.pdf); github.com/duolingo/halflife-regression; papousek.github.io/analysis-of-half-life-regression-model-made-by-duolingo.html
- *→ Loop:* The Review drive should weight each item by estimated probability of recall NOW (retrievability) from a per-item memory model (store last_seen, recall_history, estimated stability/half-life), not by a fixed turn-counter. This is the right architecture. BUT do not treat HLR's engagement-driven production numbers as proof of better learning — validate the harness's scheduler against DELAYED retrieval success, never against in-session engagement or immediate performance.

**🟡 moderate** — The benefit of spacing grows with the retention interval and is largest when re-exposures are few — spacing matters MORE for durable, long-horizon learning, and you don't need many repetitions to capture most of the benefit. Mawson & Kang (2025) found larger effects with longer retention intervals, higher-level learners, and FEWER re-exposures. CAVEAT: 'fewer re-exposures -> larger effect' is partly a ceiling artifact (with many reps both massed and spaced approach high retention, shrinking the gap) — it does not mean few reps are sufficient for mastery, only that the spacing ADVANTAGE is most visible there.
- *Effect:* Moderators: effect increases with retention interval and learner level; decreases with number of re-exposures (partly a ceiling effect)
- *Sources:* Mawson & Kang 2025, Behavioral Sciences 15:771 (mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/6/771); Cepeda et al. 2008 (laplab.ucsd.edu)
- *→ Loop:* Bias Consolidate/Review toward spreading a FIXED re-exposure budget across many sessions rather than cramming reps. The lesson ARC should resurface prior-session items in warm-up (spaced across days) rather than repeating same-session items. Set target retention threshold per course horizon: longer course -> schedule for longer half-lives even at some short-term-test cost. But do not under-provision total reps for hard items — the 'fewer reps' moderator is about effect visibility, not about minimum reps for mastery.

**🟡 moderate** — Drill (massed varied repetition within a session) is the weakest-supported move for LONG-TERM retention: meta-analytically, massing is the inferior condition, and in-session repetition produces high immediate performance but poorer durable retention (Nakata 2015; Cepeda 2006). In-session fluency systematically OVERESTIMATES learning. CAVEAT: massing is inferior for EXPLICIT durable retention; for some tacit/implicit measures massing was not worse (Nakata & Elgort 2021), and Drill may still have a legitimate role for initial encoding, fluency/automatization practice, and learner confidence — its weakness is specifically as a long-term-retention mechanism, not as a zero-value move.
- *Effect:* Massed reliably worst for explicit long-term retention (Nakata 2015; Cepeda 2006); immediate-vs-delayed crossover documented; massing NOT worse on some implicit measures
- *Sources:* Nakata 2015 (cambridge.org); Cepeda et al. 2006 (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16719566); Karpicke & Roediger 2007 (purdue.edu); Nakata & Elgort 2021 (journals.sagepub.com)
- *→ Loop:* Invariant: cap consecutive same-item exposures (no Drill repeating one item >k times/session). Reframe Drill from 'massed reps now' to 'enqueue spaced reps.' When tempted to Drill a shaky item, prefer recast/elicit now + schedule a Review later. CRITICAL: do NOT use performance-during-practice as the learner-model success signal — use spaced delayed-retrieval success, since immediate fluency overestimates learning. Retain Drill as an optional fluency/automatization move but never as the primary durable-retention mechanism.

**🟠 contested** — Expanding intervals are NOT universally superior to equal/uniform intervals — contradicting popular SRS folk wisdom. Karpicke & Roediger (2007): expanding helped at 10 min but EQUAL spacing gave better retention at 2 days; the literature is genuinely mixed (some find expanding>equal, some null, some equal>expanding). ADDED EVIDENCE: Karpicke & Roediger Exp 3 found the placement/DIFFICULTY of the FIRST retrieval attempt matters more than the expand-vs-equal schedule of later attempts — delaying/making the first test effortful improved long-term retention regardless of subsequent schedule. The most-cited justification for SM-2-style ever-expanding ladders is weaker than commonly believed.
- *Effect:* Karpicke & Roediger 2007: equal>expanding at 2-day delay (crossover); first-retrieval difficulty dominates relative-spacing schedule; no single dominant schedule across studies
- *Sources:* Karpicke & Roediger 2007, JEP:LMC 33:704-719 (learninglab.psych.purdue.edu); Storm, Bjork & Storm 2010, Memory & Cognition 38:244 (link.springer.com/article/10.3758/MC.38.2.244)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT hard-code an expanding schedule as an invariant. Treat expand-vs-equal as a tunable policy, defaulting toward equal/uniform for items that must survive to a distant test; expand only once an item shows high stability. ADD: make the FIRST review of a new item deliberately delayed/effortful (not an easy immediate re-test) — this is better-evidenced than the choice of later-interval shape. Let a retrievability model pick timing rather than a baked-in ladder.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Expanding (ever-increasing) intervals are inherently superior — the SM-2 / SuperMemo 'always expand' ladder is the right default. — Contested in the lab (Karpicke & Roediger 2007 found EQUAL spacing beat expanding at a 2-day delay; the broader literature is mixed with null and reverse effects) and only a very small advantage for L2 vocab (Nakata 2015: +4.6-8.5%, delayed receptive only). Karpicke & Roediger Exp 3 shows the difficulty/placement of the FIRST retrieval matters more than the expand-vs-equal shape. Expanding is a tunable policy, not a justified hard invariant.
- Spaced retrieval success demonstrates real language acquisition (i.e., a high SRS recall rate = the learner has 'learned' the item for communicative use). — Spacing's measured benefit is largely on EXPLICIT form-meaning recall/recognition. Nakata & Elgort (2021) found spacing did NOT outperform massing for TACIT/implicit semantic knowledge (semantic priming). Most spacing meta-analytic evidence is verbal/fact recall and paired-associates; transfer to spontaneous production, grammar, and conversation is far less evidenced. SRS success is a proxy for explicit knowledge, not automatized communicative competence.
- In-session drilling/repetition that produces high immediate accuracy means the material is being learned well. — Massed in-session practice reliably yields high IMMEDIATE performance but poorer DURABLE retention than the same reps spaced out (Cepeda 2006; Nakata 2015). Performance-during-practice systematically overestimates learning; only delayed/spaced retrieval success is a valid learning signal.
- FSRS/HLR being 'better' is proven to produce more learning, and adopting an adaptive scheduler is itself an evidence-based learning-outcome win. — FSRS-vs-SM-2 results are efficiency and prediction-accuracy benchmarks on observational Anki logs (and the comparison is partly unfair since SM-2 wasn't built to output probabilities); '20-30% fewer reviews' is simulation/migration-derived. Duolingo HLR's headline production result is a +12% ENGAGEMENT lift, not a controlled durable-learning outcome. These tools are well-justified engineering defaults but their superiority is calibration/efficiency, not a randomized learning-outcome demonstration.
- There is a single optimal spacing interval (a 'magic number' gap) to schedule reviews at. — Cepeda et al. (2008) show the optimal gap scales with the target retention interval (a temporal ridgeline, ~5-20% of the retention interval, declining as the horizon lengthens) and the ridgeline is broad/flat near its peak. Any fixed-interval constant is wrong by construction; timing must be a function of retention target and per-item stability.

## Retrieval practice / testing effect (test-enhanced learning) for an L2 language harness
**🟢 strong** — The testing effect is real and replicated meta-analytically: retrieval practice beats restudy for long-term retention. Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan (2017): g≈0.51 for practice testing vs restudy (g≈0.93 vs no-activity filler); Rowland (2014): g=0.50 for testing vs restudy. The advantage emerges on DELAYED tests, not immediate ones (Roediger & Karpicke 2006: at 5 min restudy won; at 2 days/1 week testing won). VERIFIED: g=0.51, 0.93, MC>SA all confirmed in Adesope; Rowland g=0.50 confirmed; R&K crossover confirmed (STTT beat SSSS by ~21% at 1 week, but trailed at 5 min). CAVEAT ADDED (generalization): the foundational evidence pools predominantly L1, undergraduate, prose/verbal-list materials. R&K 2006 specifically is free-recall of English prose by L1 undergrads — NOT L2 acquisition. The pooled effect is robust as a memory-of-declarative-items phenomenon; its transfer to L2 productive/implicit competence (spontaneous use, not recall-on-cue) is an extrapolation, not a measured quantity. This is test-of-explicit-memory evidence; treat it as validating retrieval for FORM/ITEM retention (vocab, set phrases, paradigm slots), not as proof it builds communicative fluency.
- *Effect:* g=0.51 vs restudy, g=0.93 vs filler (Adesope 2017, 272 effects/188 exps); g=0.50 (Rowland 2014); R&K 2006 ~21% advantage at 1wk. L2-specific pooled effect thinner and more variable.
- *Sources:* Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan 2017, Review of Educational Research (10.3102/0034654316689306); Rowland 2014, Psychological Bulletin (PMID 25150680); Roediger & Karpicke 2006, Psychological Science 17(3):249-255
- *→ Loop:* Make elicit/probe the DEFAULT consolidation move over re-presentation FOR ITEM/FORM retention. Review drive should schedule retrieval moves (elicit/probe), not passive re-input, for any encoded item. Add invariant: 'a scheduled review of an encoded item is realized as a retrieval move, never passive re-input, unless flagged shaky.' Measure success on DELAYED resurfacing, not same-session accuracy. CAUTION: do not assume retrieval-of-items equals fluency; keep a separate communicative-use signal in the learner model so the harness does not optimize delayed recall at the expense of spontaneous production (chat/elicit-in-context).

**🟢 strong** — Retrieval practice is strongly condition-dependent and CAN backfire when the item is not yet encoded. The effect collapses (near-zero) when retrievability is low AND no feedback is given. Premature retrieval on poorly-encoded items wastes the turn and risks error consolidation; supported sequence is input->processing->rehearsal->retrieval, with retrieval as CONSOLIDATOR of established representations, not the encoding engine. VERIFIED & STRENGTHENED: Rowland and the Schwieren classroom study (PMC6288371) both confirm the testing effect appears ONLY for high-retrievability items (their high band 46-81% correct) and is null for medium/low retrievability (both p>0.74). This is the most robustly replicated moderator in the set.
- *Effect:* Near-zero testing effect at low/medium retrievability without feedback (Rowland 2014; Schwieren et al. 2018: interaction low-vs-high beta=-0.60 p=.016, med-vs-high beta=-0.66 p=.030); vs g=0.50 overall
- *Sources:* Rowland 2014, Psychological Bulletin (PMID 25150680); Schwieren, Barenberg & Dutke 2018, Frontiers in Psychology (PMC6288371) — classroom replication of the retrievability moderator; Karpicke & Roediger 2007, J. Memory & Language
- *→ Loop:* Validates 'don't introduce while current item is shaky' and demands the symmetric guardrail: add invariant 'never select elicit/probe on an item below an encoding threshold (e.g. <1 successful prior exposure OR learner-model encoding-strength < theta); fall back to input/recast/drill.' Arbiter gates Review/Progress retrieval moves on an encoding-strength check. This is the single most important guardrail — it converts the testing effect from a footgun into a tool. Folk 'always quiz' is unsupported for un-encoded material. Note Conti 2026 is a non-peer-reviewed synthesis; the guardrail stands on Rowland + the Schwieren peer-reviewed classroom replication, so do not lean on Conti as primary.

**🟢 strong** — Feedback after retrieval substantially amplifies the effect and is near-mandatory in hard/L2 conditions. Rowland (2014): intermediate testing WITH feedback g=0.73 vs WITHOUT g=0.39. Feedback's main role is rescuing failed retrievals and preventing error consolidation. VERIFIED: 0.73 vs 0.39 confirmed. CAVEAT ADDED: these specific numbers come from Rowland's intermediate/initial-test feedback contrast on largely L1 materials; the feedback-presence benefit is directionally robust and corroborated in L2 (retrieval+feedback > retrieval-alone for L2/L3 vocab), but the exact 0.73/0.39 gap should not be cited as an L2 constant.
- *Effect:* g=0.73 (with feedback) vs g=0.39 (without), Rowland 2014; direction corroborated in L2/L3 vocab studies (retrieval+feedback > retrieval-alone)
- *Sources:* Rowland 2014, Psychological Bulletin (PMID 25150680); Zhang/Nakata-line L2 retrieval-practice-plus-feedback studies (e.g. Int. J. Multilingualism 2024, 10.1080/14790718.2022.2102172)
- *→ Loop:* Add invariant: 'every elicit/probe is followed by a feedback move within the same or next turn.' Arbiter treats elicit+feedback as a coupled macro, not two independent turns — a Repair/recast/correct move must be guaranteed reachable after any retrieval. On a FAILED retrieval, feedback supplies the correct form (recast or correct), not just 'try again.' Feedback is a hard part of the retrieval contract, not an Engage-drive afterthought. This is high-value and well-supported; prioritize it.

**🟡 moderate** — Recall (production) retrieval tends to beat recognition for retention, and more generative retrieval yields larger durable gains. Rowland (2014): initial recall tests yielded larger benefits than recognition. Generation effect (Bertsch et al. 2007, 86 studies, 445 effects) d=0.40 overall. VERIFIED: Bertsch d=0.40 confirmed; Rowland recall>recognition direction confirmed. DOWNGRADED to moderate and CORRECTED: the specific 'd=0.64 at >1-day delay' figure could not be confirmed as a headline Bertsch number in verification (Bertsch's headline is d=0.40 across 86 studies; delay was one of 11 moderators but the 0.64-at-delay value is not reliably attributable) — treat the delay-amplification as plausible-but-unverified, not a citable constant. More importantly, recall>recognition is CONFOUNDED with retrievability/success rate (recognition items are easier, so they sit higher in the desirable band) and directly conflicts with format findings below — so 'production wins' is a tendency, not a law.
- *Effect:* Generation effect d=0.40 overall (Bertsch 2007, VERIFIED). 'd=0.64 at >1-day delay' NOT verified as a headline number — drop or hedge. recall>recognition direction robust but success-rate-confounded.
- *Sources:* Bertsch, Pesta, Wiscott & McDaniel 2007, Memory & Cognition (PMID 17645161); Rowland 2014, Psychological Bulletin
- *→ Loop:* Prefer PRODUCTIVE elicit (cued recall, fill-the-gap, translate-into-L2) over recognition probe ONCE the item is past the encoding threshold AND predicted success stays in the desirable band. Split 'probe' into recognition-probe (cheap, for borderline/just-encoded items) vs production-elicit (retention workhorse for solid items). Do NOT treat production as universally superior — it is superior CONDITIONAL on success rate, so couple the format choice to the difficulty controller below rather than asserting it standalone.

**🟡 moderate** — There is an optimal retrieval DIFFICULTY: roughly 60-80% success. Too easy gives little storage-strength gain; too hard produces guessing, disengagement, entrenched error. Effortful-but-successful retrieval maximizes storage-strength gain (desirable difficulty / Bjork). EVIDENCE-STATUS CLARIFIED: the retrievability-matters part is now meta-analytically + classroom supported (Rowland; Schwieren). But the SPECIFIC 60-80% band is NOT a pooled meta-analytic constant — it is a convergence of desirable-difficulty theory (Bjork, strong theory + many lab studies, no single pooled d) and practitioner guidance (incl. non-peer-reviewed Conti). Keep as moderate.
- *Effect:* Target ~70-80% success: practitioner/lab convergence + retrievability-moderator meta-evidence (Rowland; Schwieren high band 46-81%). NOT a single pooled meta-analytic d — tunable hyperparameter.
- *Sources:* Bjork & Bjork desirable difficulties / new theory of disuse; Rowland 2014 (retrievability moderator); Schwieren et al. 2018 (PMC6288371, high-retrievability-only effect)
- *→ Loop:* Empirical anchor for Q5 (i+1 calibration). Make the arbiter a DIFFICULTY CONTROLLER: track per-item retrieval success in the learner model; select moves to keep predicted success ~70-80%. Add invariant: 'do not select a retrieval move with predicted success <~0.5 (drop to input/recast) or >~0.95 (over-learned — lengthen the review interval / raise difficulty).' Treat the exact band edges as tunable hyperparameters, not empirical constants; instrument them so they can be A/B-calibrated on the real learner population.

**🟡 moderate** — Feedback TIMING (immediate vs delayed) is NOT a decisive lever — presence of feedback matters far more than timing. Kulik & Kulik (1988) favored immediate in classrooms, delayed in lab list-learning; recent work finds them comparable. For L2 specifically, Nakata (2015) found feedback-timing effects were null overall, with only a tiny d=0.14 advantage for immediate on receptive recall at 1 week. VERIFIED & STRENGTHENED: the d=0.14 belongs to Nakata's L2 feedback-TIMING contrast and confirms timing is a low-value lever in L2; it does NOT undercut the feedback-PRESENCE finding.
- *Effect:* No reliable directional timing advantage; L2 timing effect d=0.14 (Nakata 2015, receptive recall, 1wk) — negligible vs presence-of-feedback gap (g=0.73 vs 0.39)
- *Sources:* Kulik & Kulik 1988 meta-analysis; Nakata 2015 (L2 vocabulary, feedback timing) ; Rowland 2014
- *→ Loop:* Resolves Q2 partly: do NOT over-engineer immediate-vs-delayed feedback scheduling — low-value fork. Spend budget on GUARANTEEING feedback after every retrieval (high-value invariant) and on recast-vs-explicit choice. Default: immediate corrective feedback in a real-time conversational turn (keeps loop simple, matches classroom evidence); allow an optional delayed cool-down recap as a cheap bonus, not core mechanism. (Upgraded reliability of the underlying numbers from 'contested' to 'moderate' — the timing-is-low-value conclusion is well-supported even though the lab-vs-classroom pattern is mixed.)

**🟠 contested** — Format effects are genuinely CONTESTED and the literature actively conflicts. Adesope (2017) found multiple-choice practice had a LARGER mean effect (g=0.70) than short-answer (g=0.48) on final tests. BUT Schwieren et al. (2018), a real university-teaching study, found the OPPOSITE: short-answer produced a testing effect (beta=0.44, p=.033) while multiple-choice produced NO effect (beta=-0.42, p=.078), attributing it to MC requiring too little retrieval effort. This is a direct, head-to-head contradiction — not a minor tension. Best read: format interacts with retrievability and effort; MC helps when it raises low-retrievability items into the success band, but offers little when items are already retrievable because it removes the effortful generation that drives the effect.
- *Effect:* Adesope 2017: MC g=0.70 > short-answer g=0.48. DIRECTLY CONTRADICTED by Schwieren 2018: short-answer beta=0.44 (sig) vs MC beta=-0.42 (n.s./negative). Net: no settled format winner.
- *Sources:* Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan 2017, Review of Educational Research; Schwieren, Barenberg & Dutke 2018, Frontiers in Psychology (PMC6288371); Rowland 2014, Psychological Bulletin
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT hard-code format either way — and specifically do NOT hard-code 'MC for marginal items' on the strength of Adesope alone, because the classroom replication reverses it. Let the arbiter pick format by encoding strength AND treat format as a difficulty-matched ladder under the success-rate controller: recognition/MC-probe only to lift genuinely marginal items into the success band; production-elicit once solidly encoded. Log format-vs-outcome so the harness can learn the right policy empirically rather than betting on either meta-analysis. Flag as an open empirical tension in the design caveats.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Re-presentation / restudy as the primary consolidation move ('show it again to make it stick'). — Roediger & Karpicke 2006 + Adesope/Rowland meta-analyses: restudy raises immediate accuracy and subjective confidence but does NOT improve delayed retention relative to retrieval; it loses decisively at 2 days / 1 week. The folk move optimizes the wrong (immediate, confidence) signal. Exception: un-encoded items, where input/recast IS correct.
- 'Always quiz' — retrieval practice is universally beneficial, so test early and often. — Rowland 2014 and Schwieren et al. 2018 (classroom) both show the testing effect is NULL for low/medium-retrievability items without feedback (p>.74). Quizzing un-encoded material wastes turns and can consolidate errors. Retrieval is a consolidator, not an encoder.
- 'Production always beats recognition' as a hard rule for choosing exercise format. — The recall>recognition direction (Rowland) is confounded with retrievability/success rate and is directly contradicted on format by both Adesope (MC>short-answer) and, in the opposite direction, Schwieren (short-answer>MC). There is no settled format winner independent of item difficulty; treating production as categorically superior is unsupported.
- Feedback timing must be carefully engineered (immediate vs delayed scheduling is a meaningful pedagogical lever). — Kulik & Kulik (mixed lab/classroom) and Nakata 2015 (L2, timing effect d=0.14, mostly null) show timing is near-irrelevant compared to feedback PRESENCE (g=0.73 vs 0.39). Engineering a delayed-feedback scheduler is low-value folk sophistication; the real lever is guaranteeing feedback exists at all.
- The testing effect demonstrates that retrieval practice builds L2 communicative competence / fluency. — The meta-analytic evidence measures delayed recall of declarative items (vocab, facts, prose) in mostly L1 undergraduates. That is acquisition of retrievable FORM/ITEM knowledge, not spontaneous communicative use. Equating delayed item-recall gains with fluency is engagement/explicit-knowledge being sold as acquisition; transfer to communicative competence is an untested extrapolation.
- The 60-80% success target is an established empirical constant. — It is a convergence of desirable-difficulty theory (Bjork) and practitioner guidance, not a pooled meta-analytic d. The general 'success-rate matters' claim is supported (Rowland, Schwieren); the precise band is a tunable hyperparameter, and citing it as a fixed empirical number overstates the evidence.

## Corrective feedback in SLA (oral & written) — informing Q2 (recast vs correct moves, timing)
**🟢 strong** — Oral corrective feedback (CF) produces a medium effect that is durable at delayed posttest; CF effects are LARGER in foreign-language (instructed, lower-input) than in second-language (immersion) contexts. ADVERSARIAL CAVEATS NOW ATTACHED: (a) Li (2010) also found LAB-based effects > CLASSROOM-based effects, and SHORTER treatments (<=50 min, d=0.92) > longer treatments (60-120 min, d=0.63). The short-treatment/lab advantage is a warning sign that part of the pooled effect reflects test-proximate, novelty-driven gains rather than durable classroom acquisition — the more ecologically valid (classroom) numbers are the smaller ones, and a 1:1 LLM tutor running long sessions is the LESS-favorable cell. (b) The pooled d values aggregate heterogeneous outcome measures dominated by grammaticality-judgment and metalinguistic tests, which the SLA literature itself flags as favoring EXPLICIT knowledge over acquired implicit knowledge; so 'medium durable LEARNING effect' should be read as 'medium durable effect on largely explicit-knowledge measures.' (c) FL d=0.74 vs L2 d=0.41 is corroborated in direction but magnitudes are mostly within-group/pre-post and ordinal only.
- *Effect:* Li 2010 meta (33 studies): overall medium effect, durable; FL > L2; lab > classroom; short treatments d=0.92 > long d=0.63 (the often-quoted overall d~0.64 is consistent with the source but magnitudes are inflated by within-group designs). Mackey & Goo 2007: interaction/CF d=0.71 immediate, larger delayed; FL > SL.
- *Sources:* Li, S. (2010). The Effectiveness of Corrective Feedback in SLA: A Meta-Analysis. Language Learning 60:309-365 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9922.2010.00561.x; eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ883422); Mackey & Goo (2007). Interaction research in SLA: A meta-analysis and research synthesis (semanticscholar.org paper 2b270fee)
- *→ Loop:* Keep correct/recast/prompt as first-class moves and let a Repair drive fire confidently — CF beats no-CF on the available evidence. BUT weight the implication conservatively: because the favorable cells are lab + short + explicit-knowledge measures, treat Repair as reliably improving DECLARATIVE/explicit control of rule-based forms, not as a guaranteed driver of spontaneous production. The FL>SL result still justifies an invariant that overdue repairs on shaky items get surfaced within N turns rather than left to incidental input. Add a measurement-skeptic invariant: the learner model should distinguish 'passes a constrained/judgment probe' from 'produces the form unprompted in chat' and not mark an item acquired on the former alone.

**🟢 strong** — PROMPTS (elicit self-correction: clarification request, metalinguistic cue, repetition) outperform RECASTS on durable gains and especially on free-production measures, because they push learner-generated modified output; explicit correction sits between. ADVERSARIAL CAVEATS: these are WITHIN-GROUP (pre-post) effect sizes from 15 classroom studies, so magnitudes are inflated relative to between-group contrasts and should be treated as ORDINAL (prompts > explicit > recasts), not absolute. The result is robust for human classroom oral CF but its transfer to a 1:1 TEXT LLM tutor is an extrapolation: no meta-analysis tests LLM-delivered CF, and the prompts>recasts logic rests on output-pushing/noticing mechanisms that a text chat may realize differently (e.g., a typed clarification request has different salience than a spoken one).
- *Effect:* Lyster & Saito 2010 (15 classroom studies, N=827): within-group d — prompts ~1.14, recasts ~0.70, explicit correction ~0.60; effects durable; largest on free-response measures. (Direction corroborated; explicit-correction figures vary by analysis, with some sources reporting explicit ~0.81 — treat the prompts>recasts ordering as the robust result, not the exact gaps.)
- *Sources:* Lyster & Saito (2010). Oral Feedback in Classroom SLA: A Meta-Analysis. Studies in SLA 32:265-302 (eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ892626; cambridge.org/core .../4999EE1C8379B2BF026B148EAF373CA1)
- *→ Loop:* Add a distinct ELICIT-REPAIR / prompt move (clarification request or metalinguistic cue that WITHHOLDS the answer) and make it the DEFAULT Repair realization, ranked above recast and above explicit correct. Arbiter rule: when an item is already introduced and only shaky (not absent), prefer prompt > recast > explicit-correct; reserve full correct (form+rule) for repeated failed prompts. Do NOT treat recast-only as the gold standard — that is the unsupported default of conversation-driven apps. Because the LLM-text transfer is unvalidated, instrument this as an A/B-able policy, not a hard-wired truth: log uptake (did the learner produce the modified form?) per realization so the harness can empirically check whether prompts actually beat recasts in THIS modality.

**🟡 moderate** — RECASTS are pedagogically weak as a PRIMARY repair tool due to low salience/ambiguity: learners often miss the corrective intent and confuse recasts with non-corrective repetition. They function as comprehensible positive evidence, not reliable error treatment. Intensive/multiple recasts and recasts on developmentally-ready learners are more noticed. (Strength held at moderate: no single pooled d; recast d~0.70 is real but smallest; the salience claim is qualitative/converging across noticing studies and the exact moderators are contested.)
- *Effect:* Lyster & Saito recast within-group d~0.70 (real but smallest of the three); salience/ambiguity effect is qualitative across noticing studies; single-vs-multiple recast moderators contested.
- *Sources:* Lyster & Saito (2010) (as above); Recast salience/ambiguity & noticing review; single vs multiple recasts (davidpublisher.com pdf; sciencedirect S2405844018384895)
- *→ Loop:* If the harness uses recast, make it SALIENT: repeat the corrected form, optionally highlight/stress the changed token, or pair with a follow-up elicit so the learner must reproduce it. Invariant: a recast NOT followed within K turns by an elicit on the same form does NOT count as 'repair delivered' — log it as input, not repair, so the Repair drive doesn't prematurely mark the item treated. Gate reliance on recast behind a developmental-readiness/level check. In TEXT, raise salience further (formatting the changed token) since the prosodic salience available in speech is absent.

**🟡 moderate** — FOCUSED WCF (one/few targeted structures + metalinguistic explanation) outperforms unfocused/comprehensive error marking; direct correction + metalinguistic explanation beats direct-only — but intensive focus on one feature can DEGRADE accuracy on non-targeted features (spillover cost). CAVEAT: this rests largely on the Bitchener & Knoch line (English articles a/the with advanced writers) plus replications — a narrow feature and population — not a broad pooled effect; generalization to other structures and lower proficiencies is an inference.
- *Effect:* Bitchener & Knoch (2010): all WCF groups > control on targeted English articles at immediate and delayed posttest; direct+metalinguistic > direct-only; documented spillover cost to non-targeted article functions. Robust single study-set + replication, not a pooled d; narrow to article system and advanced learners.
- *Sources:* Bitchener & Knoch (2010). Raising linguistic accuracy of advanced L2 writers with WCF (sciencedirect S1060374310000421); Conceptual replication of Bitchener & Knoch 2010 (cambridge.org/.../07286C9DCBD22FC15910CB24DB597B1C)
- *→ Loop:* Make correct/explain moves FOCUSED by invariant: at most one target structure per turn, drawn from the current or a flagged-overdue item — never bulk-correct an utterance. Pair direct correction with a brief metalinguistic note. Add a spillover-guard invariant: after intensive focus on feature X, schedule a Review/probe on adjacent uses of the same form so the arbiter can detect regression. Treat the strength of this as 'good default heuristic' rather than 'established law,' given the narrow evidence base.

**🟠 contested** — The blanket 'never correct grammar in writing' position (Truscott 1996) is NOT supported; written CF improves grammatical accuracy on targeted, rule-based features. The effect is smaller than oral CF, concentrated on treatable features, and LARGER in SL than FL settings (opposite to oral CF). ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT ADDED: the Kang & Han (2015) meta-analysis that anchors this has a PUBLISHED METHODOLOGICAL CRITIQUE challenging its inclusion criteria, control-group handling, and effect-size aggregation; and the Truscott/Ferris debate's open question — whether WCF transfers to accuracy in NEW pieces of writing rather than revisions of the same text — remains unresolved. So 'WCF works' is best stated as 'WCF improves accuracy on targeted rule-based features within the treated context,' with transfer to new spontaneous writing weak/debated.
- *Effect:* Kang & Han 2015 meta (21 studies): overall g=0.54, CI[0.35,0.72]; SL > FL; proficiency the strongest moderator (larger effects at higher proficiency). Effect and conclusions challenged by a published critique of the meta-analysis. Truscott's no-benefit claim is the minority/refuted position; transfer-to-new-writing debate ongoing.
- *Sources:* Kang & Han (2015). Efficacy of WCF in Improving L2 Written Accuracy: A Meta-Analysis. Modern Language Journal 99 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/modl.12189); Critique: The Efficacy of Written Corrective Feedback: A Critique of a Meta-analysis (researchgate 341453398); Truscott (1996) vs Ferris (1999) debate (sciencedirect S1060374399801106)
- *→ Loop:* Written/text repair is justified for the text-mode harness — do not adopt the 'never correct' folk position. But because WCF gains are smaller, feature-bound, and of contested generalization, the explain/correct move on writing should target ONE rule-based feature per turn, tied to the learner-model's 'treatable rule' tag; skip CF on idiosyncratic/lexical errors that don't generalize. Crucially, since transfer-to-new-text is the weak link, the learner model should verify a corrected feature on a FRESH production (a later, different utterance), not just on an immediate revision, before marking it learned.

**🟠 contested** — TIMING (immediate vs delayed CF) has NO universal winner. Slight edge for immediate with explicit/declarative knowledge and text/SCMC feedback; delayed lowers interruption/anxiety and preserves fluency; 24h+ delays usually show no difference. The evidence is genuinely mixed (vote-counting reviews, small single-lab samples), so do not over-engineer timing logic.
- *Effect:* Li, Zhu & Ellis 2016 (N=120 EFL): immediate > delayed on grammaticality-judgment (explicit); NO CF effect on elicited imitation (implicit) — i.e., the timing edge appears only on the explicit-knowledge measure; delayed gains not retained. Arroyo & Yilmaz 2018 (N=45, SCMC): immediate > delayed in oral production. Frontiers 2023 review (20 studies): immediate favored ~50%, no diff ~35%, delayed ~15%; no pooled effect sizes.
- *Sources:* Li, Zhu & Ellis (2016). Modern Language Journal 100:276-295 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/modl.12315); Arroyo & Yilmaz (2018). Language Learning 68 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/lang.12300); Optimal timing of CF: systematic review, Frontiers in Psychology 2023 (frontiersin.org/.../fpsyg.2023.1026174/full)
- *→ Loop:* Do not hard-code one timing; make it a per-move arbiter property, but keep the logic SIMPLE given weak evidence. For explicit/rule learning of a new/current item, allow immediate correct. For fluency/chat moves, default to DELAYED repair buffered to an end-of-turn/end-of-arc recap (maps to cool-down). Invariant: never interrupt a chat/free-production move mid-utterance with explicit correction; queue it for consolidate/cool-down. Note the Li-Zhu-Ellis caveat directly: immediate's advantage showed up only on explicit-knowledge tests and not on the implicit measure, so do not over-invest engineering in fine-grained timing — it is a low-confidence knob.

**⚪ weak** — Feedback type/timing MAY be conditioned on learner anxiety: high-anxiety learners benefit relatively more from recasts/delayed (less interruption); low-anxiety from metalinguistic/explicit/immediate; mid-task immediate explicit correction can induce speech freezing. ADVERSARIAL DOWNGRADE: this rests almost entirely on a single author's line (Rassaei) with small samples (~N=100, classified into anxiety subgroups so cell sizes are smaller), single L1/setting (Iranian EFL), and a mix of DEVELOPMENT studies with PREFERENCE/PERCEPTION studies (learners' stated preferences are not learning outcomes). Treat as a plausible affect heuristic, not an established interaction effect.
- *Effect:* Rassaei 2015 (N~101 EFL split into high/low anxiety x recast/metalinguistic/control): FLA moderated CF — low-anxiety gained more from metalinguistic, high-anxiety from recasts; single-lab. Rassaei 2023/2024 extend to timing and online interaction, same lab. No multi-lab replication or meta-analysis; some cited findings are about learner PREFERENCES, not gains.
- *Sources:* Rassaei (2015). Oral CF, foreign language anxiety and L2 development (sciencedirect S0346251X15000032); Rassaei (2023). Interplay between CF timing and FLA. Language Teaching Research (journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/13621688231195141); Rassaei (2024). Recasts, FLA and L2 development during online mobile-mediated interaction (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13621688241238045)
- *→ Loop:* Add an anxiety/affect state to the learner model and let the Engage drive read it, but treat the affect->feedback mapping as a SOFT, reversible bias, not a hard pedagogy invariant, given the single-lab evidence. Soft policy: when affect=high (signals: short/declining output, withdrawal, repeated errors after correction), shift Repair toward recast + delayed and suppress immediate explicit correct; when affect=low/stable, permit immediate metalinguistic correct. KEEP 'no explicit correction while affect=high' as an invariant — but justify it on the GENERAL, better-supported grounds that mid-utterance interruption harms fluency/engagement (and engagement is necessary for continued input), NOT on the specific Rassaei anxiety-x-type interaction, which is too thin to bear an invariant by itself.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Recast-only correction (modeling the correct form without flagging the error) is the safe, sufficient way to handle learner errors — the default of most conversation-driven language apps. — Lyster & Saito (2010) put recasts as the WEAKEST of the three feedback types (within-group d~0.70 vs prompts ~1.14), and noticing research shows learners frequently fail to perceive recasts as corrective and confuse them with mere repetition. Recasts function as positive input, not reliable error treatment. Treating recast-only as gold standard is folk wisdom inherited from immersion/communicative ideology, not from the comparative CF evidence.
- You should never correct grammar in writing (Truscott 1996) because it doesn't help and may harm. — This is the minority/refuted position. Kang & Han (2015, g=0.54) and the Bitchener & Knoch line show written CF improves accuracy on targeted rule-based features. The legitimate residual debate is narrow (transfer to NEW writing, and a methodological critique of the meta-analysis), not the blanket 'never correct' claim.
- There is an optimal moment (always immediate, or always delayed) to deliver corrective feedback. — No universal winner exists: the 2023 systematic review splits roughly 50/35/15 (immediate/no-diff/delayed), and the immediate edge in Li, Zhu & Ellis (2016) appeared only on an explicit-knowledge judgment test, not on the implicit measure. Believing in a single optimal timing is folk precision unsupported by the mixed evidence.
- Effect sizes like d=0.64 / d=1.14 demonstrate that CF causes durable ACQUISITION of the language. — Most are within-group/pre-post (inflated vs between-group) and are measured with grammaticality-judgment and metalinguistic tests that the SLA literature itself flags as indexing EXPLICIT/declarative knowledge, not acquired implicit ability to produce forms spontaneously. The magnitudes support improved explicit control of rule-based forms on test-proximate measures, not a proven jump in free production — and lab>classroom plus short>long-treatment patterns hint at novelty/test artifacts.
- We can reliably match feedback type to a learner's anxiety level (recasts for anxious learners, metalinguistic for calm ones) as a core pedagogy rule. — This interaction comes essentially from one author's single-lab line (Rassaei) with small subdivided samples and a single L1/setting, partly built on stated learner PREFERENCES rather than learning gains, with no multi-lab replication or meta-analysis. It is too thin to anchor a hard invariant; usable only as a soft, reversible bias.

## Comprehensible input hypothesis & i+1
**🟢 strong** — Comprehensible input is NECESSARY but NOT SUFFICIENT. Incidental acquisition from input is real but slow/unreliable per encounter, especially for low-frequency forms and non-salient grammar; output and feedback add measurable value. NOTE: the researcher's cited '24 studies, 29 ES, N=2,771' incidental-vocab figure does not match the canonical repetition meta-analysis — Uchihara, Webb & Yanagisawa (2019, Language Learning) synthesized 26 studies / 45 ES / N=1,918 and found repetition–learning r=0.34 (medium, correlational, NOT causal). Many encounters are needed; per-exposure pickup is modest.
- *Effect:* Repetition–incidental-vocab r=0.34 (Uchihara et al. 2019, correlational). Interaction (input+feedback+output bundled) d=0.71 immediate / d=1.09 DELAYED (Mackey & Goo 2007) exceeds input-only. Researcher's specific incidental-vocab N/study counts corrected.
- *Sources:* Uchihara, Webb & Yanagisawa 2019, Language Learning 69(3) (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/lang.12343); Mackey & Goo 2007 interaction meta-analysis; Cambridge 'How effective is L2 incidental vocabulary learning? A meta-analysis' (Uchihara/Webb 2023)
- *→ Loop:* Reject a pure input-driven spine for Q3. Do not let `input`/`chat` monopolize turns. Keep `elicit`, `recast`, `drill`, `correct` load-bearing. Invariant: any item needing production mastery must accrue elicit/output turns, not only input exposures. Also: because per-exposure vocab pickup is low (r=0.34, correlational), the SRS/Review machinery must guarantee MULTIPLE spaced encounters per item — single exposures will not stick.

**🟢 strong** — Explicit instruction that channels how learners PROCESS input — VanPatten's Processing Instruction / structured input — matches or outperforms output-based instruction on COMPREHENSION/interpretation measures. CAVEAT added: the Shintani, Li & Ellis (2013) meta-analysis (30 published studies, 35 projects, Language Learning 63:296-329) shows comprehension-based instruction wins on RECEPTIVE/interpretation tests, while production-based instruction equals or beats it on PRODUCTION tests — i.e. you largely train the modality you test. This is a skill-specificity result, not a blanket superiority of structured input.
- *Effect:* CBI favored on interpretation tests; PBI favored on production tests (modality-matched effects, Shintani, Li & Ellis 2013). The originally-quoted 'Qb=48.42' should be confirmed against full text before publication.
- *Sources:* Shintani, Li & Ellis 2013 meta-analysis (academia.edu/2911665); researchgate 236032380; VanPatten input processing
- *→ Loop:* Add a structured-input variant of `input` (target form is the only cue to meaning). Prefer structured `input` when Progress targets a grammatical form for RECEPTIVE knowledge; but because of skill-specificity, if the goal is PRODUCTION of that form, the arbiter must still route to `elicit`/`drill` — structured input alone will under-deliver on production.

**🟡 moderate** — Extensive reading (large volumes of comprehensible text) produces L2 gains, but the effect is SMALL-TO-MEDIUM and inflated by selection bias. Nakanishi (2015) found d=0.46 group contrasts / d=0.71 pre-post (34 studies, 43 ES, N=3,942). CRITICAL CAVEAT added by adversarial review: a propensity-score reanalysis (Hamada 2020, Frontiers in Psychology) found the reading-comprehension effect dropped from d=0.55 to d=0.37 once selection bias / non-random assignment was controlled. The literature is also geographically biased (mostly Asian EFL, English target), and Nakanishi herself flagged poor primary-study design (few RCTs, missing control groups). So input is a real learning mechanism, but the headline effect is modest and partly an artifact of weak designs and long, confounded treatments — NOT 'medium-to-large'.
- *Effect:* d=0.46 group / d=0.71 pre-post (Nakanishi 2015); but bias-corrected d~0.37 (Hamada 2020 propensity-score). Downgraded from the researcher's 'strong'.
- *Sources:* Nakanishi 2015, TESOL Quarterly 49(1):6-37 (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/tesq.157); ERIC EJ1052150; Hamada 2020, Frontiers in Psychology 11:617 / PMC7188915 (propensity-score reanalysis, d 0.55->0.37); link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-025-10068-6
- *→ Loop:* Keep `input` as a first-class, frequently-fired move and keep a minimum-input-proportion invariant — the mechanism is real. BUT do not over-budget turns to input on the assumption of a large effect: the bias-corrected effect is modest (~d=0.37) and dosage-dependent over long horizons. Pair the input invariant with comprehension-check telemetry so the harness measures whether input is actually landing, rather than assuming volume = gains.

**🟡 moderate** — The ~95-98% lexical coverage threshold (Nation's ~1-in-50 reading / 1-in-100 listening unknown-word ratio) is an evidence-informed, MEASURABLE operationalization of 'comprehensible-but-challenging' that can replace vague i+1. CAVEAT added: coverage thresholds predict COMPREHENSION, not acquisition rate, and the specific 95% vs 98% figures are population-average heuristics with substantial individual variance (background knowledge, cognates, working memory shift them). Treat the band as a tunable default, not a hard scientific constant.
- *Effect:* n/a (Nation coverage thresholds; ~95-98% coverage for adequate comprehension is well-replicated, but these are comprehension thresholds, not learning-gain thresholds).
- *Sources:* Nation 2001/2006 lexical coverage; Hu & Nation 2000 (95% vs 98% coverage and comprehension); scholarspace.manoa.hawaii.edu RFL
- *→ Loop:* Implement Q5 as a concrete invariant on `input`: target unknown-token density within the coverage band given the learner model's known-word set (pedagogy-in-code per Q4). But make the band a tunable parameter the harness can widen/narrow based on the learner's measured comprehension-check success, since the optimal density for a given learner is empirical, not fixed.

**🟡 moderate** — Textual/input enhancement (bolding, flooding) reliably boosts NOTICING but has only small effects on ACQUISITION and can slightly impair meaning comprehension — a form/meaning tradeoff. CONFIRMED: Lee & Huang (2008, 16 studies) found grammar-learning d=0.22 and comprehension d=-0.26. A newer meta-analysis reports g~0.54 between-subjects but is heterogeneous; the durable, replicated signal is 'small and mixed'.
- *Effect:* Lee & Huang 2008: grammar d=0.22, comprehension d=-0.26 (confirmed). Newer de Gruyter 2025 g=0.54, heterogeneous — treat as small-to-medium and unstable.
- *Sources:* Lee & Huang 2008, SSLA 30:307-331; degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/iral-2025-0118
- *→ Loop:* Treat enhancement as a low-cost augmentation of `input`, not a primary mechanism. Invariant: never let enhancement override comprehensibility — if highlighting a form would degrade meaning-getting, prefer `recast`/`explain`. Do not weight enhancement like drilling.

**🟠 contested** — Lichtman & VanPatten (2021) 'Was Krashen right?' rehabilitate the SPIRIT of Krashen (communicatively-embedded comprehensible input is central; implicit acquisition differs from explicit learning; development is partly ordered/impervious to instruction) but NOT i+1 or the affective filter as literal constructs. This is a position/review paper with multiple published rebuttals — genuinely contested, not consensus.
- *Effect:* n/a (position paper; commentaries by Loewen, Wulff, Morgan-Short, Gregg dispute several claims).
- *Sources:* Lichtman & VanPatten 2021, Foreign Language Annals 54(2):283-305 (doi 10.1111/flan.12552); Loewen 2021 (doi 10.1111/flan.12550); Wulff 2021 (doi 10.1111/flan.12555)
- *→ Loop:* Supports a Q3 design where conversation/meaning-focused input/elicit carries real teaching load, not decoration. But because 'development is ordered/impervious to instruction' is contested, do NOT hard-code a fixed grammar sequence as inviolable; let Review/Probe respond to the learner's emergent order via the learner model.

**🟠 contested** — Krashen's strong position ('acquisition cannot come from learning; output is unnecessary') is not supported; output, interaction, and corrective feedback contribute. CAVEAT: the headline d=1.09 (Mackey & Goo 2007) is the DELAYED-posttest interaction effect with input+feedback+output BUNDLED (immediate d=0.71) — it cannot isolate output's independent contribution and likely overstates it; it is upper-bound evidence that input+interaction beats input-only, not a clean output effect. Also note Mackey & Goo report small study counts per moderator and heterogeneity.
- *Effect:* Interaction d=0.71 immediate / d=1.09 delayed, BUNDLED (Mackey & Goo 2007); production-based instruction equals CBI on production tests (Shintani, Li & Ellis 2013). Researcher's '1.09' clarified as the delayed, bundled figure.
- *Sources:* Mackey & Goo 2007 interaction meta-analysis; Shintani, Li & Ellis 2013; Loewen 2021 commentary
- *→ Loop:* For Q3, use the drive-arbiter spine (not pure SRS, not pure conversation): Progress/Engage push input+communication; Repair/Review/Consolidate ensure output, feedback, and spaced retrieval get turns. Invariant against the input-only failure mode: if N consecutive turns have been input/chat with no elicited production, Consolidate or Repair must be allowed to win.

**🔴 refuted** — i+1 is UNFALSIFIABLE as a precise construct: there is no independent measure of 'i', so 'i+1' cannot be operationalized or tested. The general principle (slightly-beyond-current-level input) is sound; the specific +1 metric is dogma. Adversarial review CONFIRMS this — Gregg (1984) and McLaughlin (1987) show 'i', 'understanding', and 'noticing a gap' are not operationalized, and Krashen offers no mechanism for converting comprehended input into competence.
- *Effect:* n/a (logical/measurement critique). Rating upheld: refers to the LITERAL +1 metric, not the weaker challenging-but-comprehensible principle.
- *Sources:* Gregg 1984 'Krashen's Monitor and Occam's Razor'; McLaughlin 1987; jehd.thebrpi.org Vol4No4 critical review of the Input Hypothesis
- *→ Loop:* For Q5, do NOT encode a literal 'i+1' target. Calibrate by OBSERVABLE proxies: % unknown tokens per text and comprehension-check success. Invariant: keep `input` within a measured comprehensibility band and adjust on actual comprehension signals, not an unmeasurable 'level+1'.

**🔴 refuted** — The Affective Filter Hypothesis is unfalsifiable folk wisdom. Anxiety/motivation matter for engagement, but a 'filter that blocks acquired input' has no measure and no mechanism; it should not drive the control loop. Adversarial review CONFIRMS: Gregg (1984) notes no evidence for the filter's existence and no mechanism; it also fails to explain why no such filter blocks L1 acquisition in children.
- *Effect:* n/a (construct unmeasurable). Note: broader affect–achievement and anxiety–performance correlations ARE real but modest and are NOT the 'filter'; do not conflate.
- *Sources:* Gregg 1984; McLaughlin 1987; academypublication.com tpls vol09 critical evaluation of the Monitor Model
- *→ Loop:* Keep the Engage drive for retention/participation (observable), but never justify a pedagogical decision via an 'affective filter'. Do NOT add an invariant like 'lower difficulty when learner seems anxious to unblock acquisition' — there is no evidence input is blocked. Engage optimizes session continuation, not a hypothetical filter.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- i+1 as a precise, codeable difficulty target ('teach at level + 1'). — There is no independent measure of 'i' (Gregg 1984; McLaughlin 1987). The construct is unfalsifiable and cannot be operationalized. Folk wisdom widely treated as a precise dial; it is not. Replace with measurable proxies (unknown-token density, comprehension-check success).
- The Affective Filter — a mental filter that BLOCKS comprehensible input from becoming acquisition when anxiety is high. — No measure, no mechanism, no evidence the filter exists; it also fails to explain unfiltered L1 acquisition in children (Gregg 1984). Affect/anxiety correlate modestly with performance, but that is not 'a filter that blocks acquisition'. Must not justify any control-loop invariant.
- 'Comprehensible input alone is sufficient; output is unnecessary' (Krashen's strong claim). — Contradicted by interaction/feedback meta-analytic evidence (Mackey & Goo 2007) and by production-test results where output-based instruction matches comprehension-based (Shintani, Li & Ellis 2013). Input is necessary but not sufficient.
- Extensive reading delivers large L2 gains, so volume of input is the main lever. — The effect is small-to-medium and shrinks to ~d=0.37 once selection bias is controlled (Hamada 2020). Treating raw input volume as the dominant mechanism overstates a modest, design-inflated effect.
- Input enhancement (bolding/flooding) is a strong teaching technique for grammar. — Boosts noticing but yields only d=0.22 on grammar acquisition and can hurt comprehension (d=-0.26) (Lee & Huang 2008). It is a small, mixed-effect augmentation, not a primary mechanism.

## Output / pushed output & speaking practice
**🟢 strong** — Output (production-based, PBI) and input (comprehension-based, CBI) BOTH produce large measured gains and are roughly skill-matched: CBI is superior for RECEPTIVE knowledge (especially immediate posttest), while PBI equals CBI for PRODUCTIVE knowledge; the receptive advantage of input shrinks at delay. CAVEAT (added): the very large effects in this literature are inflated by design — most primary studies use explicit, narrow, often immediately-posttested grammar targets in lab/short-treatment conditions, and many report within-group pre/post gains. The Shintani (2015) figures cited as d=2.60 (receptive) / d=2.03 (productive) could NOT be verified against the primary tables (Oxford paywall; numbers are from secondary summaries) and are implausibly large for acquisition rather than explicit-knowledge measurement — treat them as unverified and do not anchor any design parameter on them. The robust, verifiable result is directional (CBI>PBI receptive immediate; PBI=CBI productive; gap narrows at delay), not the specific magnitudes.
- *Effect:* Shintani, Li & Ellis (2013), ~35 study reports/30 studies: both CBI and PBI large effects on receptive AND productive measures; CBI>PBI receptive (immediate), diminishing at delay; PBI=CBI productive — DIRECTION verified via abstract/RG summary. Shintani (2015) PI meta (42 experiments/33 studies): PI>PB receptive, PB=PI productive — direction verified; the specific d=2.60/2.03 NOT verified and likely reflect lab/explicit-knowledge design inflation.
- *Sources:* Shintani, Li & Ellis (2013) Language Learning 63:296-329; Shintani (2015) Applied Linguistics 36(3):306, processing-instruction meta-analysis
- *→ Loop:* Reject any architecture treating input as the sole acquisition engine and output as mere 'practice/reveal.' Make Progress eligible to advance an item on EITHER receptive OR productive evidence, tracked as SEPARATE mastery states. Invariant: an item's productive-mastery flag can ONLY be set by successful elicit/chat production, never by input/comprehension probes. But do NOT import the inflated lab effect sizes as expected gains or calibration constants — the magnitudes do not transfer to a continuous tutoring loop.

**🟢 strong** — Explicit corrective feedback during/after output is effective and need NOT be immediate-only; recasts (non-flagging models) work but are weaker and easily missed for some forms, while more explicit feedback/prompts are stronger for accuracy. Recast-ONLY is an under-powered policy for many target forms. VERIFIED via two meta-analyses. ADDED caveat: Li (2010) found lab > classroom and SHORTER treatments > longer — i.e. the d~0.61 is partly a short-term, often explicit-knowledge, lab effect; durable classroom gains are smaller. Explicit feedback's advantage is largest immediately and on explicit/metalinguistic measures; implicit/recast is more DURABLE at delay. So 'explicit is better' is time- and measure-dependent.
- *Effect:* Li (2010): overall CF d~0.61 (RE d~0.64), 33 studies; explicit short-term advantage (explicit d~0.81 vs recast d~0.70) but implicit/recast more durable at delay; lab>classroom, shorter>longer treatments (verified). Lyster & Saito (2010), 15 classroom studies/827 learners: CF durable; prompts>recasts for accuracy; effects clearest on free-constructed-response measures (verified).
- *Sources:* Li (2010) Language Learning 60:309-365; Lyster & Saito (2010) SSLA 32:265-302
- *→ Loop:* Resolve Q2 AGAINST recast-only. Arbiter selects by form type + learner state: recast when confidence is low or error is developmental/non-target; prompt/correct (explicit) when the item is a current target AND confidence is adequate. Invariant: a correct move requires the item already introduced and confidence above a floor; else downgrade to recast. Feed every corrected/recast error into Repair for that item. Because explicit gains are partly short-term/explicit-knowledge, weight DELAYED-posttest-style spaced re-checks (review) to confirm the correction actually stuck, rather than trusting immediate uptake.

**🟡 moderate** — Skills are LARGELY SPECIFIC: comprehension practice transfers poorly to production and vice versa; receptively-trained learners are slower/less accurate when tested productively. This is the strongest mechanistic case that output practice is non-optional if speaking is a goal. CAVEAT (added): the core direct evidence is a single Chinese-L2 lab experiment (Li & DeKeyser 2017) plus the broader DeKeyser skill-acquisition program; there is no meta-analysis of cross-skill transfer, and skill-specificity is strongest for the AUTOMATIZED/SPEED component, not for underlying declarative knowledge. So the claim supports 'output needed for fluent production,' not 'input gives nothing usable for production.'
- *Effect:* Li & DeKeyser (2017), Chinese L2 lab experiment: significant skill-specificity; reverse-skill testing markedly worse accuracy/RTs (asymmetric, near-zero cross-skill transfer of automatized speed). Single experiment + theory, not pooled — downgraded from 'strong' to 'moderate' on that basis.
- *Sources:* Li & DeKeyser (2017), skill specificity in Chinese L2; DeKeyser & Suzuki (2025), 'Skill acquisition theory,' in Theories in SLA, 4th ed.
- *→ Loop:* If the goal includes speaking, elicit and chat are MANDATORY, not optional. Invariant: every item targeted for productive mastery must accumulate a minimum count of production attempts (elicit/chat) before Progress retires it. Weight Engage/chat up specifically for automatization (speed), distinct from the noticing function of single elicits. Frame this as automatization of the production channel, NOT as 'input is useless for production.'

**🟡 moderate** — PUSHED output (production straining the learner just beyond current ability) induces NOTICING of form gaps and improved subsequent learning — the empirically supported part of Swain's hypothesis. CAVEAT (added): the evidence base is thinner than 'moderate' might imply. The flagship Izumi (2002) result used a HARD, LOW-SALIENCE form (English relativization); the main conceptual replication (Toth/'Spanish future tense', 2014) used a MORE SALIENT form and is a different design. There is no meta-analysis isolating pushed output. Effects may be form-salience-dependent: pushing on low-salience forms may notice-but-not-learn. Keep as moderate, but treat the i+1 'push' as a hypothesis to A/B test in the harness, not a settled mechanism.
- *Effect:* Izumi (2002, SSLA): output (reconstruction) group out-noticed and out-learned input-enhancement group on English relative clauses — verified single experiment. Partial/conceptual replications on counterfactual conditionals and Spanish future tense (different salience); NO pooled d isolating 'pushed output.'
- *Sources:* Izumi (2002) SSLA 24:541-577; Toth (2014) Foreign Language Annals, pushed output / Spanish future tense; Swain (1985, 1995) Output Hypothesis
- *→ Loop:* Allow elicit to sometimes target a form slightly ABOVE stable mastery to trigger noticing, then immediately follow with input/recast (gap-then-fill). Add a 'pushed-elicit' sub-mode. Invariant: a pushed-elicit that surfaces an error must be followed within N turns by a recast or input exposure of the same form. Make pushed-elicit frequency a tunable/loggable parameter so the noticing benefit can be empirically checked rather than assumed, especially for low-salience target forms.

**🟡 moderate** — TASK REPETITION reliably improves L2 production, but the magnitude and which dimension benefits most are NOT settled. CORRECTED: the two 2025 written meta-analyses CONFLICT. One (31 studies/65 samples) reports accuracy d=1.19, lexical d=0.75, fluency d=0.50, syntactic d=0.37. The other (Liu & Tang 2025; 17 studies/442 learners) reports a SMALL overall g=0.25, with fluency the LARGEST dimension (g=0.51), accuracy and lexical SMALL (g=0.24 each), and syntactic complexity NON-SIGNIFICANT (g=0.06, p=.57) — directly contradicting the d=1.19 accuracy / d=0.37 syntactic figures. The honest read: task repetition's best-supported, replicated benefit is FLUENCY (~g=0.50); accuracy and complexity gains are unstable across meta-analyses and may reflect heterogeneity, exact-vs-procedural repetition, and FL-vs-L2 context. Downgrade the 'largest gains in accuracy and complexity' framing — that is one meta-analysis, contradicted by the other.
- *Effect:* Liu & Tang (2025, Language Teaching Research): overall g=0.25; fluency g=0.51 (sig), accuracy g=0.24 (sig, SMALL), lexical g=0.24, syntactic complexity g=0.06 (NS). Competing 2025 written meta (31/65): accuracy d=1.19, lexical d=0.75, fluency d=0.50, syntactic d=0.37 — conflicts on accuracy/syntactic. 2025 oral meta: direction same, fluency robust, exact CALF d's not all recoverable. Strength downgraded from 'strong' to 'moderate' due to cross-meta conflict.
- *Sources:* Liu & Tang (2025) 'Task repetition on L2 writing performance: a meta-analysis,' Language Teaching Research, sagepub 13621688251381268; Task repetition and L2 written performance: A meta-analysis (2025), JSLW S1060374325000803; Task repetition and L2 oral performance: A meta-analysis (2025), System S0346251X25002787
- *→ Loop:* Add a 'task-repeat' move (re-run a recent elicit/chat prompt after a short spacing gap). Target it primarily at FLUENCY bottlenecks (latency/disfluency on an already-produced structure) — that is the replicated benefit. Do NOT assume task-repeat will fix accuracy or complexity; route accuracy errors to recast/correct instead. Prefer exact repetition for form stabilization, light variation for generalization; cap spacing short for fluency goals. Keep the accuracy/complexity benefit as an unproven hypothesis to verify in-loop.

**🟡 moderate** — Willingness to Communicate (WTC) is the proximal gate on whether a learner actually produces output, driven most by PERCEIVED communicative competence, then negatively by anxiety, then by motivation; WTC predicts actual speaking behavior. CAVEAT (added): these are CORRELATIONAL meta-analytic associations, not causal — high WTC may co-cause and be co-caused by successful output, so 'raising WTC raises output' is an inference. Only the anxiety correlate (r=-.29) was independently verified here; the perceived-competence-largest and motivation r=.37 values come from secondary summaries (Springer paywall) and should be treated as approximate.
- *Effect:* Shirvan, Khajavy, MacIntyre & Taherian (2019) J Psycholinguistic Research 48:1241-1267: all three correlates moderate; perceived communicative competence LARGEST positive (abstract-verified as largest; exact r not recovered); anxiety r=-.29 (verified); motivation ~r=.37 (NOT independently verified, secondary summary). Leeming (2024): WTC/self-efficacy/perceived competence predict spoken task production. Correlational; causal direction unestablished.
- *Sources:* Shirvan, Khajavy, MacIntyre & Taherian (2019), PubMed 31342240; Leeming (2024) Language Learning, WTC/self-efficacy predicting spoken task production
- *→ Loop:* Add an estimated WTC/confidence state to the learner model (inferred from latency, length, hedging, abandonment). Make Engage and an elicit guardrail WTC-aware: when confidence is low, DOWN-weight pushed-elicit and explicit correct (anxiety-raising), UP-weight recast and low-stakes at-or-below-level chat to rebuild perceived competence. Invariant: never chain multiple explicit corrections when confidence is low. Treat the WTC->output link as a design heuristic to monitor (does down-weighting correction actually increase this learner's output?), since the evidence is correlational.

**🟠 contested** — Whether output CAUSES new acquisition vs. merely REVEALS/AUTOMATIZES existing knowledge is genuinely contested. The strong causal claim (free output builds new interlanguage) is weakly supported; the well-supported roles are (a) noticing-the-gap, (b) hypothesis-testing, (c) automatization/fluency of already-acquired forms, and (d) productive-skill specificity. 'Speaking causes acquisition' is folk overreach. VERIFIED: no meta-analysis isolates a causal acquisition effect of free output independent of the input/feedback it elicits.
- *Effect:* No meta-analysis isolates a causal acquisition effect of free output. Interaction-plus-output studies show output adds PRODUCTIVE retention but does not beat input-only for RECEPTIVE acquisition — consistent with automatize/reveal, not build. This adversarial review found no counter-evidence overturning the 'contested' label.
- *Sources:* Shintani, Li & Ellis (2013) (productive vs receptive split); Swain & Lapkin languaging line; languaging scoping review PMC10205504 (2023)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT let Progress credit raw output volume as acquisition. Model chat/elicit value as (1) a noticing trigger feeding Repair/Review, and (2) an automatization counter for already-introduced items — NEVER as introduce-equivalent. Keep introduce input-led. This keeps pedagogy-in-code (Q4) honest about what output does.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- 'Speaking causes acquisition' / output builds new interlanguage knowledge (strong Output Hypothesis). — No meta-analysis isolates a causal acquisition effect of free/communicative output independent of the input and feedback it generates. Supported roles are noticing-the-gap, hypothesis-testing, and automatization/fluency of already-acquired forms — i.e. output reveals/automatizes more than it builds. The researcher already marked this contested; adversarial review confirms it.
- 'Task repetition produces its largest gains in accuracy and structural complexity.' — This rests on one written meta-analysis (accuracy d=1.19, syntactic d=0.37) that is directly contradicted by Liu & Tang (2025), which found accuracy only g=0.24 and syntactic complexity NON-significant (g=0.06, p=.57). The only dimension robust across meta-analyses is FLUENCY (~g=0.50). The 'largest gains in accuracy/complexity' framing is an artifact of selecting the more favorable meta-analysis.
- Effect sizes of d>2 (e.g., processing-instruction d=2.60/2.03) represent the real-world power of comprehension/output instruction. — Could not be verified against primary tables; effects this large in instructed SLA almost always reflect within-group pre/post designs, immediate posttests, narrow explicit grammar targets, and lab conditions — measures of explicit knowledge, not durable acquisition. Importing such magnitudes into a tutoring control loop would massively overstate expected gains.
- Recasts (implicit, non-flagging models) are a sufficient stand-alone correction policy. — Lyster & Saito (2010) found prompts > recasts for accuracy, and Li (2010) found recasts weaker short-term and easily missed for many forms. Recast-only is a folk-comfortable (low-anxiety) but under-powered policy; it should be state- and form-conditioned, not the default. (The findings correctly reject recast-only; flagged here because recast-only is a common harness-design temptation.)
- Raising a learner's WTC/confidence will increase their output (and thus learning). — The WTC evidence is CORRELATIONAL meta-analytic association, not causal; WTC and successful output likely co-determine each other. Designing the arbiter as if boosting estimated confidence mechanically yields more (or better-learned) output treats a correlation as a causal lever — defensible as a monitored heuristic, not an established effect.

## Interaction hypothesis & negotiation of meaning
**🟢 strong** — Conversational interaction (with negotiation of meaning) reliably produces learning gains over non-interactive input. Mackey & Goo (2007) meta-analyzed 28 interaction studies and found large effects favoring interaction over control, with gains that GREW on delayed posttests (FL settings d≈0.88 > SL settings d≈0.64, no difference at delay). Keck et al. (2006) independently meta-analyzed task-based interaction studies and found large effects that PERSISTED or grew on delay (short-delay d≈1.12, long-delay d≈1.18). Two independent meta-analyses converging on large, durable effects is a strong warrant for treating conversation as an acquisition channel, not just practice. ADVERSARIAL CAVEATS (added): (a) These are HUMAN-interlocutor lab/classroom studies; none used an AI/LLM interlocutor, so the d≈0.9-1.2 figures do NOT transfer directly to this harness — they bound what human negotiation can do, not what an LLM chat partner will deliver. (b) Many primary studies use short treatments and selected-response/discrete-point posttests that lean toward explicit/measurable knowledge rather than spontaneous free-production acquisition; effect sizes are best read as CEILINGS. (c) Older interaction meta-analyses do not fully correct for publication bias. The DIRECTION (interaction > control, durable, FL≥SL) is robust and replicated; the exact decimals should be treated as optimistic.
- *Effect:* interaction vs control: FL d≈0.88 / SL d≈0.64 (Mackey & Goo 2007, k=28); task-based delayed d≈1.12-1.18 (Keck et al. 2006). Treat as ceilings; human-interlocutor, partly discrete-point measures
- *Sources:* Mackey & Goo 2007, Interaction research in SLA: A meta-analysis and research synthesis (in Mackey ed., Conversational Interaction in SLA); Keck, Iberri-Shea, Tracy-Ventura & Wa-Mbaleka 2006, in Norris & Ortega (eds.) Synthesizing Research on Language Learning and Teaching, pp.91-131; https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-teaching/article/interaction-and-instructed-second-language-acquisition/78A156EE200F744F5978F99BFB073DBE; https://eprints.lancs.ac.uk/id/eprint/59863/
- *→ Loop:* Q3: do NOT make curriculum the sole driver. The `chat` move should be a real acquisition channel, not a reward/cool-down. Reweight Engage so chat fires regularly (not only at lesson edges); Progress should be satisfiable THROUGH conversation, not only via introduce/drill. Add invariant: an item the learner is shaky on is eligible to be resurfaced INSIDE chat (interaction-embedded review), not only via the isolated `review`/`drill` move. BUT: because the warrant is human-interlocutor evidence, instrument the harness to actually measure learning gains from chat (probe before/after), rather than ASSUMING the d≈1.0 transfers to an LLM partner.

**🟢 strong** — Interactional corrective feedback embedded in meaningful exchange produces medium-large, durable learning. Li (2010), k=33, found overall CF d≈0.61 (fixed) / 0.64 (random), maintained over time; implicit feedback (recasts, clarification requests, elicitation) was better MAINTAINED on delayed tests, while explicit feedback (metalinguistic, explicit correction) was larger in the SHORT term. Lab > classroom, FL > SL, and SHORTER treatments > longer treatments in Li's data. ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT (added): the lab>classroom and selected-response-friendly measures mean these d's overstate durable free-production gains in a real long-running, classroom-like harness. Human-interlocutor evidence again.
- *Effect:* d≈0.61-0.64 overall (Li 2010, k=33); implicit more durable on delay, explicit larger short-term; lab>classroom, FL>SL, shorter>longer treatments
- *Sources:* Li, S. 2010, The effectiveness of corrective feedback in SLA: A meta-analysis, Language Learning 60:309-365; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9922.2010.00561.x
- *→ Loop:* Q2: Repair drive and `correct`/`recast` moves are evidence-backed — keep them. Weight toward IMPLICIT feedback (recast/prompt) for durability, and bias toward SHORT focused episodes (Li: shorter treatments outperformed longer). Invariant: cap consecutive explicit-correction turns; after correcting, return to meaning. `explain` (long metalinguistic) is rationed by the arbiter, not used as default repair. NOTE the genuine tension with Lyster & Saito on treatment length (see next finding) — do not treat 'short is always better' as settled.

**🟢 strong** — FOLK WISDOM FLAG — 'always correct errors explicitly and immediately with the rule.' The evidence does NOT support explicit immediate correction as the default. Explicit CF is less DURABLE than implicit (Li 2010, though explicit is larger in the short term), and prompts (which withhold the answer) beat recasts on free production (Lyster & Saito 2010). Heavy metalinguistic explanation is not the high-leverage move it is traditionally assumed to be. CORRECTION (adversarial): the original finding's supporting bullet 'long explicit treatment < short' overstates the case — only Li 2010 found shorter>longer; Lyster & Saito 2010 found long ≥ short-to-medium. Drop treatment length as a justification; the durability (implicit>explicit on delay) and prompts>recasts-on-free-production points are the sound ones.
- *Effect:* implicit > explicit on DURABILITY (Li 2010); prompts > recasts on free production (Lyster & Saito 2010). Treatment-length claim removed as a warrant (Li and L&S conflict)
- *Sources:* Li 2010 (Language Learning 60:309-365); Lyster & Saito 2010 (SSLA 32:265-302); https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ892626
- *→ Loop:* Q2/Q4: do NOT default to `correct`+`explain`. Arbiter priority for Repair: prompt (push self-repair) > salient recast > brief explicit correction > long `explain` (last resort, rationed). Invariant: `explain` (long metalinguistic) is capped per session and gated on a learner request or repeated failure of lighter feedback. Treat 'explain the grammar rule' as a low-frequency move, not the backbone. (Note: explicit CF IS larger in the short term per Li, so for a learner who needs an immediate fix on a discrete point, a brief explicit correction is legitimate — the myth is making it the DEFAULT, not banning it.)

**🟡 moderate** — The active ingredient is NEGOTIATION OF MEANING: when comprehension breaks down, interactional modifications (clarification requests, confirmation/comprehension checks) make input comprehensible AND push the learner to selectively attend to form; clarification requests in particular are more likely than confirmation checks to force restructuring. Negotiation links input, selective attention, and output in one episode (Pica; Doughty & Pica 1986; Keck et al. 2006 found jigsaw/required-information-exchange tasks produce the largest interaction effects). ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT (added): this is a MECHANISM claim supported by correlational/process evidence plus the meta-analytic gains; it is plausible but the specific causal chain (negotiation -> selective attention -> acquisition) is not isolated by a controlled meta-analysis. Treat as a well-motivated design heuristic, not a proven causal lever.
- *Effect:* n/a (mechanism). Indirect support: negotiation-rich jigsaw/required-information-exchange tasks show the largest interaction effects (Keck et al. 2006). Downgraded from strong to moderate: mechanism not causally isolated
- *Sources:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interaction_hypothesis; Pica (negotiation of meaning); Doughty & Pica 1986; Keck et al. 2006 (jigsaw/required-information-exchange largest)
- *→ Loop:* Add a conversational-repair sub-move usable inside `chat` and `elicit`: when the learner's utterance signals a comprehension gap, issue a clarification request rather than silently moving on. Invariant: on detected learner non-comprehension of input, the harness MUST negotiate (clarification/check) before proceeding. Prefer information-gap (jigsaw-like) task structures when realizing `elicit`/`chat`. The Repair drive fires on COMPREHENSION breakdowns, not only production errors. (Mechanism is moderate, so make this a default-on behavior that is cheap to run, not a heavy invariant the whole pedagogy leans on.)

**🟡 moderate** — PROMPTS (output-pushing feedback: clarification requests, elicitations, metalinguistic clues, repetition-of-error) tend to outperform RECASTS, especially on free-production measures. Lyster & Saito (2010), k=15, N=827: CF had significant durable effects, larger for prompts than recasts, most apparent on free constructed-response measures; younger learners benefited more. Prompts force MODIFIED/PUSHED OUTPUT, which recasts do not. CORRECTION (adversarial): the original finding's claim that 'long explicit treatments underperform short ones' is NOT supported by Lyster & Saito and is partly contradicted by it — L&S found effects of LONG treatments were LARGER than short-to-medium (though indistinguishable from brief). Only Li (2010) found shorter>longer. The two meta-analyses DISAGREE on treatment length; this should not be stated as a settled fact. Also: the prompts>recasts gap is real but moderator-laden and the k is small (15 studies), so 'prompts > recasts' is a directional tendency, not a large robust margin.
- *Effect:* prompts > recasts on free production (Lyster & Saito 2010, k=15, N=827); younger > older; treatment-length finding CONTESTED (L&S: long ≥ short-to-medium; Li 2010: short > long)
- *Sources:* Lyster & Saito 2010, Oral feedback in classroom SLA: A meta-analysis, Studies in SLA 32:265-302; https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ892626; https://escholarship.mcgill.ca/concern/articles/6682x769r
- *→ Loop:* Q2: do NOT make `recast` the only no-flag feedback move. Add a PROMPT move (elicit-the-correction / clarification-request / metalinguistic-hint) that withholds the target form and pushes self-repair. Arbiter rule: when the learner is judged developmentally ready for the item, prefer prompt over recast to force modified output; when the item is brand-new/shaky, prefer recast (lower processing load). Invariant: after a prompt, give the learner a turn to self-correct before supplying the form. Do NOT encode 'short feedback always beats long' as an invariant — the meta-analyses conflict on treatment length; make episode length a tunable, not a hard rule.

**🟡 moderate** — Conversational/dialogue AI agents DO produce measurable L2 gains, validating an LLM-driven chat-as-acquisition design — but effects are MODERATE, not large, and design-dependent (meaning constraint, modality, GenAI vs rule-based). Meta-analyses: chatbot-assisted learning g≈0.48 (Wang, Cheung, Neitzel & Chai 2025, 28 studies, 70 ES, robust variance estimation); Lyu 2025 and others land in a similar small-to-moderate band. GenAI chatbots > rule-based chatbots. ADVERSARIAL CAVEATS (added/strengthened): (a) A 2025 education-wide chatbot meta-analysis found that AFTER correcting for publication bias the large raw effect dropped to small-to-moderate — overestimation due to publication bias is documented in this literature, so g≈0.48 may itself be optimistic. (b) Outcome measures are heterogeneous and many are short-duration; NOVELTY/ENGAGEMENT effects are a live confound (learners try harder with a new chatbot) and are easily mistaken for durable learning. (c) The biggest single threat to applying the human-interaction meta-analyses (findings 1-6) to this harness is exactly the human-vs-AI interlocutor gap — the chatbot MAs are the relevant evidence and they are only moderate. Treat g≈0.48 as the realistic expectation for this harness, NOT the d≈1.0 from human-interaction studies.
- *Effect:* chatbot-assisted learning g≈0.48 (Wang et al. 2025, k=28, 70 ES); small-to-moderate after publication-bias correction in education-wide MAs; GenAI > rule-based; novelty/engagement confound plausible
- *Sources:* Wang, Cheung, Neitzel & Chai 2025, Does Chatting with Chatbots Improve Language Learning?, Review of Educational Research; https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/00346543241255621; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/ijal.12668; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1041608025000226; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10639-023-11805-6
- *→ Loop:* Q3/Q4/Q6: `chat` is empirically justified as a driver but design matters more than its existence. Keep pedagogy partly IN CODE (Q4): the MAs show unstructured/rule-based systems underperform, so the negotiation/feedback/SRS invariants do real work the raw LLM won't reliably supply. Modality (Q6): consider spoken/multimodal where feasible. CRITICAL: calibrate expectations to g≈0.48 (bias-corrected lower), not d≈1.0; and distinguish engagement metrics from learning — gate Progress/Consolidate on actual probe-measured gains, not on time-on-task or chat volume.

**🟠 contested** — Recasts work but are CONTESTED in magnitude and reliability: their corrective intent is ambiguous, learners frequently fail to distinguish a recast from a non-corrective repetition (especially at lower proficiency / before developmental readiness), and WM/processing limits blunt the comparison. Rassaei (2022, k=24) found overall g=0.70 (CI 0.48-0.93), equally effective for implicit and explicit knowledge, and STRONGER in FL than SL (weak effects in SL both short- and long-term). Efficacy is moderated by salience, proficiency, intonation, and readiness. Confirmed against source.
- *Effect:* recasts g=0.70 (CI 0.48-0.93) (Rassaei 2022, k=24); FL strong / SL weak; equally effective for implicit & explicit knowledge; moderated by salience/proficiency/readiness
- *Sources:* Rassaei 2022, The effects of recasts on L2 grammar: a meta-analysis, Language Learning Journal 52(1); https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09571736.2022.2097298
- *→ Loop:* Q2/Q5: make recasts SALIENT — invariant: a recast must isolate/stress the corrected form (partial recast on the error, not a full fluent reformulation that hides it), with light prosodic/typographic emphasis. Arbiter gate: do NOT recast a form the learner is not yet developmentally ready for; route to `input`/`introduce` to build readiness first. Invariant: don't expect uptake from a single recast on a shaky item; track noticing, and escalate to a prompt or brief explicit correction if no uptake. NOTE Rassaei's FL>SL split is FAVORABLE for a self-study (FL-like) harness, but is HUMAN-interlocutor evidence.

**⚪ weak** — MODIFIED/PUSHED OUTPUT is a plausible mechanism: being pushed (e.g. by a clarification request) to reprocess output yields noticing of gaps and accuracy gains (Nobuyoshi & Ellis 1993; Swain's output hypothesis). Caveat (already in finding, upheld): direct causal evidence that output ALONE drives acquisition is WEAK — there is no large meta-analysis isolating pushed output; support rests on small primary studies. Output's role is best supported as part of the interaction loop (noticing + hypothesis testing), not as a standalone driver.
- *Effect:* n/a — no meta-analysis isolating pushed output; evidence is small primary studies (Nobuyoshi & Ellis 1993). Downgraded moderate->weak: thin, non-meta-analytic, output-alone causation unestablished
- *Sources:* Swain 1985/1995 output hypothesis; Nobuyoshi & Ellis 1993; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comprehensible_output
- *→ Loop:* Q3/Q2: the `elicit` move can sometimes be PUSHED — set a slightly-above-current target and, if output is non-target/vague, issue ONE clarification request to force reformulation, then support. Learner model tracks whether modified output followed a push (noticing signal) and feeds Consolidate/Review. Because the evidence is weak, do NOT build a heavy invariant around pushed output: pair every push with available input/recast as a fallback so a stuck learner isn't left flailing, and keep push frequency low/tunable.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Always correct errors explicitly and immediately with the rule (explicit metalinguistic correction as the default repair). — Li (2010): implicit feedback is more DURABLE than explicit on delayed posttests (explicit only wins short-term). Lyster & Saito (2010): prompts that withhold the form beat recasts, and explicit correction is not the highest-leverage option on free production. Heavy `explain` is a last-resort, rationed move, not the backbone. (Nuance: explicit CF is legitimately larger in the SHORT term, so brief explicit fixes are fine — the myth is making them the default.)
- Shorter corrective-feedback treatments are always better than longer ones (used in the original findings to justify capping feedback length). — Only Li (2010) found shorter > longer. Lyster & Saito (2010) found the OPPOSITE: long treatments were LARGER than short-to-medium (and indistinguishable from brief). The two meta-analyses conflict, so 'short always wins' is not an empirically settled rule and should be a tunable parameter, not a hard invariant.
- Recasts are a reliable, strong corrective tool you can drop in and expect uptake from. — Recast efficacy is CONTESTED (Rassaei 2022 g=0.70 but heavily moderated; strong in FL, WEAK in SL). Corrective intent is ambiguous — learners often cannot distinguish a recast from a non-corrective repetition, especially at low proficiency / before developmental readiness. A single recast on a shaky item should not be expected to produce uptake without salience and readiness.
- Chat with a conversational AI delivers the large interaction-hypothesis gains (d≈1.0) seen in the SLA literature. — The d≈0.6-1.2 figures come from HUMAN-interlocutor lab/classroom studies. The actual AI-chatbot meta-analyses are only MODERATE (g≈0.48), drop to small-to-moderate after publication-bias correction, and are confounded by novelty/engagement effects. Conflating engagement/time-on-task with durable learning, and human-interaction effect sizes with AI-interaction effect sizes, is unsupported.
- Pushed/modified output is an independently established driver of acquisition. — There is no meta-analysis isolating pushed output; the warrant is a handful of small primary studies (Nobuyoshi & Ellis 1993) plus the theoretical output hypothesis. Output's evidential support is as part of the interaction loop (noticing/hypothesis-testing), not as a standalone causal driver.

## Explicit vs implicit instruction & focus-on-form
**🟢 strong** — Explicit instruction outperforms implicit instruction ON AVERAGE across L2 grammar outcomes, but the headline magnitudes are overstated and measure-dependent. The DIRECTION (explicit > implicit on aggregate, especially constrained/metalinguistic outcomes) is robustly replicated (Norris & Ortega 2000; Spada & Tomita 2010; Goo et al. 2015). HOWEVER: the specific decimals 'explicit g=1.29 vs implicit g=0.77' could NOT be located in any primary or secondary source during verification and appear to be a fabricated/garbled pairing — they do not match Goo et al.'s actually reported figures. Goo et al. 2015's explicit-vs-IMPLICIT contrast effects are MODEST: g≈0.45 on free production and g≈0.58 on constrained production (the g≈1.4 figure circulating for Goo is the explicit group's pre-post gain on free production, NOT the explicit-over-implicit difference — conflating these is the single biggest error in the original finding). Goo et al. also found implicit instruction's overall mean effect HIGHER than in Norris & Ortega, i.e. the gap is NARROWING in newer studies, and several recent studies find NO significant explicit advantage. Treat 'explicit somewhat > implicit on most measures' as the supportable claim; treat any large headline effect size as artifact.
- *Effect:* Direction strong; magnitude moderate-to-small for the explicit-vs-implicit CONTRAST. Goo et al. 2015 explicit>implicit: g≈0.45 free production, g≈0.58 constrained production (34 studies). Norris & Ortega 2000 focused-instruction pre-post d≈0.96 overall (NOT an explicit-vs-implicit contrast). The cited 'g=1.29 vs 0.77' is UNVERIFIED and likely spurious.
- *Sources:* Norris & Ortega 2000, Language Learning 50(3) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136; Goo, Granena, Yilmaz & Novella 2015, in Rebuschat (ed.), John Benjamins — https://benjamins.com/catalog/sibil.48.18goo; Goo et al. 2015 free/constrained g values via secondary summary — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/300473140_Implicit_and_explicit_instruction_in_L2_learning
- *→ Loop:* Keep explain and correct as real, default-available moves (direction holds). But do NOT over-weight them on the basis of a large effect size that does not exist for the explicit-vs-implicit contrast. Set explain/correct as MODERATE-prior moves at introduction/shaky stage, not dominant ones. The arbiter must not treat explicit moves as a reliably large win over implicit input/recast; the real-world contrast effect is small-to-medium and shrinking.

**🟢 strong** — The explicit advantage is substantially inflated by outcome-measure type — this is the MOST important and best-supported caveat, and it should be elevated from 'moderate' toward 'strong.' Effects are largest on metalinguistic/selected/constrained-response measures that favor explicit DECLARATIVE knowledge; the advantage shrinks markedly on free-production / spontaneous-use measures. Independent critiques (Taylor; 'Another Look at Norris and Ortega') argue the tests largely assessed EXPLICIT knowledge rather than the implicit knowledge responsible for communication, so the meta-analyses partly measure 'did the explicit group learn the explicit thing we taught and tested explicitly' — a near-tautology. Goo et al.'s own free-production explicit-vs-implicit g≈0.45 (vs constrained g≈0.58) confirms the shrinkage.
- *Effect:* Goo et al. 2015: explicit>implicit g≈0.45 free vs g≈0.58 constrained. Spada & Tomita 2010 free-production explicit d≈0.75 (still smaller than constrained gains). Direction of measure-type moderation consistent across syntheses.
- *Sources:* Norris & Ortega 2000 (authors' own caveat on constrained, accuracy-biased tests) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136; Another Look at Norris and Ortega 2000, SALT — https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/SALT/article/view/1434; Taylor, 'The Justification for Explicit Grammar Instruction is Overstated', MexTESOL — https://www.mextesol.net/journal/index.php?page=journal&id_article=46438; Ellis weak-interface discussion — https://sites.lsa.umich.edu/nickellis-new/wp-content/uploads/sites/1284/2021/07/Weak-Interface.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Strongest design lever in this dimension. Invariant: an item is promoted to 'known/consolidated' ONLY on free-production-like evidence (open-context elicit, chat), never from constrained drill or metalinguistic probe. Progress drive gates on production evidence. Explicit-rule/probe success advances ONLY a separate 'declarative known' flag that does not unlock Review/Consolidate completion. This is the one control-loop implication the verification strengthens rather than weakens.

**🟡 moderate** — Explicit instruction is more effective for BOTH simple and complex features in aggregate (Spada & Tomita 2010 found larger explicit-over-implicit effects for simple AND complex, including on free production of complex forms), so complexity does not cleanly flip the advantage to implicit. CAVEAT ADDED: Spada & Tomita's simple/complex split is operationalized by 'number of criteria to reach the correct form,' a coarse proxy that has been criticized; the complex-feature cells rest on fewer studies and wider confidence intervals; and the result does NOT mean complex rules are easy to STATE usefully — it means explicit treatment (which can include input flood, structured input, feedback, not only rule statements) helped. Do not read this as 'always verbalize complex rules.'
- *Effect:* Spada & Tomita 2010: explicit free-production d≈0.75 averaged across simple+complex; explicit>implicit for both feature types (41 studies). Downgraded from 'strong' to 'moderate' due to coarse complexity operationalization and thinner complex-feature cells.
- *Sources:* Spada & Tomita 2010, Language Learning 60(2) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9922.2010.00562.x
- *→ Loop:* Do not route complex items AWAY from explicit treatment on the folk assumption they are 'too hard to state.' But 'explicit treatment' ≠ 'long rule explanation': for complex items prefer structured input + input flood + targeted feedback over a dense explain dump. Per-item 'complexity' field should raise the prior on explicit TREATMENT generally (structured input/feedback), and only modestly on the verbal explain move; cap explain length for high-complexity items to avoid overloading declarative working memory.

**🟡 moderate** — Focus-on-form (attention to form within meaning) and focus-on-forms (decontextualized form study) produced equivalent and large effects in Norris & Ortega; neither is clearly superior in aggregate, and meaning+form beats meaning-alone. CAVEAT ADDED: the FonF/FonF(s) coding in Norris & Ortega has been directly criticized as unreliable (the same treatment can be coded either way; secondary recoding may have biased the equivalence conclusion), so 'equivalent' is a weak, contested equivalence rather than a demonstrated null. Ellis (2016) himself flags the construct as fuzzy. The defensible core is only the directional claim: pure incidental exposure with NO attention to form is the weakest condition; drawing attention to form within meaningful use is well supported.
- *Effect:* Norris & Ortega 2000: FonF vs FonF(s) 'equivalent and large' (but coding reliability contested); meaning+form > meaning-alone (direction consistent, magnitudes vary).
- *Sources:* Norris & Ortega 2000 — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136; Ellis 2016, Focus on form: A critical review, LTR — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362168816628627; Effectiveness of grammar instruction: analysis of a meta-analysis (coding critique) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/291175466_The_effectiveness_of_grammar_instruction_Analysis_of_a_meta-analysis
- *→ Loop:* Q3: hybrid is best supported, but rest it on the FIRM leg (attention-to-form > meaning-alone), not the SHAKY leg (FonF=FonF(s) equivalence). Invariant: chat (meaning-focused) should not run alone indefinitely; when a target item surfaces or a current-item error occurs, the arbiter may interrupt with recast or brief explain. Do not implement a pure-conversation (meaning-alone) spine nor a pure-drill (forms-alone) spine; interleave. This supports the drive-arbiter spine over rigid phase-machine or pure-SRS for Q1, but note the arbiter-vs-phase-machine choice is an ENGINEERING preference the meta-analyses do not directly adjudicate.

**🟡 moderate** — Explicit declarative knowledge does not directly become fluent spontaneous production; it must be proceduralized/automatized through extensive form-meaning practice (DeKeyser skill-acquisition theory; weak-interface position). This is a THEORETICAL model with supporting lab/longitudinal work, NOT a meta-analytic effect. CAVEAT ADDED: the strong/weak/no-interface debate is genuinely unresolved — the no-interface camp (Krashen, and implicit-learning researchers) denies declarative→implicit conversion altogether; adopting the weak-interface model is a defensible mainstream bet, not settled fact. The 'proceduralization complete within ~16 trials' figure is from a specific small DeKeyser (1997) lab study with an artificial/limited grammar and should NOT be treated as a tuning constant for a real-language harness.
- *Effect:* n/a (theoretical model; DeKeyser 1997 proceduralization within first ~16-item block is a single small lab result, not a generalizable parameter).
- *Sources:* DeKeyser & Suzuki 2025, Skill acquisition theory, in VanPatten et al. (eds.) — https://yuichisuzuki.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/PreprintDeKeyser-R.-M.-Suzuki-Y.-2025.-Skill-acquisition-theory.-In-...pdf; Suzuki & DeKeyser (in press) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/393630707
- *→ Loop:* Per-item move sequence (introduce/explain → form-MEANING drill → open elicit/chat) is sound as a DESIGN HYPOTHESIS but is theory-driven, not effect-size-backed; instrument it and let the learner model's production evidence override it. Invariant: explain fires ONCE near introduction, not repeatedly; drill must be form-meaning (varied, meaningful), not mechanical. Track per-item stage (declarative/procedural/automatized). Do NOT hardcode '16 trials' or any fixed proceduralization count — make it adaptive to per-item production accuracy.

**🟡 moderate** — Both recast (implicit CF) and explicit correction work; explicit/more-salient feedback tends to produce LARGER IMMEDIATE gains on most measures, but the advantage is smaller and less durable than the original finding implies, and the better-supported contrast is PROMPTS > RECASTS rather than 'explicit correction > recast.' CORRECTIONS: (1) Li (2010) immediate gap is modest — explicit d≈0.81 vs recast d≈0.70 — not a large separation. (2) Implicit/recast effects were BETTER MAINTAINED over time. (3) Lyster & Saito's durable advantage was for PROMPTS (output-pushing, e.g. elicitation/clarification) over recasts, which is a different mechanism than 'explicit rule-giving correction.' So 'favor explicitness' should be refined to 'favor output-pushing/prompting feedback and salience,' not necessarily heavy metalinguistic correction.
- *Effect:* Li 2010: explicit feedback d≈0.81 vs recast d≈0.70 immediate (overall CF d≈0.64); implicit better maintained at delay. Lyster & Saito: prompts > recasts, durable.
- *Sources:* Li 2010 meta-analysis — https://www.academia.edu/2911659/Li_S_2010_The_effectiveness_of_corrective_feedback_in_SLA_A_meta_analysis; Lyster & Saito (oral CF meta-analysis) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/234580927_Oral_Feedback_in_Classroom_SLA_A_Meta-Analysis; Goo et al. 2015 — https://benjamins.com/catalog/sibil.48.18goo
- *→ Loop:* Q2: reject pure recast-only, but ALSO reject 'maximize explicit metalinguistic correction.' Best-supported policy: prefer OUTPUT-PUSHING PROMPTS (an elicit/clarification move that makes the learner self-repair) over both bare recasts and heavy rule-corrections, especially for items the learner has declarative knowledge of. Add a 'prompt' variant to the move set (or treat elicit-for-repair as a CF mode). Use explicit correct when the error is a current target and the learner already knows the rule (a flaggable slip); use recast to preserve meaning-flow when Engage dominates. Invariant: do not explicitly correct an un-introduced item (recast/model only). Because explicit CF decays faster, schedule a production re-probe after explicit corrections rather than marking the item resolved.

**🟠 contested** — Durability of instructional effects is genuinely under-tested and decay should be the default assumption — BUT the original finding's framing is internally inconsistent with the literature it cites. Norris & Ortega actually concluded instructional effects were fairly DURABLE on average (maintained at the delays tested), even though all groups declined somewhat from immediate posttest. The more decision-relevant finding is the OPPOSITE asymmetry to what helps explicit moves: in corrective-feedback research (Li 2010), IMPLICIT/recast effects were BETTER MAINTAINED over time than explicit-feedback effects, even though explicit was larger immediately. So decay, where measured, tends to ERODE the explicit advantage specifically. Delayed posttests remain rare and short, so durability rests on a thin base for all conditions.
- *Effect:* n/a. Norris & Ortega: effects 'durable' on average with declines across conditions. Li 2010: explicit feedback larger immediately (d≈0.81 vs recast d≈0.70) but implicit effects better maintained at delay.
- *Sources:* Norris & Ortega 2000 (durability noted; short interventions/delays) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136; Li 2010, The effectiveness of corrective feedback in SLA: a meta-analysis, Language Learning 60 — https://www.academia.edu/2911659/Li_S_2010_The_effectiveness_of_corrective_feedback_in_SLA_A_meta_analysis; Grammar-instruction durability discussion — https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1086281.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Empirical backbone of the Review drive and spaced-resurfacing invariant survives. Keep 'must surface overdue reviews within N turns'; weight Review by time-since-last-success AND by whether prior success was on a production measure (production decay is the safer default). Because explicit gains decay FASTER than implicit ones, do not assume an item demonstrated via a metalinguistic/explain success is durably learned — re-probe it in production. Never permanently retire an item via Consolidate; keep SRS-style resurfacing. Supports SRS layered UNDER the arbiter (arbiter spine + SRS-informed Review weighting), not SRS as sole spine.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- 'Explicit instruction beats implicit by a large margin (e.g. g≈1.3 vs 0.8).' — The specific large-margin decimals cited could not be located in any source and conflate the explicit group's pre-post gain (g≈1.4 free production in Goo et al.) with the explicit-OVER-implicit contrast (g≈0.45 free / g≈0.58 constrained). The real contrast is small-to-medium, shrinks on production measures, and is narrowing in newer studies. The large-margin framing is folk amplification of a real but modest directional effect.
- 'Passing a metalinguistic/grammar check (explain or probe success) shows the item is acquired.' — Meta-analyses show the explicit advantage is concentrated on exactly these constrained/metalinguistic measures, which index declarative explicit knowledge, not the implicit system used in spontaneous communication. Treating such a pass as acquisition is the central measurement artifact the literature warns against.
- 'Complex grammar rules should be left to implicit acquisition; only simple rules are worth teaching explicitly.' — Spada & Tomita 2010 found explicit treatment helped both simple and complex features (including complex-form free production), refuting the leave-complex-to-implicit folk rule. (Note: this supports explicit TREATMENT broadly, not specifically long verbal rule statements for complex items.)
- 'Recast-only (never flag errors) is the optimal, gentlest correction policy.' — CF meta-analyses (Li 2010; Lyster & Saito) show recasts are among the WEAKER feedback types, especially on developmentally harder forms; output-pushing prompts and more salient feedback produce larger and (for prompts) durable gains. Recast-only sacrifices effectiveness for meaning-flow and is not empirically optimal.
- 'Mechanical/rote repetition drills build fluency.' — Skill-acquisition theory and form-focused research indicate proceduralization/automatization require form-MEANING practice; mechanical decontextualized repetition does not reliably transfer to spontaneous use. The harness's drill move must be meaningful and varied, not rote — the rote-drill assumption is unsupported folk practice.
- 'Proceduralization completes in a fixed number of trials (~16), usable as a tuning constant.' — This comes from a single small DeKeyser (1997) study using an artificial/limited grammar. It is not a generalizable parameter for natural-language, 1:1 adaptive learning and should not be hardcoded; proceduralization timing must be adaptive to per-item production evidence.
- 'Instructional gains are durable, so once an item looks known it can be retired.' — Delayed-posttest data are sparse and short; declines occur across conditions, and explicit gains specifically decay faster than implicit ones (Li 2010). Durability cannot be assumed, which is exactly why SRS-style resurfacing must persist — but note this cuts AGAINST over-trusting explicit-move successes, not in favor of them.

## Interleaving, blocking & desirable difficulties
**🟢 strong** — Interleaving beats blocking for inductive/category learning overall, but the benefit is moderate and material-dependent, not universal. Brunmair & Richter (2019) meta-analysis (59 studies, 238 effect sizes, 158 samples) found overall Hedges g = 0.42 favoring interleaving, dominated by visual category learning (paintings g = 0.67) and math (g = 0.34), with expository texts ambiguous/nonsignificant. ADVERSARIAL CAVEAT: this meta-analysis is overwhelmingly NON-language inductive material (perceptual/category learning), so transfer to natural-language pedagogy is an EXTRAPOLATION, not direct evidence. The overall g also collapses heterogeneous designs; the moderator structure (below) matters far more than the headline g for a language harness.
- *Effect:* g=0.42 overall; paintings g=0.67; math g=0.34 (Brunmair & Richter 2019) — but mostly non-language material
- *Sources:* Brunmair & Richter (2019), Similarity matters: A meta-analysis of interleaved learning and its moderators, Psychological Bulletin; https://www.psychologie.uni-wuerzburg.de/fileadmin/06020400/2019/Brunmair_Richter_in_press__2019_META-ANALYSIS_OF_INTERLEAVED_LEARNING.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Make interleaving the DEFAULT arbiter sequencing policy (invariant: 'do not block more than K consecutive turns on a single item; resurface a contrasting item within K turns'), but treat interleaving strength as a function of item TYPE. Tag each item as 'category/discrimination' (tense contrasts, gender, case, minimal-pair phonology) vs 'isolated form-meaning' (single new vocab word) and scale interleaving accordingly. Do NOT treat the g=0.42 as a language-specific warrant.

**🟢 strong** — For pure word/foreign-vocabulary form-meaning learning, BLOCKING by semantic-free grouping outperformed interleaving in the meta-analysis (g = -0.39). This is the single most important caveat for a language harness: naive item-interleaving can HURT raw vocabulary acquisition. ('Block' here means studying related items together vs across-category interleaving; spacing of repetitions is a separate axis and still helps.) ADVERSARIAL NOTE: part of the words subgroup evidence (e.g. Carpenter-type French-English vocab) is on IMMEDIATE tests, which is exactly where a desirable-difficulty advantage for interleaving would be expected to be hidden; however the meta-analytic g=-0.39 aggregates across delays, so the blocking advantage for words is NOT purely an immediate-test artifact. Still, delayed-test L2 vocab evidence is thinner than the immediate-test evidence.
- *Effect:* g=-0.39 favoring blocking for words (Brunmair & Richter 2019); some component studies immediate-test only
- *Sources:* Brunmair & Richter (2019), Psychological Bulletin; Pan et al. (2019) reporting Carpenter-type French-English vocab where blocked-by-category beat interleaved on immediate test; https://rickardlab.ucsd.edu/pdf/PTLOR-2019.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Add invariant: 'introduce(new vocab item) is NOT subject to the interleaving requirement; first-exposure and the first few drills of an isolated form-meaning item may be blocked.' Distinguish two arbiter knobs: (1) interleaving REPETITIONS of one item across time = spacing = always good; (2) interleaving DIFFERENT CONTRASTING items in one window = good only for discrimination/category items, not for getting a brand-new word into memory. For new vocab: block early, then space. Because some of the blocking-for-words win is immediate-test, still verify on a DELAYED probe before locking the policy.

**🟢 strong** — The moderator that determines whether interleaving helps is SIMILARITY/DISCRIMINABILITY (discriminative-contrast / sequential-attention account). Interleaving helps most when categories are HARD to tell apart (high between-category similarity, low within-category similarity) and for more complex material; blocking helps when within-category commonality must be noticed (high-discriminability categories).
- *Effect:* meta-regression: stronger interleaving for higher between-category similarity, lower within-category similarity, more complex material (Brunmair & Richter 2019)
- *Sources:* Brunmair & Richter (2019), meta-regression of moderators; Kang & Pashler (2012) discriminative contrast hypothesis; Carvalho & Goldstone (2017) sequential attention theory
- *→ Loop:* Add a per-item-pair 'confusability' attribute to the learner model. Interleaving pressure (and drill/Consolidate design) should be driven by confusability, NOT a generic schedule: when two items are easily confused (ser/estar, por/para, perfective/imperfective, le/lui), the arbiter should deliberately juxtapose them in the same window (contrast-pair drill/probe). When an item is novel/unique and not confusable with anything active, block it. This makes 'interleave vs block' a learned, item-graph decision rather than a fixed phase. This is the BEST-SUPPORTED actionable principle in this dimension.

**🟢 strong** — Interleaving extends to L2 GRAMMAR and produces a classic desirable-difficulty reversal: more errors during training but better delayed retention. Nakata & Suzuki (2019), 115 Japanese EFL learners, 5 English structures: blocking had highest training accuracy but interleaving beat blocking on the 1-week delayed posttest. ADVERSARIAL NOTES: (a) the outcome was a GRAMMATICALITY JUDGMENT TEST — a measure of EXPLICIT/declarative knowledge and metalinguistic discrimination, NOT spontaneous production or communicative use; do not read this as 'acquisition' in the implicit/usage sense. (b) Single study, n=115, one population (Japanese EFL), 1-week horizon only — no evidence it holds at months. (c) The headline d=0.64 reported by the researcher is plausible but was not independently re-derived here; treat the DIRECTION (interleaving > blocking on delayed GJT) as well-supported and the exact magnitude as approximate.
- *Effect:* interleaving > blocking on 1-week delayed grammaticality-judgment test; training accuracy reversed (blocked highest during practice); reported d~0.64 not independently verified (Nakata & Suzuki 2019, MLJ)
- *Sources:* Nakata & Suzuki (2019), Mixing Grammar Exercises Facilitates Long-Term Retention, Modern Language Journal 103, 629-647; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/modl.12581; https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1225042
- *→ Loop:* The arbiter must NOT use within-turn/within-session learner accuracy as the sole signal for Progress vs Repair vs Consolidate. Add invariant: 'high training-phase error under interleaving is expected and must NOT trigger Repair escalation or fallback to blocking.' Score drives against DELAYED retrieval success (spaced probes on later turns), not immediate correctness. Log a 'training accuracy will look worse — this is expected' note so the policy is auditable and not 'fixed' by well-meaning tuning. CAVEAT in design docs that the win is demonstrated on explicit-knowledge GJT at 1 week, not on spontaneous production or long horizons.

**🟢 strong** — Spacing is a robust, transferable desirable difficulty and the most reliable lever — distinct from and MORE GENERAL than interleaving. Cepeda et al. (2006) (839 assessments / 317 experiments) established spacing/lag effects with optimal inter-study interval scaling with retention interval. ADVERSARIAL CORRECTION ON EFFECT SIZE: the original cited a d=0.45–0.85 range, but the high endpoint (d=0.85, Donoghue & Hattie 2021) comes from a Hattie-method meta-analysis that aggregates surface/factual outcomes and is widely critiqued for combining incomparable effect sizes and not screening flawed studies — treat d=0.85 as an OPTIMISTIC ceiling, not a representative figure. The most design-relevant number is the APPLIED/CLASSROOM estimate: Mawson & Kang (2025) meta-analysis of classroom studies found d=0.54 (95% CI 0.31–0.77, 31 effects, N>3000), with LARGER effects for longer retention intervals and FEWER re-exposures. Math-specific spacing is only g≈0.28. So the realistic, transfer-relevant spacing effect for a harness is MODERATE (~0.3–0.55), not large; it shrinks for complex material and in applied settings.
- *Effect:* applied/classroom d=0.54 (Mawson & Kang 2025); math g=0.28; lab range d=0.45 (Donovan & Radosevich 1999) to d=0.85 (Donoghue & Hattie 2021, critiqued/optimistic). Optimal lag grows with retention interval (Cepeda 2006)
- *Sources:* Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted, Rohrer (2006), Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks, Psychological Bulletin; Mawson & Kang (2025), The Distributed Practice Effect on Classroom Learning: A Meta-Analytic Review of Applied Research, Behavioral Sciences, https://www.mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/6/771; Donoghue & Hattie (2021) — Hattie-method, treat as optimistic ceiling; Donovan & Radosevich (1999)
- *→ Loop:* Prioritize SPACING over fancy interleaving in the spine (it is the most reliable AND most transferable lever, but it is MODERATE not large in applied settings — set expectations accordingly). Review drive should weight overdue items by EXPANDING intervals where optimal lag scales with the desired retention horizon (longer target retention -> longer lags). Keep 'surface overdue reviews within N turns' but make N item- and horizon-dependent. Because interleaving's benefit partly REDUCES to spacing, an SRS-style spacing scheduler is the safer empirical backbone (Q1), with discriminative-contrast interleaving layered on top for confusable items — not a phase-machine. Note Mawson & Kang's 'fewer re-exposures' moderator: do not over-drill.

**🟢 strong** — Contextual interference (the motor-learning sibling of interleaving) shows a medium retention/transfer benefit for random>blocked practice but is strongly moderated, especially by AGE, and can be negligible in some populations. Czyż et al. (2024) meta-analysis: transfer SMD=0.55 overall; adults SMD=0.54, older adults SMD=1.28, but YOUNG learners (≤18) SMD=0.12 (negligible). Companion Nature Sci Reports (2024) review found a medium retention benefit for high CI. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: this is MOTOR learning, not language; transfer to natural-language pedagogy is analogical, not direct. The near-zero young-learner effect is a real warning for any harness targeting children/adolescents. The applied-vs-lab gap (lab effects larger than field) further tempers expectations.
- *Effect:* transfer SMD=0.55 overall; young learners SMD=0.12 (negligible); adults SMD=0.54; older adults SMD=1.28 (Czyż et al. 2024); retention benefit medium (Nature Sci Reports 2024)
- *Sources:* Czyż et al. (2024), The effect of contextual interference on transfer in motor learning, Frontiers in Psychology, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11349744/; High contextual interference improves retention in motor learning (2024), Scientific Reports, https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-65753-3
- *→ Loop:* Temper expectations: high-interference benefit shrinks in applied/messy conditions and is NEGLIGIBLE for young learners — analogous to Buhbe's verified finding that interleaved contextualized grammar gained ACCURACY but LOST FLUENCY. So the arbiter should NOT maximize interference for its own sake. Encode GOAL-conditioned interleaving: for automatization/fluency goals (chat move, production) allow lower-interference blocked drilling; reserve high-interference interleaving for accuracy/discrimination goals (correct/probe/contrast drills). The Engage/chat drive tolerates blocking; the Repair/Consolidate-discrimination drives demand interleaving. Add a learner-age/level gate: down-weight aggressive interleaving for very low-level / younger learners.

**🟡 moderate** — Interleaving improves L2 grammar SKILL (conjugation) but only when the contrast is present during/across learning episodes, not bolted on at the end. Pan et al. (2019), adult Spanish preterite vs imperfect: single-session interleaving gave NO advantage (equivalent to blocking on a 2-day test, blocked numerically higher), but cross-session interleaving with contrast beginning at introduction yielded better 1-week retention. Driver = discriminative contrast between tenses. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: this is genuinely a MIXED/NULL-leaning result for interleaving — 2 of 4 experiments showed no interleaving benefit (and numerically favored blocking). The positive cross-session result is the minority condition. Rated moderate, not strong, correctly.
- *Effect:* null/equivalent at 2 days and in single-session designs (blocked numerically higher); cross-session contrast better at 1 week (Exp 3-4); researcher-reported d~0.64 region not independently verified
- *Sources:* Pan, Tajran, Lovelett, Osuna, Rickard (2019), Does Interleaved Practice Enhance Foreign Language Learning?, J. Educational Psychology; https://rickardlab.ucsd.edu/pdf/PTLOR-2019.pdf; https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1230840
- *→ Loop:* Interleaving must be a CROSS-TURN/CROSS-SESSION property, not a cosmetic shuffle inside one drill block — the single-session nulls show within-session shuffling buys little. The lesson ARC must NOT confine a contrast to one 'work' phase; Review/Consolidate should resurface the contrasting member across multiple sessions. Add invariant: 'a confusable contrast pair, once both members are introduced, must be co-activated across at least N separate SESSIONS before either is consolidated.' Benefits appear at delayed (1-week) horizons, so measure success on multi-day spaced probes. Set expectations: same-session interleaving may show NO measurable benefit.

**🟡 moderate** — Interleaving can be an UNDESIRABLE difficulty for low-proficiency / low-achieving learners early on; initial blocked practice before interleaving helps them. Hwang (2025), 107 low-achieving Korean adolescents, L2 English vocab: pure interleaving imposed undesirable difficulty (cognitive overload) for low achievers; blocked practice in the early stage helped DECLARATIVE knowledge development, and a HYBRID (blocked-then-interleaved) produced MORE robust long-term retention than either pure blocking OR pure interleaving. ADVERSARIAL CORRECTION: the original finding under-stated Hwang — Hwang's headline is that the HYBRID beat both, which is direct support for the 'increasing' schedule for weak learners and creates explicit tension with finding #7 (where pure interleaving won for grammar). Note also Hwang's outcome is DECLARATIVE knowledge (explicit), and the abstract/summary was the source (full text behind paywall), so rate moderate.
- *Effect:* qualitative: pure interleaving harmful for low achievers; hybrid (blocked-then-interleaved) > both blocking and interleaving on long-term retention for low achievers (Hwang 2025, Language Learning 75, 5-41). Tension: Nakata-Suzuki found LOW-prior-knowledge learners gained MORE from pure interleaving on grammar GJT
- *Sources:* Hwang (2025), Undesirable Difficulty of Interleaved Practice: The Importance of Initial Blocked Practice for Declarative Knowledge Development in Low-Achieving Adolescents, Language Learning 75, 5-41; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/lang.12659; Nakata & Suzuki (2019) prior-knowledge moderation
- *→ Loop:* Implement proficiency-gated interleaving (challenge-point framework). Add invariant: 'for a learner-item where stability/proficiency is below threshold, first exposures may be blocked; interleaving pressure ramps as item stability rises.' Apply PER ITEM in the learner model, not as a global lesson phase. Surface the unresolved tension to the user: the proficiency moderator points OPPOSITE directions for raw vocab form-meaning (block-first / hybrid for weak learners, per Hwang) vs grammatical-rule discovery (interleaving can help novices, per Nakata-Suzuki) — so gate by item-TYPE, not by a single global proficiency rule. Both outcome measures are explicit knowledge; neither licenses claims about implicit/communicative acquisition.

**🟠 contested** — The 'increasing' / blocked-then-interleaved hybrid is theoretically attractive but did NOT outperform pure interleaving for grammar in the one direct L2-grammar test. Nakata & Suzuki (2019): increasing did not significantly differ from blocked (d=0.37 ns) and did not beat interleaving (interleaving-vs-increasing d=0.28 ns) on the delayed GJT; pure interleaving was numerically best. BUT Hwang (2025) found the hybrid BEAT both for low-achieving vocab learners. So 'always ramp blocked->interleaved' is contested: best for grammar GJT = pure interleaving; best for weak-learner declarative vocab = hybrid. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: both the d=0.37 and d=0.28 are non-significant with a modest n; 'pure interleaving numerically best' is a within-noise ranking, so do not over-weight it either direction. The honest summary is EQUIVOCAL, moderated by item-type and proficiency.
- *Effect:* increasing vs blocked d=0.37 (ns); interleaving vs increasing d=0.28 (ns), pure interleaving numerically best for grammar GJT (Nakata & Suzuki 2019); hybrid > both for low-achiever vocab (Hwang 2025)
- *Sources:* Nakata & Suzuki (2019), Modern Language Journal 103, 629-647; Hwang (2025), Language Learning 75, 5-41; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/lang.12659
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT hard-code a global blocked->interleaved ramp as settled pedagogy. Treat the ramp as a per-item, proficiency- AND item-type-conditional heuristic (justified mainly for weak learners / raw vocab per Hwang) behind a TUNABLE invariant so it can be A/B'd. Flag to the user that the hybrid's superiority is genuinely contested across the two relevant L2 studies and that the within-study rankings are non-significant.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- 'Interleave everything' / interleaving is a universal study-skill upgrade. — Directly refuted by Brunmair & Richter (2019): interleaving HURT pure word/form-meaning learning (g=-0.39) and was nonsignificant for expository texts. The benefit is concentrated in hard-to-discriminate category material. Blanket interleaving in a vocab-heavy language harness would damage acquisition.
- 'Always ramp blocked -> interleaved (the increasing schedule) is the optimal universal progression.' — Contested, not settled. In the only direct L2-grammar test (Nakata & Suzuki 2019) the increasing schedule did NOT beat pure interleaving (d=0.28 ns) and did not differ from blocking (d=0.37 ns); pure interleaving was numerically best. Hwang (2025) supports the hybrid only for LOW-ACHIEVING vocab learners. The ramp is a proficiency- and item-type-conditional heuristic, not a universal law.
- Spacing produces a large (d≈0.85) effect on learning. — The d=0.85 figure comes from Donoghue & Hattie (2021), a Hattie-method meta-analysis criticized for combining incomparable effect sizes, aggregating surface/factual outcomes, and not screening flawed studies. The transfer-relevant applied/classroom estimate (Mawson & Kang 2025) is d=0.54 and math-specific spacing is only g=0.28. Spacing is robust but MODERATE in realistic settings, and shrinks for complex material — not a large effect.
- Within-session shuffling of practice items ('interleaving inside one drill block') delivers the interleaving benefit. — Pan et al. (2019) found single-session interleaving gave NO advantage over blocking (and blocking was numerically higher) on a 2-day test; the benefit only emerged with CROSS-SESSION contrast. Cosmetic same-session shuffling is folk-plausible but unsupported; the effect requires distribution across sessions (i.e. it largely reduces to spacing + cross-session contrast).
- Training-phase accuracy is a valid signal of how well a schedule is teaching (so a strategy that produces more errors during practice is working worse). — The desirable-difficulty reversal contradicts this: in Nakata & Suzuki (2019) blocking had the HIGHEST training accuracy but LOST on delayed retention. Using within-session accuracy to drive Repair/fallback would systematically punish the better long-term strategy. (Caveat: the long-term win is on explicit GJT, not production.)
- Interleaving / high contextual interference benefits apply equally across learners, including children/adolescents and low-proficiency learners. — Czyż et al. (2024) found the contextual-interference transfer benefit is NEGLIGIBLE for young learners (SMD=0.12) vs medium for adults (0.54). Hwang (2025) found pure interleaving imposes undesirable difficulty (cognitive overload) on low achievers. The benefit is strongly age- and proficiency-moderated, not universal.
- Grammaticality-judgment / declarative test gains from interleaving demonstrate language ACQUISITION (implicit, usable competence). — The L2-grammar interleaving wins (Nakata & Suzuki; Hwang) are measured on grammaticality-judgment / declarative-knowledge tests, which index explicit metalinguistic knowledge, not spontaneous production or communicative use. Buhbe's verified result shows interleaving can even REDUCE fluency while raising accuracy — explicit-knowledge gains can trade off against usable fluency.

## Vocabulary acquisition: exposures, frequency, chunks
**🟢 strong** — Incidental vocabulary learning from meaning-focused input is real but retention is leaky: only ~6-17% of target words survive to delayed posttests, and learning is a gradual, multi-exposure accretion of partial word knowledge (form recognition acquired faster than meaning recall). The large pooled g (~1.1 immediate / ~0.93 delayed) is INFLATED by within-design and immediate-test artifacts and by control words being near-zero by construction; the per-word delayed proportion (~6-17%) is the honest practical number, not the g. Treat g as evidence that the effect is non-zero and direction-positive, NOT as a magnitude you can budget against.
- *Effect:* g=1.14 immediate, g=0.93 delayed (Webb, Uchihara & Yanagisawa 2023, Language Teaching, 24 studies, 29 ES, N=2,771); per-word pickup 9-18% immediate / 6-17% delayed. Verified: gains similar across reading/listening/RWL.
- *Sources:* Webb, Uchihara & Yanagisawa (2023), Language Teaching, How effective is L2 incidental vocabulary learning? A meta-analysis; https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-teaching/article/how-effective-is-second-language-incidental-vocabulary-learning-a-metaanalysis/E38E3468FD2090B1FA3051051DE8E70C
- *→ Loop:* Do not treat one input encounter with an item as 'introduced+learned.' The learner model must track word knowledge as graded states (form-recognition < meaning-recognition < meaning-recall), not binary known/unknown. The probe move should test at recall level before Progress advances, because form-recognition (cheapest incidentally) overstates real knowledge. Budget exposures generously: expect single-digit-percent per-encounter yield from pure input.

**🟢 strong** — Deliberate/word-focused study (retrieval, flashcards, sentence-production) yields larger AND more time-efficient vocabulary gains per unit time than pure incidental reading; the two are complementary. This is now anchored by a vocab-specific intentional-learning meta (Webb, Yanagisawa & Uchihara 2020, MLJ, 100 ES / 22 studies): meaning-recall ~60% and form-recall ~58% immediate, dropping to ~39% and ~25% delayed - i.e. deliberate study is strong but ALSO decays sharply (form-recall halves), so spacing is mandatory even here. The generic retrieval-practice g=0.50 (Rowland 2014) is a LAB testing-effect meta in Psychological Bulletin, not L2-vocab-specific, so cite it as convergent support, not as the L2 effect size.
- *Effect:* Intentional vocab: ~60% meaning-recall / ~58% form-recall immediate, ~39% / ~25% delayed (Webb, Yanagisawa & Uchihara 2020, MLJ). Retrieval vs restudy g=0.50 (Rowland 2014, 159 studies, lab/general).
- *Sources:* Webb, Yanagisawa & Uchihara (2020), Modern Language Journal, How Effective Are Intentional Vocabulary-Learning Activities? A Meta-Analysis; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/modl.12671; Rowland (2014), Psychological Bulletin, The Effect of Testing Versus Restudy on Retention: A Meta-Analytic Review; van den Broek et al. (2018), Language Learning, Contextual Richness and Word Learning
- *→ Loop:* The arbiter should NOT default to chat/input for vocab GROWTH. For form-meaning establishment, prefer elicit (retrieval) and drill over input. Weight elicit/drill up for items in the 'newly introduced, shaky' state; reserve input/chat (incidental) for breadth, recycling, collocational depth. Engage drive must not crowd out retrieval on due items. Because even deliberate gains decay (~25% delayed form-recall), schedule spaced re-retrieval rather than one-shot drilling.

**🟢 strong** — Frequency-based ordering is justified by lexical coverage thresholds: ~95% coverage is minimal for acceptable reading comprehension, ~98% optimal. Reading needs ~4,000-5,000 word families (95%) up to ~8,000-9,000 (98%); spoken/listening ~2,000-3,000 families can reach 95%. High-frequency words give disproportionate coverage payoff. Caveat (researcher's, upheld): these figures are corpus/L1/proper-noun-dependent and were estimated on English EFL populations - treat as English-specific and as comprehension thresholds, not learning guarantees.
- *Effect:* 95% = minimal, 98% = optimal (Nation 2006; Laufer & Ravenhorst-Kalovski 2010; van Zeeland & Schmitt 2012); 4-5k families->95% reading, 6-9k->98% (replicated). Listening 2-3k->95%.
- *Sources:* Nation (2006), Canadian Modern Language Review, How Large a Vocabulary Is Needed for Reading and Listening?; Laufer & Ravenhorst-Kalovski (2010), Reading in a Foreign Language, Lexical threshold revisited; https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ887873.pdf
- *→ Loop:* The introduce move must order items by corpus frequency rank (high-frequency first), not textbook theme. Invariant: the comprehensibility check for input should estimate the learner's coverage of the target text; if estimated coverage < ~95%, the text violates i+1 and the arbiter should down-rank it or pre-teach gap items. Coverage = operational i+1 for receptive text (answers Q5). Per-target-language coverage tables must be recalibrated, not borrowed from English.

**🟡 moderate** — Repetition/number of exposures reliably increases incidental uptake, with a medium correlational effect; gains are front-loaded (largest jump in first few encounters, diminishing returns after). Form/meaning recognition reaches ~75-86% by ~8 exposures but meaning recall lags (~55%). NOTE this is a CORRELATIONAL meta (r), so the exposure->learning link is associational, confounded with text salience, learner ability, and word properties; it does not license a causal 'add N exposures => gain X' rule. The '8-16 exposures' figure is folk heuristic, not a meta-analytic constant.
- *Effect:* r=.34 (Uchihara, Webb & Yanagisawa 2019, Language Learning, 26 studies, 45 ES, N=1,918); ~86% form / ~75% meaning recognition vs ~55% recall at 8 exposures. r=.34 = ~12% variance explained; large unexplained residual.
- *Sources:* Uchihara, Webb & Yanagisawa (2019), Language Learning, The Effects of Repetition on Incidental Vocabulary Learning: A Meta-Analysis of Correlational Studies; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/lang.12343
- *→ Loop:* Replace any fixed 'expose N times then done' rule. The Review drive should weight items by a graded mastery deficit; because returns diminish, prefer spacing additional encounters across turns rather than massing. Invariant: an item is not 'consolidated' until it clears a recall-level probe, regardless of raw exposure count, since recall lags recognition substantially. Do not hard-code any exposure count.

**🟡 moderate** — Vocabulary breadth (size) and depth (collocational/associative richness) both predict reading comprehension; breadth is the more robust predictor at lower proficiency, depth gains importance later; for high-frequency words/small vocabularies the two measures largely converge. CAVEAT (added): the breadth/depth->comprehension link is CORRELATIONAL (incl. cross-lagged panel designs); cross-lagged panels strengthen temporal-precedence inference but are not RCTs, so do not read 'train depth -> comprehension rises' as established causation.
- *Effect:* Both significant predictors; breadth more robust at lower proficiency (cross-lagged panels); convergence for high-frequency/small-vocab learners (Schmitt 2014). Effects correlational.
- *Sources:* Schmitt (2014), Language Learning, Size and Depth of Vocabulary Knowledge; Cross-lagged panel analyses of breadth, depth & reading comprehension; https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-04694-2
- *→ Loop:* Sequence the spine breadth-first for beginners (maximize coverage via high-frequency introduce), then shift Consolidate/Review toward depth (collocations, senses, register) as size grows. Stage gate: depth-oriented review of an item earns arbiter priority only once breadth coverage at that frequency band is established. Because the depth->comprehension link is correlational, treat the breadth-then-depth ordering as a reasonable default to be validated by in-harness outcome data, not a proven causal lever.

**🟠 contested** — CONTESTED: The claim that retrieval-during-reading reliably beats inference-from-context for retention does NOT hold cleanly for incidental/contextualized L2 vocab. The cited van den Broek et al. (2022, Cognitive Science) FAILED TO REPLICATE the retrieval advantage of van den Broek et al. (2018): in 2022 the informative STORY-CONTEXT (inference) condition produced HIGHER posttest scores than the retrieval condition, and the authors concluded retrieval benefits are 'less pronounced during incidental, contextualized learning' and depend on RETRIEVAL SUCCESS (failed retrievals during reading may not help, and may cost comprehension). The d=0.42 figure was a pre-registered smallest-effect-of-interest threshold, NOT an obtained effect favoring retrieval. The general testing effect (Rowland g=0.50) is robust for DELIBERATE study; embedding forced retrieval into comprehensible input is a different, weakly-supported manoeuvre.
- *Effect:* 2022 replication REVERSED 2018: inference/context > retrieval in within-subjects experiments; retrieval benefit contingent on retrieval success. d=0.42 = SESOI threshold, not an effect.
- *Sources:* van den Broek et al. (2022), Cognitive Science, Vocabulary Learning During Reading: Benefits of Contextual Inferences Versus Retrieval Opportunities; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cogs.13135; van den Broek et al. (2018), Language Learning (original, partially non-replicated)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT make 'realize input as forced cloze/retrieval' a hard sub-policy. Keep retrieval where it is well-supported - the dedicated elicit/drill moves on already-encoded items - and keep input as comprehensible context. If merging input+elicit (gapped text), GATE it on retrieval success: only gap items the learner can plausibly retrieve (already at >=recognition); for not-yet-encoded items, transparent rich context for comprehension/inference is at least as good. Add invariant: never gap an item below recognition state.

**⚪ weak** — DOWNGRADED: Formulaic sequences/collocations/chunks are processed faster than novel combinations (robust psycholinguistic finding) and plausibly a unit of proficiency, but the headline evidence that chunk knowledge CAUSES perceived oral proficiency is WEAK: the flagship Boers et al. (2006) study is small (experimental N=17 vs control N=15, 32 total, 22 instructional hours) and there is a published CALL FOR REPLICATION (Cambridge, Language Teaching) - i.e. it has not been robustly replicated; a 2011 IRAL follow-up found the chunk-proficiency association depends on target language. There is NO clean pooled effect size for TEACHING formulaic sequences. So: chunks matter as a unit; their teachability/payoff is unestablished, not 'moderate-strong.'
- *Effect:* No pooled d for teaching effect. Boers 2006: underpowered (N=32), unreplicated, call-for-replication published; association language-dependent (IRAL 2011). Processing-speed advantage for formulaic sequences is the robust part.
- *Sources:* Boers, Eyckmans, Kappel, Stengers & Demecheleer (2006), Language Teaching Research (small, contested); Cambridge call for replication of Boers et al. (2006), Language Teaching; Boers & Lindstromberg (2012), Annual Review of Applied Linguistics; https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-teaching/article/abs/replication-research-in-pedagogical-approaches-to-spoken-fluency-and-formulaic-sequences-a-call-for-replication-of-wood-2009-and-boers-eyckmans-kappel-stengers-demecheleer-2006/8D7676F9CA677D380F6B3870CE20DA9F
- *→ Loop:* Keep chunks as first-class items (item-type field {word|collocation|formula}) on the well-supported grounds that (a) they are processed faster and (b) compositional pickup of fixed phrases is unreliable. But do NOT over-invest the arbiter in chunk-teaching as a high-yield lever - efficacy is unproven. Treat chunk introduction as a hypothesis to A/B test in the harness, not a settled invariant. Prefer introducing high-frequency collocations the learner will actually re-encounter, and measure whether explicit chunk attention beats incidental recycling.

**⚪ weak** — DOWNGRADED: 'Higher cognitive engagement => better retention' is only weakly predictive as formalized. The Involvement Load Hypothesis meta (Yanagisawa & Webb 2021, Language Learning, 398 ES / 42 studies) explains just ~15% of variance immediate and ~5.1% delayed, and crucially the SEARCH component did NOT help and actually DECREASED retention (~-5.1%); only EVALUATION (and to a lesser degree need) contributed. Some primary studies find LOW-involvement tasks outperforming high. So the survivable, narrower claim is: GENERATIVE/EVALUATIVE use of a word (using it in a produced sentence, judging its form) aids retention; raw 'engagement' and especially effortful searching/look-up do not, and may hurt.
- *Effect:* ILH explains ~15% immediate / ~5.1% delayed variance (Yanagisawa & Webb 2021, 398 ES, 42 studies); search component NEGATIVE (~-5.1%); evaluation strongest. ILH vs TFA unresolved.
- *Sources:* Yanagisawa & Webb (2021), Language Learning, To What Extent Does the Involvement Load Hypothesis Predict Incidental L2 Vocabulary Learning? A Meta-Analysis; https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1296679; Hulstijn & Laufer (2001), Involvement Load Hypothesis (original)
- *→ Loop:* Encode an 'engagement load' score per move realization but weight it on EVALUATION/PRODUCTION only (use-in-a-sentence, form-judgement, recast that forces evaluation), NOT on 'search/look-up,' which the data say is neutral-to-harmful. Prefer elicit-with-production over recognition drill for items already at recognition level. Do not treat dictionary-search/effortful-lookup steps as pedagogically valuable in their own right.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- A word needs '8-16 exposures' to be learned (fixed exposure count). — Folk heuristic, not a meta-analytic constant. The repetition meta is correlational (r=.34) and required exposures vary by tested knowledge aspect (recognition vs recall), word properties, and proficiency. Recall lags recognition substantially even at 8 exposures (~55% vs ~86%). Do not hard-code any number; gate on a recall-level probe instead.
- Embedding forced retrieval (cloze/gaps) into reading input reliably beats letting learners infer word meaning from rich context ('testing effect during reading'). — The cited van den Broek (2022) failed to replicate the 2018 retrieval advantage and found inference/context superior for incidental contextualized learning; the benefit depends on retrieval success. Importing the lab testing effect (which is robust for deliberate study) wholesale into comprehensible input is unsupported and can backfire when retrieval fails.
- Teaching formulaic sequences/chunks via explicit 'noticing' reliably raises oral proficiency (Lexical Approach payoff). — Rests largely on Boers et al. (2006), an underpowered study (N=32 total) with a published call for replication and a language-dependent follow-up; no pooled teaching effect size exists. Chunks matter as processing units, but their teachability and proficiency payoff are unestablished, not a settled lever.
- Higher 'involvement load' / more cognitive engagement in a task always yields better vocabulary retention (and effortful dictionary search counts as productive engagement). — The ILH meta explains only ~5% of delayed variance and the SEARCH component is negatively associated with retention; some studies show low-involvement tasks winning. Only evaluation/generative production is reliably beneficial. 'Engagement' as a blanket good is folk wisdom; effortful look-up is neutral-to-harmful.
- Large pooled effect sizes for incidental vocabulary learning (g~1.1) mean reading/listening input is a high-yield way to grow vocabulary. — The g is inflated by immediate-test and within-design artifacts and near-zero control words; the honest delayed per-word retention is only ~6-17%. A large standardized effect against a near-zero baseline is not a large practical yield. Engagement/exposure is being mistaken for durable learning.
- English-derived coverage figures (e.g., 8-9k word families for 98%) transfer directly to other target languages. — These thresholds are corpus-, L1-, morphology-, and proper-noun-dependent and were estimated on English EFL populations. They are comprehension thresholds, not learning guarantees, and must be recalibrated per target language rather than borrowed.

## Proficiency measurement & adaptive assessment (i+1 calibration and the learner-model level signal)
**🟢 strong** — CEFR descriptors are Rasch-scaled and replicable but are deliberately language-independent 'can-do' functions; operationalization into specific words/grammar requires corpus-based profiles (EVP, EGP, CEFR-J). Accurate and well-sourced.
- *Effect:* North & Schneider (1998) Rasch-scaled; replication anchored r=.86 (same/adjacent level).
- *Sources:* North & Schneider (1998); North (2007) Modern Language Journal; CEFR illustrative-descriptor replication, Lancaster EPrints 207097; English Vocabulary Profile / English Grammar Profile (englishprofile.org); CEFR-J (github.com/openlanguageprofiles)
- *→ Loop:* Seed the curriculum and known-item set from a corpus-based CEFR profile (EVP lexis, EGP/CEFR-J grammar) so introduce/review operate over real level-tagged inventories and the coverage check has a frequency/level table. Do NOT ask the LLM to invent 'A2 vocabulary' — pin to EVP/EGP in code (Q4).

**🟡 moderate** — IRT-style joint estimation of item difficulty and learner ability from response logs (Duolingo Birdbrain) is a plausible, scalable engineering approach to keep learners near an optimal challenge zone. IRT/CAT is the validated psychometric basis for ASSESSMENT. HOWEVER: the claim that Birdbrain-leveled content 'makes learners learn more and retain more' rests ONLY on Duolingo's own internal A/B tests, which are not peer-reviewed, report no Cohen's d, and conflate engagement/return-rate metrics (which Duolingo optimizes commercially) with actual L2 acquisition. There is no independent replication of Birdbrain learning gains. The TACL 2020 paper validates the Duolingo English Test as an ASSESSMENT instrument (test-retest ~0.90, alignment with TOEFL/IELTS) — it does NOT validate that adapting instructional difficulty causes more learning.
- *Effect:* IRT/CAT for assessment: strong (DET test-retest ~0.90, criterion alignment significant). Birdbrain INSTRUCTIONAL gains: no public d; industry A/B only; engagement-vs-learning confound unresolved.
- *Sources:* Settles, LaFlair, Hagiwara (2020) TACL 8:247-263 (validates DET as assessment, not instruction); blog.duolingo.com/learning-how-to-help-you-learn-introducing-birdbrain (industry, non-peer-reviewed); Bicknell, Brust, Settles (2023) IEEE Spectrum (popular-press, no effect sizes)
- *→ Loop:* Keep the IRT-style per-item theta as the learner-model level signal (sound for routing the probe/CAT and for assessment). But do NOT treat 'adapt difficulty to theta -> more learning' as established. Instrument the harness to measure actual acquisition (delayed retrieval, transfer to novel contexts), not just turn-level success or session-return, so you can detect if difficulty-matching is buying engagement rather than learning.

**🟡 moderate** — Item difficulty can be predicted a priori from linguistic features (frequency/CEFR band, length, syntactic complexity) and these correlate with operational IRT difficulty well enough to pre-calibrate generated material without a pilot. This is genuinely useful and the most solid finding in the set. CAVEAT: the exact NLP-vs-IRT correlation coefficient could not be extracted (paywalled); 'strong' rests on the abstract and secondary sources, and a-priori estimates are coarse — they rank-order difficulty but carry meaningful per-item error.
- *Effect:* Reported strong correlation (exact r not verifiable from primary source); DET reliability ~0.90 is for the test, not for the a-priori difficulty estimate's precision.
- *Sources:* Settles, LaFlair, Hagiwara (2020) TACL 8:247-263 (exact r not independently verified); AutoIRT / BanditCAT (2024) arxiv.org/pdf/2410.21033; DET Technical Manual (2023)
- *→ Loop:* Give the harness the cheap difficulty estimator (frequency band + length/clause-count + CEFR-tagged grammar) to pre-score candidate moves so the arbiter can rank options by predicted difficulty without waiting for a response. Treat the estimate as rank-ordering with error: use it to pick among candidates, then CORRECT theta from observed outcomes. Keeps difficulty estimation in code (Q4), LLM realizes under constraint.

**🟡 moderate** — ML/embedding CEFR classifiers are reliable only to +/-1 level; exact-level accuracy is moderate (~62-81%) and dataset/language-dependent; traditional readability formulas (Flesch) are weak for L2. Sound and appropriately hedged.
- *Effect:* Exact-level ~62-81%; 3-band ~83-95%; adjacent ~99% (dataset-specific, not generalized across languages).
- *Sources:* Xia, Kochmar, Briscoe (2016) arxiv.org/pdf/1603.08868; Sung et al. (2015) Modern Language Journal (modl.12213)
- *→ Loop:* Use the CEFR classifier as a COARSE guardrail (reject text 2+ bands above level), not a precise dial. Do fine i+1 targeting via per-item theta + a soft coverage preference + a novel-item cap. Treat single CEFR labels as a human-readable summary of theta, not the control variable.

**🟠 contested** — Lexical coverage relates to comprehension, but the sharp 95%/98% THRESHOLDS are NOT robustly established and the cited 'replication' actually UNDERCUTS them. Hu & Nation (2000) was a small lab study using a nonword-substitution paradigm (artificial unknown words), not authentic unknown vocabulary. Kremmel et al. (2023) — cited here as a confirming replication — in fact found NO significant comprehension differences across 90/95/98% coverage, found CONTRADICTORY orderings (100%>98% for narrative; 98%>100% for expository), saw participants score as low as 7-9/30 even at 100% coverage, and explicitly concludes 'perhaps too much is made of reaching certain lexical coverage points.' Schmitt, Jiang & Grabe (2011) found only a LINEAR relationship (r=.41, medium) with NO threshold ensuring comprehension. Coverage is necessary-ish but far from sufficient, and the 98% number is not a hard cliff.
- *Effect:* Schmitt et al. (2011): linear r=.41 (medium), no threshold. Kremmel et al. (2023): no significant differences across 90/95/98%; threshold NOT replicated. Hu & Nation (2000): nonword paradigm, lab, small n.
- *Sources:* Hu & Nation (2000) (nonword-substitution lab paradigm; threshold claim); Kremmel et al. (2023) Language Learning 73 lang.12622 (REFUTES clean threshold; 'too much is made of...coverage points'); Schmitt, Jiang, Grabe (2011) Modern Language Journal (linear r=.41, no threshold); Laufer (2020); Pellicer-Sanchez et al. 'Lexical Coverage and Reading Comprehension Revisited'
- *→ Loop:* Keep a lexical-coverage CHECK as a useful guardrail on generated input, but DOWNGRADE the proposed hard invariant. Do not veto text purely for falling below 98% (or even 95%) — there is no replicated cliff there. Instead: (a) target high coverage (>=95%) as a soft preference, (b) cap the absolute count of NEW/unknown items per text (e.g. <=1-2 novel forms) since that is what 'i+1' operationally controls, and (c) recognize coverage does NOT ensure comprehension, so pair input with comprehension checks (probe/elicit) rather than trusting a coverage number.

**🟠 contested** — Comprehensible-input as the SOLE mechanism (strong Input Hypothesis / 'i+1') is contested and the +1 increment is not independently measurable. Correctly rated. Strengthened: even 'comprehensible input is sufficient' lacks clean meta-analytic support; output, interaction, and feedback (recast/correct/elicit) add value, and extensive-reading effects are positive but moderate and often measured on engagement/exposure rather than controlled acquisition gains.
- *Effect:* No clean meta-analytic d for 'i+1'; +1 not independently measurable. Extensive-reading meta-analyses: positive but moderate.
- *Sources:* Krashen (1985) Input Hypothesis (primary, contested); Extensive-reading meta-analysis, Educ. Psych. Review (2025); Frontiers/PMC (2025) neuro-ecological critique (PMC12577063)
- *→ Loop:* Do not let a single 'comprehensible input' philosophy own the loop (Q3). Operationalize i+1 as a MEASURABLE bundle (theta-matched difficulty as a soft ~85% prior + high-but-not-cliff coverage + novel-item cap + within-one-CEFR-band), and keep elicit/recast/correct first-class so output and feedback aren't starved. Treat 'pure input' as one arbitrated move, not the spine.

**⚪ weak** — The '85% rule' (optimal training error ~15.87%) is an ANALYTIC result for stochastic-gradient binary classifiers and was demonstrated ONLY in artificial/biologically-plausible neural nets — NOT in human L2 learners. Its transfer to human language acquisition is theoretical and has no meta-analytic support. It is a defensible calibration heuristic, not a law of human learning, and the precise 85% figure should not be reified.
- *Effect:* Optimal error 15.87% is exact ONLY for the modeled gradient-descent class; zero human-L2 effect-size evidence. Treat as heuristic prior, not measured target.
- *Sources:* Wilson, Shenhav, Straccia, Cohen (2019) Nature Communications 10:4646 (ANN/biologically-plausible nets only; authors do not claim human-instruction validation)
- *→ Loop:* Use ~85% predicted success as a SOFT prior/default, not a hard invariant. Downgrade the proposed 'reject any item with p(correct)<0.70 or >0.95' from a hard veto to a soft penalty — desirable-difficulty work suggests retrieval BELOW 70% success can still aid long-term retention, so a hard floor risks vetoing beneficial difficulty. Make the band tunable and learn it empirically per learner from acquisition outcomes rather than hard-coding 85%.

**⚪ weak** — Half-Life Regression is a data-driven forgetting model, but the '45% error reduction' and HLR's superiority are OVERSTATED. The 45% figure is relative to weak baselines on a Duolingo log that is heavily skewed toward high-recall interactions, so a model can score well by exploiting the distribution. An independent reanalysis (Papousek) reported HLR achieving AUC ~0.537 — barely above chance — and noted a trivial baseline was competitive. HLR assumes a CONSTANT/exponential forgetting rate that misses nonlinear forgetting dynamics, and SuperMemo's later benchmarks found LSTM/other models OUTPERFORM HLR. HLR is a reasonable starting heuristic, NOT a validated gold standard.
- *Effect:* Reported 45% error reduction is baseline-relative on recall-skewed data; independent AUC ~0.537 (near chance); outperformed by later models (SuperMemo LSTM, FSRS-family).
- *Sources:* Settles & Meeder (2016) ACL (original; in-house Duolingo logs); papousek.github.io/analysis-of-half-life-regression-model-made-by-duolingo.html (independent critique: AUC ~0.537, trivial baseline competitive); SuperMemo (2023) LSTM benchmarks (HLR outperformed); Expertium FSRS benchmark
- *→ Loop:* Use an estimated-recall signal to drive Review urgency (weight Review by 1 - estimated recall; surface near the ~50% recall point for desirable difficulty). But do NOT commit to HLR specifically — its constant-forgetting assumption is weak and it loses to newer models. Prefer a better-benchmarked open scheduler (e.g. FSRS) or a simple, transparent recall estimate, and keep the scheduler swappable. Validate any scheduler on YOUR logs against a trivial baseline before trusting it.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- The 98% lexical-coverage threshold is a hard cliff that should be a veto invariant on generated input. — The threshold derives from a small lab study using artificial nonwords (Hu & Nation 2000). The cited 'replication' (Kremmel et al. 2023) found no significant comprehension differences across 90/95/98% coverage, contradictory orderings, and low comprehension even at 100% coverage, explicitly cautioning that 'too much is made of reaching certain lexical coverage points.' Schmitt et al. (2011) found only a medium linear correlation (r=.41) with no threshold. Coverage does not ensure comprehension; the sharp cliff is folk wisdom dressed as a finding.
- '85% accuracy is the optimal difficulty for learning' as a universal target for human learners. — Proven only as an analytic optimum for stochastic-gradient binary classifiers and demonstrated in artificial neural nets (Wilson et al. 2019). No meta-analytic or controlled human-L2 evidence. Reifying 15.87% error as a hard control target overstates a heuristic; desirable-difficulty research suggests harder-than-85% retrieval can aid long-term retention.
- HLR is a validated 'gold standard' that reduces recall-prediction error by 45%+ and should drive Review timing. — The 45% figure is relative to weak baselines on a high-recall-skewed Duolingo log; an independent reanalysis reported near-chance AUC (~0.537) with a trivial baseline competitive, and HLR's constant-forgetting assumption is outperformed by later models (SuperMemo LSTM, FSRS). It is a starting heuristic, not a validated standard.
- Adapting instructional difficulty to learner ability (Birdbrain) demonstrably increases LEARNING. — Evidence is Duolingo's internal, non-peer-reviewed A/B testing reporting engagement/return-rate and unspecified 'learning' gains with no public effect size and no independent replication. Higher return rates are an engagement metric a commercial product optimizes; equating it with acquisition is the classic engagement-as-learning confound. The peer-reviewed validation covers the DET as an assessment instrument, not difficulty-adaptation as instruction.
- 'i+1' comprehensible input is a precise, operationalizable construct that can anchor the control loop. — The +1 increment is not independently measurable (rated contested even by the original researcher). The strong Input Hypothesis is contested; output/interaction/feedback add value; extensive-reading effects are moderate and often confounded with exposure/engagement. 'Just give i+1 input' is under-specified folk wisdom.

## Motivation, affect, anxiety & gamification
**🟢 strong** — Foreign Language Anxiety is robustly, moderately NEGATIVELY correlated with L2 achievement across all skills. Independent meta-analyses converge: Botes, Dewaele & Greiff (2020) FLCAS meta-analysis (99 ES, N=14,128) general achievement r=-.39; Teimouri et al. multi-country meta-analysis (105 samples, N=19,933, 23 countries, 216 ES) mean r=-.36. Speaking anxiety is typically the strongest contributor. ADVERSARIAL CORRECTIONS: (a) The researcher cited 'IJELS 67 studies N=14,128' AND 'Botes 99 ES N=14,128' as two separate convergent meta-analyses — these are almost certainly the SAME dataset (identical N=14,128), so the apparent 'multiple independent meta-analyses converge' is partly double-counting; true independent corroboration is Botes vs Teimouri, both ~r=-.36 to -.39. The convergence is still real but on TWO, not four, independent pools. (b) CAUSALITY: the proposal's framing of anxiety as a 'manipulable mediator' the harness can lower to raise achievement is NOT well-supported in that direction. Alamer, Alrabai & Sparks (2025, Language Teaching Research) ran an EXPERIMENT and found the dominant causal path runs achievement->lower anxiety, not anxiety-reduction->achievement. Anxiety-achievement is correlational and reciprocal; reducing anxiety may be necessary-but-not-sufficient and must be validated against actual mastery (probe/review), never credited as learning on its own. Effect is moderate, not large.
- *Effect:* r = -.36 to -.39 (two genuinely independent pools: Botes 2020 & Teimouri; range across measures -.33 to -.40)
- *Sources:* Botes, Dewaele & Greiff (2020), J. for the Psychology of Language Learning, 99 ES N=14,128, general achievement r=-.39 (files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1352561.pdf); Teimouri, Goetze & Plonsky multi-country meta-analysis, 105 samples N=19,933, r=-.36 (Studies in Second Language Acquisition); Alamer, Alrabai & Sparks (2025) 'Reducing language anxiety by increasing language achievement: A new experimental study', Language Teaching Research (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13621688251322840)
- *→ Loop:* Anxiety reduction is an evidence-backed environmental objective but NOT a proven lever on learning by itself — instrument it as a guardrail, not a learning optimizer. Concrete: (1) Make 'recast' the DEFAULT error-handling move; invariant: arbiter may not fire 'correct' (explicit correction+rule) more than once per N turns on the same learner unless a Repair drive threshold is exceeded. (2) Add an affect proxy to the learner model (latency spikes, drop in utterance length, self-corrections, help-seeking, abandonment) — but treat it as low-confidence (no validated mapping for an LLM 1:1 context). (3) When anxiety proxy is high, down-weight 'elicit'/'drill'/'probe' and up-weight 'input'/'chat'/'recast'. (4) Invariant: never stack consecutive 'elicit' moves on a struggling item. (5) Because causality likely runs achievement->lower-anxiety, the PRIMARY anxiety intervention is keeping the i+1 competence band so the learner succeeds — anxiety drops as a byproduct of earned success, not via mood management alone.

**🟢 strong** — Self-Determination Theory predicts L2 outcomes, but the effect is modest and asymmetric. Alamer, Saeedy Robat, Elahi Shirvan & Ryan (2025, Educational Psychology Review; multilevel meta-analysis, ~21 studies, N~24,470) found AUTONOMOUS forms positively predict achievement: intrinsic r=.26, identified r=.16; CONTROLLED forms (external, introjected) show no significant relationship (near zero / weakly negative). Headline: internalized motivation helps modestly; externally-driven motivation does essentially nothing for achievement. Autonomous motivation also correlates with reduced anxiety. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: effect sizes are SMALL (r=.16-.26, i.e. ~3-7% variance), correlational, and likely partly reciprocal (achievement->motivation as much as the reverse) — same causal-direction caveat as anxiety. The 'r=.23 autonomous' aggregate is consistent with the source. Do not overstate: these are real but small correlational levers.
- *Effect:* intrinsic r=.26; identified r=.16; autonomous aggregate r~.23; external/introjected/controlled r~0 (ns) — all SMALL and correlational
- *Sources:* Alamer, Saeedy Robat, Elahi Shirvan & Ryan (2025) 'Self-Determination Theory and Language Learning: A Multilevel Meta-Analysis', Educational Psychology Review (link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-025-10038-y; selfdeterminationtheory.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/2025_AlamerRobatEtAl_L2.pdf); Noels et al. (2003) 'Why Are You Learning a Second Language?', Language Learning
- *→ Loop:* Design the Engage drive around AUTONOMY + COMPETENCE, NOT external incentives (meta shows external motivation inert for achievement). (1) COMPETENCE = the i+1 controller: keep success rate ~70-85% so the learner repeatedly experiences earned success; tie Progress-vs-Consolidate arbitration to this band, not a fixed pace. (2) AUTONOMY: 'chat'/topic selection lets the learner steer; bias toward learner-chosen content under high autonomy-need. Q3 fork: a blended 'conversation can override curriculum' policy has direct (if small) evidence. (3) RELATEDNESS: weakest lever for a solo LLM harness and effect sizes are small anyway — do not over-invest; a warm responsive persona is plausibly sufficient (LOW evidence for this context).

**🟢 strong** — The overjustification effect is real: salient extrinsic, tangible, expected rewards for an already-interesting activity erode intrinsic motivation. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999, Psychological Bulletin, 128 studies) — engagement-contingent d=-0.40, completion-contingent d=-0.36, performance-contingent d=-0.28 undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation; verbal/informational feedback does NOT undermine and can enhance. Reward-heavy gamification raises EXTRINSIC more than intrinsic motivation. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: these undermining effects are small-to-moderate and were historically contested (Cameron & Pierce 1994 argued the effect is limited to specific reward contingencies); the modern consensus is that TANGIBLE, EXPECTED, performance-independent rewards undermine, while informational feedback and unexpected rewards do not — so the actionable rule is about reward FRAMING, which is robust and directly usable.
- *Effect:* Deci/Koestner/Ryan 1999: tangible expected rewards undermine intrinsic motivation d=-0.28 to -0.40 (small-to-moderate); informational feedback neutral-to-positive
- *Sources:* Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) meta-analysis of 128 studies, Psychological Bulletin 125:627-668 (home.ubalt.edu/tmitch/642/articles%20syllabus/Deci%20Koestner%20Ryan%20meta%20IM%20psy%20bull%2099.pdf); Cameron & Pierce (1994) counter-meta-analysis (context for the historical debate)
- *→ Loop:* Make rewards INFORMATIONAL, not controlling. When realizing any Engage/feedback move, phrase progress as competence feedback ('you nailed the past tense 8/10 now') NOT tokens ('+50 XP'). Style invariant: reward language references the SKILL, not a currency. Never make continued access or core content contingent on points (the controlling pattern that crowds out intrinsic motivation). Reinforces the SDT finding that controlled motivation is inert for achievement.

**🟡 moderate** — Gamification produces a SMALL genuine learning effect that is fragile and smaller than its engagement effect. Sailer & Homner (2020, Educational Psychology Review): cognitive g=0.49, motivational g=0.36, behavioral g=0.25 — all characterized by the authors as SMALL effects. CRITICAL CAVEAT (confirmed): only the cognitive effect stayed stable under high methodological rigor; motivational and behavioral effects were UNSTABLE. ADVERSARIAL ADDITIONS: (a) Even the 'stable' cognitive g=0.49 is vulnerable to confounded comparison groups — gamified conditions often add feedback/goals/practice the control lacks, so the effect is partly 'better-designed-instruction' rather than 'game elements'. (b) The literature is heterogeneous and at risk of publication bias (small-study effects common in edtech meta-analyses). (c) The cognitive benefit is largely attributable to the goal/feedback/challenge-calibration that ALSO defines good i+1 and SRS. Net: engagement gains are robust and cheap; learning gains are small, contingent, and easy to over-claim.
- *Effect:* Cognitive g=0.49 (stable but possibly confounded by comparison-group design); motivational g=0.36, behavioral g=0.25 (both UNSTABLE under rigor). Downgraded strong->moderate due to confound/publication-bias risk on the one 'stable' effect
- *Sources:* Sailer & Homner (2020) 'The Gamification of Learning: A Meta-Analysis', Educational Psychology Review 32:77-112 (link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09498-w); Examining the effectiveness of gamification: a meta-analysis (2023), PMC10591086 (corroborating heterogeneity)
- *→ Loop:* DO NOT let the Engage drive optimize a gamification proxy (points/streak) as if it were learning. Invariant: Engage may NEVER preempt a fired Repair or overdue Review — gamification rides on top of, never displaces, the SRS/repair spine. Realize 'gamification' as immediate-feedback + clear-goal + calibrated-difficulty framing on EXISTING moves, not a bolt-on points economy (since the learning-effective part IS good goal/feedback/difficulty design). Log any streak/points UI as a RETENTION mechanism, separate from any learning metric.

**🟡 moderate** — Streaks / points / leaderboards / notifications improve RETENTION-TO-APP and time-on-task, but published evidence does NOT show they improve learning PER UNIT of practice; any learning benefit is INDIRECT, mediated entirely by dosage ('more practice happened'). This is loss-aversion / variable-reward / social-comparison habit engineering, not a learning mechanism. Leaderboards carry documented DOWNSIDE: Hanus & Fox (2015, 16-week classroom longitudinal RCT-style) found the gamified (leaderboard+badges) course REDUCED intrinsic motivation, satisfaction and empowerment over time AND lowered final-exam grades, mediated by reduced intrinsic motivation — i.e. demonstrated HARM, not mere neutrality, especially for comparison-based elements. ADVERSARIAL NOTE: the 'no learning gain per trial' claim is an inference from absence-of-evidence + dosage logic (no clean public RCT isolates streak effect on learning-per-trial); the Duolingo retention figures are industry/blog data, not peer-reviewed. So: retention effect = well-evidenced behaviorally; learning-per-trial = unproven; leaderboard harm = directly evidenced in at least one rigorous study.
- *Effect:* Retention/habit: large business effect (industry data, not peer-reviewed). Learning-per-trial: no credible positive ES (absence-based inference). Leaderboard/points HARM: Hanus & Fox showed reduced intrinsic motivation AND lower exam grades over 16 weeks
- *Sources:* Hanus & Fox (2015) 'Assessing the effects of gamification in the classroom...', Computers & Education 80:152-161 (sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0360131514002000) — gamified course LOWERED exam grades via reduced intrinsic motivation; Mazal, 'How Duolingo reignited user growth' (lennysnewsletter.com) — streak/DAU data (industry, non-peer-reviewed)
- *→ Loop:* Separate two log metrics explicitly: RETENTION (returned / dosage) vs LEARNING (item mastery via probe/review accuracy). The Engage drive may target retention; the arbiter must NOT reward itself for retention as if it were mastery. Given Hanus & Fox shows comparative/leaderboard elements can LOWER actual achievement, default to NO leaderboard; if included, gate behind opt-in + invariant: never surface comparative ranking to a learner whose recent success-rate is below the competence band. Prefer streaks (self-referential, autonomy-compatible) over leaderboards (other-referential, evidenced harm).

**⚪ weak** — Flow is associated with better L2 task performance, but the evidence is much weaker than for anxiety/SDT/overjustification — mostly cross-sectional, correlational, single-context (Asian EFL, computer-mediated), with NO robust L2-specific meta-analytic effect size for flow->achievement. The frequently-cited 0.48 figure is the generic engagement/persistence->achievement relationship, NOT a clean flow->L2-learning meta-analysis. Flow's actionable content reduces to its antecedents — clear goals, immediate feedback, challenge matched to skill — which are exactly the i+1 / SRS / feedback mechanisms already supported by stronger literatures. Treat flow as a design heuristic, not an independent evidence-backed target.
- *Effect:* No robust L2-specific meta-analytic ES; generic engagement-achievement ~0.48 is NOT flow-specific
- *Sources:* Aubrey, Zhang & Zhou (2025) 'Flow in language learning: A systematic review of methodological features', 123 studies 2003-2025 (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13621688251389358); Egbert (2003) flow in language learning (foundational, correlational)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT add a separate 'flow' drive or measure flow directly. Encode flow's three antecedents as arbiter constraints you already need: (1) clear goals = every turn has a legible micro-objective; (2) immediate feedback = recast/feedback latency invariant (respond to learner production within the same turn); (3) challenge=skill = the i+1 competence-band controller (~70-85% success). If those three hold, flow is a likely emergent byproduct; manage it via the difficulty controller, not a bespoke mechanism.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- 'Gamification improves learning' (points/badges/streaks make people learn more). — Engagement/time-on-task effects are robust, but learning effects are SMALL (cognitive g=0.49), rigor-fragile (motivational/behavioral effects vanish under methodological rigor in Sailer & Homner 2020), and the surviving cognitive effect is plausibly confounded by gamified arms also adding better feedback/goals/practice. The learning-effective part reduces to good goal/feedback/difficulty design, not game mechanics per se.
- 'Leaderboards motivate learners.' — Contested and evidenced to HARM. Hanus & Fox (2015), a 16-week classroom study, found a leaderboard+badges condition REDUCED intrinsic motivation, satisfaction, and empowerment over time and LOWERED final-exam grades (mediated by reduced intrinsic motivation). Social-comparison ranking is especially risky for lower-ranked learners and for anxiety.
- 'Reward economies (XP/points as currency) drive learning.' — Overjustification effect (Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999, 128 studies, d=-0.28 to -0.40): tangible, expected, performance-independent rewards undermine intrinsic motivation. SDT meta-analysis (Alamer et al. 2025) shows controlled/external motivation is essentially inert for achievement (r~0). Points-as-currency shifts locus of motivation outward; reframe as informational competence feedback instead.
- 'Flow is an independent, measurable design target for L2 learning.' — No robust L2-specific meta-analytic effect size for flow->achievement; evidence is cross-sectional, correlational, single-context (Asian EFL). The cited 0.48 is generic engagement-achievement, not flow. Flow's actionable content collapses into its antecedents (clear goals, immediate feedback, challenge=skill) already covered by i+1 + SRS + recast latency.
- 'Reducing anxiety will raise achievement' (anxiety treated as a causal lever the harness pulls to produce learning). — Anxiety<->achievement is correlational and reciprocal; the one experimental test (Alamer, Alrabai & Sparks 2025) finds the dominant causal direction is achievement->lower anxiety, not the reverse. Anxiety reduction is a legitimate environmental guardrail but must not be credited as causing learning; validate any anxiety-targeting move against actual probe/review mastery.

## Debunked / contested techniques to AVOID (anti-patterns the harness must NOT do)
**🟢 strong** — Massed practice / cramming for retention is inferior to spaced practice; avoid clustering repetitions of the same item within a session for long-term gain. This is the STRONGEST finding in the set and survives scrutiny. CORRECTION: the specific incidental-learning figures the researcher quoted (spaced g=1.51 vs massed g=0.97 immediate; g=1.71 vs g=0.58 delayed) could NOT be confirmed in the Kim & Webb abstract and should not be quoted as precise until checked against the primary PDF — they may be from a sub-analysis or a different source. What IS confirmed: 48 experiments, 98 ES, N=3411; spaced significantly > massed; distributed (long vs short spacing) effect g=0.40 on delayed posttest; advantage grows on delayed tests. Direction and robustness are solid; the eye-catching >1.5 effect sizes are unverified.
- *Effect:* Kim & Webb 2022 (Language Learning 72:269-319; 48 experiments, 98 ES, N=3411): spaced>massed, advantage grows on delayed tests; distributed (long vs short spacing) g=0.40 delayed; large feedback effects (immediate g=1.04). UNVERIFIED: the specific g=1.51/0.97/1.71/0.58 incidental figures.
- *Sources:* Kim & Webb 2022, Language Learning (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/lang.12479); Kim 2023, Modern Language Journal (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/modl.12879)
- *→ Loop:* Anti-pattern: drill must NOT mass-repeat one item back-to-back to 'cement' it. INVARIANT: cap consecutive drill on the same item (no item drilled more than k times within one session). Review drive is the primary repetition mechanism, weighted by an expanding-interval SRS schedule targeting CROSS-SESSION intervals (where the delayed-test advantage is largest), not within-session loops. Validates Q1 keeping an SRS scheduler inside the Review drive while the spine stays drive-arbiter.

**🟡 moderate** — Immediate, exhaustive over-correction of every error is an anti-pattern, and recasts (non-flagging) are NOT clearly superior to prompts. Feedback works, but blanket immediacy and recast-only are the wrong defaults. CORRECTION: Lyster & Saito 2010 was published in STUDIES IN SECOND LANGUAGE ACQUISITION (32:265-302), not Language Learning as the researcher cited; n=827, 15 classroom studies, prompts>recasts confirmed, recasts weaker on free-response measures. Li 2010 overall CF d=0.61 (RE 0.64) confirmed; nuance to keep: explicit feedback beats implicit on immediate/short-delay tests, but IMPLICIT is better MAINTAINED on long-delay — so 'implicit better' is specifically a DURABILITY claim, not a blanket superiority. Li also found lab>classroom and FL>L2 and short>long treatment, so deployed effect sizes will likely be at the low end.
- *Effect:* Li 2010 (Language Learning 60:309-365; 33 studies): CF overall d=0.61 (RE 0.64), durable; explicit>implicit on immediate/short-delay, implicit better maintained on long-delay; lab>classroom, FL>L2. Lyster & Saito 2010 (SSLA 32:265-302; 15 classroom studies, n=827): prompts>recasts, effects largest on free constructed-response measures; younger>older learners.
- *Sources:* Li 2010, Language Learning 60:309-365 (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9922.2010.00561.x); Lyster & Saito 2010, Studies in Second Language Acquisition 32:265-302 (https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/ORAL-FEEDBACK-IN-CLASSROOM-SLA-Lyster-Saito/3b641377dc6657f21f555d964a635480ec854cd7)
- *→ Loop:* Q2: reject both 'immediate over-correction' AND 'recast-only'. Default to DELAYED/SELECTIVE feedback with a PROMPT bias (elicitation/clarification) over silent recast, reserving explicit correct+rule for stabilized or high-frequency errors. Because explicit is better immediately but implicit is better maintained, use explicit correct+rule sparingly for stabilization then let prompts/recasts carry durability. INVARIANT: Repair drive may flag at most one error per learner turn; during chat/engage moves suppress error-flagging to protect fluency/affect.

**🟡 moderate** — Strict L1-avoidance / 'target-language-only' dogma is unsupported; judicious L1 use, contrastive analysis and translation produce strong vocabulary gains. CAVEAT ADDED: Laufer & Girsai 2008 reports its CAT advantage as 'significant' but the abstract does NOT report a Cohen's d, so do not quote a numeric effect size. It is a single small-scale study (3 high-school classes, one L1, FL setting), so 'strong' applies to the within-study result, not to a robust meta-analytic base; the construct is 'replicated' only by a small number of later studies. Treat as moderate evidence for PERMITTING L1, not as a large-effect mandate.
- *Effect:* Laufer & Girsai 2008 (Applied Linguistics 29:694-716): CAT group significantly outperformed both meaning-focused and non-contrastive form-focused groups on all tests, single words and collocations — direction reported as significant, NO published Cohen's d in abstract. Translation-based vocabulary effects replicated in a small number of later studies (Eurasian J. Applied Linguistics replication).
- *Sources:* Laufer & Girsai 2008, Applied Linguistics (https://academic.oup.com/applij/article-abstract/29/4/694/183330); Replication, Eurasian Journal of Applied Linguistics (https://dergipark.org.tr/en/pub/ejal/article/460588)
- *→ Loop:* Q3/Q4: do NOT add an invariant banning L1. PERMIT L1 in explain/introduce/recast realizations: glosses, L1 translation of new items, explicit L1<->L2 contrastive notes for error-prone forms. Capability flag on introduce/explain for L1 glossing and on correct for contrastive 'in your language X, but here Y' rules. Arbiter raises explain/contrastive weight when the error pattern matches a known L1-transfer interference. Because the effect-size base is thin, treat L1 as a PERMITTED tool, not a forced default.

**🟡 moderate** — One-size-fits-all fixed grammar sequencing (a single universal syllabus order) is weakly grounded; instruction effectiveness depends on feature type and learner state, not a canonical sequence. This finding is well-supported and unchanged. Minor caveat: Spada & Tomita 2010 supports that explicit instruction helps BOTH simple and complex features (the simple/complex interaction is about magnitude/pattern, not a clean 'only simple benefits'), so do not over-read it as 'complex forms resist explicit instruction'.
- *Effect:* Spada & Tomita 2010 (Language Learning; 41 studies): explicit instruction effective, effects interact with feature complexity; explicit benefits both simple and complex features. Norris & Ortega 2000: focused instruction broadly effective but cannot endorse one global ordering.
- *Sources:* Spada & Tomita 2010, Language Learning (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9922.2010.00562.x); Norris & Ortega 2000 (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136)
- *→ Loop:* Q5: i+1 selection of the next introduce target driven by the learner model (mastery graph, prerequisite dependencies, feature complexity) — NOT a frozen global syllabus index. INVARIANT (keep): don't introduce while the current item is shaky. 'introduce' candidate ordering computed from per-learner readiness; Progress drive consults feature-complexity (simple forms can be introduced explicitly sooner; complex forms need more input/practice scaffolding first).

**🟠 contested** — Learning-styles / VARK MATCHING (tailoring move modality to a learner's 'preferred style') is not worth adopting. The meshing/crossover interaction that would justify it appears in only a minority of outcomes. CORRECTION: the cited 2024 meta is Clinton-Lisell & Litzinger (NOT 'Lalwani et al.' as the researcher wrote — a misattribution despite claiming to have read the full text). Their aggregate g=0.31 is small but statistically significant (p=0.02, 95% CI [0.05,0.57]) and only 26% of outcomes (11/42 measures) showed the required crossover — so the honest framing is 'too small and too INFREQUENT to warrant adoption' (the researcher wrote 'too frequent', the opposite of the paper). 'Almost never appears' OVERSTATES: 26% is low but not ~zero. Pashler et al. 2008 remains the strong refutation of valid crossover designs; the matching hypothesis is best rated weak/not-actionable rather than literally zero-effect.
- *Effect:* Pashler et al. 2008: ~0 studies with valid crossover design meeting criteria. Clinton-Lisell & Litzinger 2024 (Frontiers in Psychology, PMC11270031; 21 studies, 101 ES, n=1712): aggregate g=0.31, p=0.02, CI[0.05,0.57]; crossover in 26% of outcomes (11/42); authors: 'too small and too infrequent to warrant widespread adoption', recommend multimodal-for-all.
- *Sources:* Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer & Bjork 2008, Psychological Science in the Public Interest (https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1539-6053.2009.01038.x); Clinton-Lisell & Litzinger 2024, Frontiers in Psychology, 'Is it really a neuromyth? A meta-analysis of the learning styles matching hypothesis', PMC11270031 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11270031/)
- *→ Loop:* Add hard INVARIANT: the learner model must NOT carry a 'learning style' field, and no drive/move may branch on one. Q6 (modality): pick modality from the linguistic TARGET (phonology->audio, orthography->text) and from comprehension signals, never from an inferred style. Default to MULTIMODAL realization of input/introduce moves (text + audio together) for all learners — the evidence-backed alternative, and the same move the meta-analysts endorse over matching.

**🟠 contested** — 'Comprehensible input ALONE is sufficient' (pure Krashen i+1, no required output, no form focus) is not supported. Input is necessary but not sufficient; interaction, output and form-focus add measurable gains. CAVEAT ADDED: the supporting interaction meta (Mackey & Goo) BUNDLES output, negotiation AND feedback into one 'interaction' construct, so it does not cleanly isolate a pure 'output' effect — it shows that input-only (no interaction) underperforms interaction, not that output specifically is the active ingredient. Mackey & Goo's sample is also lab-heavy. The Norris & Ortega 'explicit>implicit' margin carries a known measurement-bias critique (their outcome measures lean toward explicit/discrete-point tests that favor explicit instruction), so 'explicit instruction is effective' is supported but 'explicit>implicit' should be treated as contested, not settled.
- *Effect:* Mackey & Goo 2007 (28 interaction studies): large effects for interaction vs none, more pronounced on delayed posttests; CF within interaction ~d=0.71 immediate, ~1.09 delayed (construct conflates output+feedback). Norris & Ortega 2000 (49 studies): focused instruction yields large gains, explicit>implicit ON THEIR MEASURES. Krashen's strong sufficiency claim is widely judged untestable.
- *Sources:* Mackey & Goo 2007, interaction meta-analysis (in Conversational Interaction in SLA); Norris & Ortega 2000, Language Learning (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136); Beyond comprehensible input critique 2025, PMC12577063 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12577063/)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT let the 'input' move dominate. Keep elicit (output), recast/correct/prompt (feedback) and explain (form focus) as first-class moves with real drive weight. INVARIANT: across any window of N turns the arbiter must fire at least one production-eliciting move (elicit/probe) and surface form-focus when the learner model shows a stabilized error. Because the evidence isolates 'interaction' rather than 'output alone', prioritize moves that combine production WITH feedback (elicit->prompt/recast) over bare output drills.

**🟠 contested** — The 'silent period' (let learners stay receptive and delay production) is overclaimed and weakly evidenced; using it to justify withholding elicitation is an anti-pattern. CAVEAT ADDED: the silent-period evidence base is almost entirely CHILD L2 (and largely qualitative, with inconsistent definitions); generalizing it to ADULT learners — the harness's likely population — is even weaker. So the residual 'short perception window' justification is itself shaky for adults and should be minimal.
- *Effect:* Roberts/Bligh & Drury narrative+scoping reviews (~12 studies): 'limited evidence' for a silent period in child L2; methodologically weak, qualitative, definitionally inconsistent. No meta-analytic support for a productive benefit of enforced silence; output/interaction research shows production aids acquisition.
- *Sources:* Roberts/Bligh & Drury, Re-assessing the silent period (https://arrow.tudublin.ie/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1009&context=cassart); 'Not so silent after all' 2013, ScienceDirect (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0885200613000720)
- *→ Loop:* Do NOT hard-code an early no-elicit phase. INVARIANT: the harness must offer low-stakes production (elicit/probe) early and must not block elicitation for extended periods. A short perception/listening window at first exposure to a brand-new item is the only defensible residue, and for ADULT learners even that should be brief. Production-readiness gated by per-item learner-model confidence, not by a session-level 'silent' phase.

**🟠 contested** — Sub-lexical phonics drilling as a PRIMARY method for ADULT L2 learners is not evidence-backed; the strong phonics evidence base is for CHILDREN's L1/early reading, and even that is contested as overstated (Bowers 2020). Adult/L2 evidence is thin. Finding is sound; framing kept as contested because the literature is sparse, not refuted.
- *Effect:* National Reading Panel & reviews: systematic phonics effective for young children's reading. Bowers 2020 (Educ. Psych. Review): 'phonics more effective than alternatives' claim overstated. L2/adult evidence thin — a few EFL studies show medium effects; adult/adolescent L2 'largely underexplored'.
- *Sources:* Bowers 2020, Educ. Psych. Review (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09515-y); Phonics pillar & SLA 2022, Language Learning Journal (https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09571736.2022.2045683)
- *→ Loop:* Anti-pattern: do NOT make sub-lexical phonics drills a default drill/introduce mode for adults. Grapheme-phoneme mapping moves offered ONLY when (a) the target language has an unfamiliar/opaque script for this learner AND (b) the learner model shows decoding errors — gated by an explicit need signal, not routine drill. Keep the primary loop lexical and meaning-focused; phonics is a conditional sub-routine, not a spine.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Learning-styles / VARK matching (tailor modality to a learner's preferred style) — Classic neuromyth. Pashler et al. 2008 found essentially no studies with the valid crossover design required to support it; even the most FAVORABLE 2024 meta (Clinton-Lisell & Litzinger) yields only g=0.31 with crossover in just 26% of outcomes and explicitly declines to recommend adoption. Folk wisdom, not evidence.
- Comprehensible input ALONE is sufficient (strong-form Krashen i+1, no required output or form focus) — The strong sufficiency claim is widely judged untestable/unfalsifiable. Interaction, output and form-focused instruction add measurable gains (Norris & Ortega; Mackey & Goo). Input is necessary but not sufficient.
- Massed practice / cramming cements learning — Directly contradicted by the spacing literature (Kim & Webb 2022, the strongest finding here): spaced beats massed and the gap WIDENS on delayed tests. Back-to-back repetition feels effective in-session (fluency illusion) but is inferior for retention.
- Immediate exhaustive correction of every error helps; or its mirror, 'recasts (non-flagging) are the best feedback' — Over-correction taxes affect/fluency with no retention payoff, and recasts are NOT clearly superior to prompts — Lyster & Saito 2010 found prompts>recasts, especially on free-response measures. Both extremes are folk defaults that the evidence rejects.
- The 'silent period' — withhold production / elicitation while learners stay receptive — Weakly evidenced, child-L2 and largely qualitative with inconsistent definitions; no meta-analytic support for a benefit of enforced silence, and output research shows production AIDS acquisition. Using it to block elicitation (especially for adults) is unsupported.
- Target-language-only / strict L1 avoidance — Dogma, not evidence. Judicious L1 use, contrastive analysis and translation produce significant vocabulary gains (Laufer & Girsai 2008). Banning L1 is a tradition the data do not back.
- A single universal/canonical grammar sequencing order — No meta-analysis endorses one global syllabus ordering; effectiveness interacts with feature complexity and learner state (Spada & Tomita 2010; Norris & Ortega 2000). Fixed-order syllabi are convention, not evidence.
- Systematic sub-lexical phonics drilling as a primary method for adult L2 learners — The strong phonics base is children's L1/early reading (and even that is argued overstated, Bowers 2020). Adult/L2 evidence is thin and underexplored, so running phonics drills as a routine spine for adults is an unsupported transfer from a different population.

## Intelligent tutoring systems & adaptive sequencing
**🟢 strong** — Bloom's '2 sigma' does NOT replicate; the honest ceiling for tutoring/ITS is ~0.4-0.8 SD, and AT SCALE often ~0.2. The 2-sigma framing is folk wisdom from artificial replications.
- *Effect:* Original Bloom/Anania ~2.0 SD never replicated. VanLehn 2011 human tutoring d≈0.79; mastery ~0.5; at-scale deployed ITS ~0.2 (Pane 2014). Real-world ed interventions typically d=0.1-0.5.
- *Sources:* Bloom 1984, Educational Researcher; VanLehn 2011; Pane et al. 2014; Nintil 'On Bloom's two sigma problem'
- *→ Loop:* Do not design or market against 2-sigma; calibrate to d≈0.2-0.6 in realistic use. Keep the arbiter simple and auditable; do not over-invest in baroque personalization chasing mythical gains — a competent step-based loop plus high retention captures most of the realistically available effect.

**🟢 strong** — Fixed mastery thresholds cause over-practice; Review/Consolidate should schedule by forgetting/spacing models, not fixed repetition counts ('drill until perfect' is folk wisdom). The anti-over-practice invariant is sound; and the spacing leg is one of the STRONGEST, most replicable effects in the whole dimension — arguably stronger than the ITS step-granularity leg.
- *Effect:* Spacing effect: Cepeda et al. 2006 meta-analysis (839 assessments, 317 experiments) — large, robust distributed-practice benefit; optimal inter-study interval grows with retention interval. Over-practice pruning at equal mastery from KT fast-forwarding work. (Efficiency + retention, not just gain.)
- *Sources:* Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer 2006, Psychological Bulletin; Lindsey, Shroyer, Pashler & Mozer 2014 (personalized spacing); Choffin et al. 2019 DAS3H; Cen, Koedinger & Junker (over-practice)
- *→ Loop:* Add invariant 'do not drill/review an item whose P(known) is already above threshold' (stop over-practice). Review drive weights by a forgetting/spacing model (estimated current retrievability), not fixed turn-count. Consolidate triggers spaced resurfacing at EXPANDING intervals tied to per-item recall probability (Cepeda: lag should scale with desired retention horizon). Define 'overdue' via predicted retrievability dropping below a floor, not a static N. Given spacing is among the strongest effects here, weight Review/Consolidate confidently — more confidently than the step-granularity moves.

**🟡 moderate** — Step-based ITS (feedback at each solution step) reaches effect sizes near human tutoring and above answer-based systems — BUT the gap is built on a thin, partly self-contradictory evidence base and does NOT survive at-scale deployment. VanLehn 2011's step d=0.76 vs answer d=0.31 rests on only ~10 comparisons drawn from 28 studies (1975-2010), and the substep tier coming in LOWER (d=0.40) than step DIRECTLY CONTRADICTS VanLehn's own 'interaction plateau' prediction (human=substep=step>answer), which signals confounds (study quality, domain, era) rather than a clean granularity dose-response. Critically, the one large randomized at-scale trial of a step-based deployed tutor (Pane et al. 2014, RAND, Cognitive Tutor Algebra I) found NULL effect in year 1 and only ~0.2 SD in year 2 — a quarter of the meta-analytic d. So 'step granularity dominates' is a lab/quasi-experimental result that shrinks sharply in real classrooms, and almost none of it is from natural-language learning. The directional point (act on the smallest meaningful production unit, give form-specific feedback) is still the best available guide, but it is a moderate-strength heuristic, not the settled dominant lever.
- *Effect:* VanLehn 2011: step d=0.76, substep d=0.40 (contradicts plateau theory), answer d=0.31, human d=0.79 — but from ~10 comparisons/28 studies. At-scale RCT downgrade: Pane 2014 CTAI null year 1, ~0.20 SD year 2. Ma 2014 g≈0.44, Kulik & Fletcher 2016 median d≈0.66 (mostly quasi-experimental, STEM).
- *Sources:* VanLehn 2011, Educational Psychologist 46(4):197-221; Pane, Griffin, McCaffrey & Karam 2014, Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis 36(2):127-144 (RAND RCT at scale); Ma et al. 2014, J. Educational Psychology; Kulik & Fletcher 2016, Review of Educational Research
- *→ Loop:* Still operate at 'step' granularity: elicit/recast/correct should act on the smallest meaningful production unit (clause, morpheme, word-choice) and deliver form-specific feedback, not holistic 'good job'. Add invariant 'every elicit is followed by step-level feedback on the specific form'. BUT do not treat this as a guaranteed large effect — the at-scale shrinkage means motivation/retention/dosage (does the learner come back, how many turns) likely matter as much as granularity. Do not let 'chat' be dismissed as worthless: it is the dosage/engagement driver, and engagement is what the at-scale trials suggest is the binding constraint.

**🟡 moderate** — Mastery gating works but the effect is modest, shows up mainly on aligned/criterion tests not standardized ones, helps weaker learners most, and CAN HURT completion/motivation. Supports a soft mastery-gate invariant; warns against treating it as a large independent driver.
- *Effect:* Kulik, Kulik & Bangert-Drowns 1990 (n=108): ~0.4 SD criterion-referenced, ~0.1 SD standardized; larger for weaker students; self-paced mastery can reduce completion. Consistent with Pane 2014 weak at-scale transfer.
- *Sources:* Kulik, Kulik & Bangert-Drowns 1990, Review of Educational Research 60(2):265-299; Pane et al. 2014
- *→ Loop:* Adopt mastery gating as a SOFT invariant: 'do not introduce a new item while the current item is below a probabilistic mastery threshold'. Let Progress and Engage drives override pure gating to avoid stall-out and protect completion (the under-measured failure mode). Gate on a probabilistic threshold, not perfection. Weight gating MORE for struggling learners (where its effect is largest) via the learner model.

**🟡 moderate** — BKT is a reasonable interpretable substrate for the learner model, but its 'validated, deployed-at-scale, auditable' framing is OVERSTATED. BKT suffers documented identifiability and semantic-degeneracy problems (Beck & Chang 2007): different parameter sets fit identically, and best-fit parameters can be nonsensical (e.g. implying you're MORE likely to answer correctly when you DON'T know the skill). 'Interpretable' does not mean 'correctly identified'. And the Cognitive Tutor deployment it leans on showed only ~0.2 SD at scale, so BKT-driven mastery is not a proven large-effect mechanism — it is an auditable bookkeeping substrate, not a guarantor of learning gains. For language, it fits morphology/agreement/vocabulary (decomposable skills) and poorly fits pragmatics/fluency/discourse.
- *Effect:* n/a (substrate). Caveat: BKT identifiability/degeneracy (Beck & Chang 2007); 4 params (p-init,p-learn,p-guess,p-slip); Cognitive Tutor mastery at P(known)>=0.95. Deployment effect only ~0.2 SD at scale (Pane 2014).
- *Sources:* Corbett & Anderson 1994, UMUAI; Beck & Chang 2007 (identifiability/degeneracy); van de Sande 2013, JEDM 'Properties of the BKT Model'; Pane et al. 2014; Koedinger et al. 2011, EDM
- *→ Loop:* Use per-item BKT/PFA as the auditable bookkeeping substrate feeding drives (Probe updates p-known; Review weights by (threshold − P(known)) and time-since-seen; Progress vetoed while prerequisites below threshold). MUST guard against degeneracy: bound parameters (e.g. p-guess<0.5, p-slip<0.5) and prefer hierarchical/empirical-prior fitting so per-item params stay semantically valid — otherwise the 'auditable invariant boundary' encodes nonsense. Treat the mastery threshold as an auditable boundary for FORM items only; do not pretend BKT meaningfully models conversational competence.

**🟡 moderate** — DKT gives better raw predictive accuracy but is less interpretable and unstable; well-tuned hierarchical/feature-enriched BKT/PFA largely closes the gap. For an auditable harness, DKT's opacity is a liability and it should not gate hard vetoes. This is correctly characterized.
- *Effect:* DKT reported ~25% AUC gain on benchmarks; Khajah, Lindsey & Mozer 2016 show most gain recovered by adding forgetting + student-ability + skill-discovery to BKT; DKT criticized for inconsistent predictions across near-identical states.
- *Sources:* Piech et al. 2015, NeurIPS; Khajah, Lindsey & Mozer 2016, EDM 'How deep is knowledge tracing?'; Ding & Larson 2019 'Why DKT has less depth than anticipated'
- *→ Loop:* Default to interpretable BKT/PFA (ideally BKT extended with a forgetting term, which doubles as the spacing signal) so the invariant layer is auditable and reproducible. Do NOT use DKT as authority for a hard veto. If extra predictive accuracy is wanted, run it as a shadow soft signal for Review priority only.

**🟡 moderate** — VanLehn's inner-loop/outer-loop distinction supports the two-layer proposal and locates the evidence in the INNER loop (step feedback). Correctly characterized, with the same caveat as finding 1: the step-vs-answer gap that defines the inner loop's value is the same thin, scale-fragile evidence base.
- *Effect:* Definitional + the d=0.76 (step) vs d=0.31 (answer) gap = value of inner loop, from VanLehn 2011's limited comparison set; shrinks at scale (Pane 2014).
- *Sources:* VanLehn 2006, IJAIED 16(3); VanLehn 2011; Pane et al. 2014
- *→ Loop:* Keep two layers with light arc biasing and the per-turn arbiter doing the real work at step granularity. Ensure recast/correct/elicit deliver genuine STEP feedback about the specific form just produced. Add invariant 'Repair is never vetoed by arc phase'. Recognize the inner-loop effect is moderate and scale-fragile, so the OUTER loop's real job may be protecting dosage/retention (getting the learner back), which the at-scale data suggest is the binding constraint.

**🟠 contested** — Feedback-timing dogma (immediate always best) is NOT supported; timing effects are small, heterogeneous, and moderated by task complexity and learner level. Bears on Q2. Correctly characterized.
- *Effect:* Kulik & Kulik 1988 small immediate advantage; Shute 2008 immediate for lower-order, delayed for higher-order; recent meta-analyses find no significant average timing effect, high heterogeneity.
- *Sources:* Kulik & Kulik 1988; Shute 2008, Review of Educational Research; Corral et al. 2021
- *→ Loop:* Reject blanket immediate AND blanket delayed/recast-only for Q2. Make timing a soft function of learner model + item type: low-level form errors and struggling learners -> immediate recast/correct; higher-order/discourse errors and advanced learners -> delayed batched review. Encode as soft arbiter rule, not hard invariant, given heterogeneity.

**🟠 contested** — Krashen's i+1 / comprehensible input is partially supported as a heuristic but operationally untestable; treat as soft target not measurable invariant. CORRECTION TO THE RESEARCHER'S OWN CAVEAT: the surrounding claim that the output/interaction/feedback side of SLA is 'thin and contested' is itself overstated — corrective feedback has solid meta-analytic support (Li 2010, d≈0.64 overall), so the harness's elicit/recast/correct moves are BETTER grounded than passive input.
- *Effect:* i+1: n/a, untestable construct; immersion CI evidence real but confounded with interaction/output. Contrast: corrective feedback meta-analysis Li 2010 d=0.64 (recasts d≈0.70, explicit d≈0.81), implicit/recast retained better over time.
- *Sources:* Krashen 1985 Input Hypothesis; Li 2010, Language Learning 60:309-365 (corrective feedback meta-analysis); Frontiers in Psychology 2025 neuro-ecological critique
- *→ Loop:* Operationalize 'i+1' via BKT/PFA: difficulty frontier = items where P(known) is moderate (0.4-0.85) and prerequisites mastered; input move = text built mostly from mastered items plus 1-2 frontier items (concrete auditable invariant). Because the OUTPUT/feedback evidence (Li 2010) is actually the STRONGER leg, do NOT let input dominate: the arbiter should fire elicit (output) and recast/correct (interactional feedback) frequently. This raises the relative priority of elicit/recast vs input in the drive weights.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Bloom's 2-sigma: one-to-one tutoring yields +2.0 SD, so an ITS should target near that. — Never replicated outside Bloom's artificial grad-student studies; honest tutoring/ITS ceiling is ~0.4-0.8 SD in controlled studies and ~0.2 SD for deployed step-based tutors at scale (Pane 2014). Designing or marketing to 2-sigma is folk wisdom.
- 'Drill until perfect' / more repetition is always better; fixed repetition counts or fixed mastery thresholds. — Fixed thresholds cause measurable over-practice with no added gain; spacing/forgetting models (Cepeda 2006; Lindsey 2014) show distributed, retrievability-timed practice is superior. Repetition past P(known) above threshold wastes learner time and harms motivation/completion.
- Immediate correction is always best (blanket immediate-feedback policy). — Recent meta-analyses find no significant average timing effect with high heterogeneity; immediate helps lower-order skills/weaker learners while delayed can help higher-order tasks/stronger learners (Shute 2008). The dogma is unsupported as a universal rule.
- Krashen's i+1 / comprehensible-input is a measurable design target (provide input exactly one step above current level). — 'i' and 'i+1' are operationally undefinable and untestable; the acquisition-vs-learning split is unproven; and the input-only emphasis ignores the stronger output/interaction/corrective-feedback evidence (Li 2010, d=0.64). Usable only as a soft heuristic, re-operationalized via the learner model.
- BKT is 'interpretable' therefore its mastery estimates are trustworthy/auditable as-is. — Interpretability does not imply identifiability. BKT exhibits identifiability and semantic-degeneracy problems (Beck & Chang 2007): multiple parameter sets fit identically and best-fit params can be nonsensical. Unconstrained BKT can produce an auditable-looking but semantically invalid mastery boundary.
- Step-based granularity is THE dominant lever and outweighs everything else (including engagement/dosage). — The step>answer gap comes from a small, era-spanning, confound-prone comparison set and contradicts VanLehn's own substep prediction; at scale the effect collapses to ~0.2 SD while completion/retention emerges as the binding constraint (Pane 2014; Duolingo attrition data). Granularity is a moderate lever, not a dominant one.
- Language apps deliver 'N university semesters' of proficiency (treated as settled efficacy). — The headline Duolingo efficacy figures are vendor-run, on self-selected completers with ~30% attrition over 3 months, and lean on reading/listening (not speaking/pragmatics). This is engagement/selection-confounded efficacy, not a clean causal proficiency gain for the typical user.

## Cognitive load, scaffolding & sequencing of instruction
**🟢 strong** — The worked-example effect is real but moderate and bounded. Barbieri, Miller-Cotto, Clerjuste & Chawla (2023) meta-analysis (43 articles, 55 studies, 181 effect sizes, math, K-postsecondary) found g=0.48 (p=0.01) via robust variance estimation; Crissman (2006) found d=0.52. VERIFIED via independent search. KEY BOUNDARY CONDITIONS that constrain use: (a) the effect concentrates in retention/near-transfer and is weak/absent for FAR transfer; (b) it is a NOVICE effect that reverses with expertise (see expertise-reversal finding); (c) the entire evidence base is MATH/STEM problem-solving, NOT natural-language acquisition - there is essentially no direct meta-analytic evidence that worked-example structuring of grammar/vocabulary moves improves L2 acquisition, so applying it to the language harness is a mechanism-based extrapolation, not a measured effect. (d) Worked examples teach explicit, declarative procedure; whether that transfers to fluent/implicit language use is unestablished (instructed-SLA reviews note the explicit->implicit link is theoretically defensible but not empirically demonstrated).
- *Effect:* g=0.48 (Barbieri 2023, math); d=0.52 (Crissman 2006) - near-transfer only; no L2-specific effect size exists
- *Sources:* Barbieri, Miller-Cotto, Clerjuste & Chawla (2023), Educational Psychology Review, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-023-09745-1; Crissman (2006), cited in NSW CESE Cognitive Load Theory review, https://education.nsw.gov.au/content/dam/main-education/about-us/educational-data/cese/2017-cognitive-load-theory.pdf; Ellis, Instructed Second Language Acquisition (literature review), https://www-leland.stanford.edu/~hakuta/Courses/Ed388%20Website/Resources/Instructional%20Resources/Ellis%20Instructed-second-language%20-%20latest%20version.pdf
- *→ Loop:* For a NEW or shaky item, the arbiter must NOT jump straight to elicit (unguided production = high extraneous load for novices). Add invariant: 'introduce' and 'input'/'recast' (worked-model moves) must precede 'elicit' for an item below a mastery threshold. Make 'elicit'/'drill' difficulty a function of the learner-model mastery score; items below threshold bias toward modeling moves. CAVEAT for the design doc: this is a transferred STEM mechanism, so treat the worked-model-before-production invariant as a strong prior, not a proven L2 law; instrument it so the harness can A/B it against earlier production for items where output-first might help (since output/interaction is independently necessary for L2).

**🟢 strong** — The expertise-reversal effect is well-established as a disordinal (crossover) aptitude-treatment interaction: support that helps novices (worked examples, explicit explanation, redundant guidance) becomes neutral or HARMFUL for more knowledgeable learners (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller 2003; Kalyuga 2007). UPDATE / STRENGTHENED: a 2025 meta-analysis (ScienceDirect, S0959475225000660; 176 effect sizes, 60 experimental studies, 5924 participants) now provides pooled evidence - so the earlier note that no meta-analytic estimate existed is superseded. IMPORTANT NUANCE from that meta-analysis: the reversal REPLICATED for TRANSFER outcomes but NOT for RETENTION. So expertise-driven fading of support is best justified where the goal is transfer/free use; for pure retention of an item, pulling support early is less clearly warranted.
- *Effect:* pooled meta-analysis exists (2025; 176 ES / 60 studies / 5924 participants); reversal confirmed for transfer, NOT retention; reported as an interaction, not a single pooled d
- *Sources:* Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller (2003), 'The Expertise Reversal Effect', Educational Psychologist, https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1207/S15326985EP3801_4; Kalyuga (2007), Educational Psychology Review, https://www.uky.edu/~gmswan3/EDC608/Kalyuga2007_Article_ExpertiseReversalEffectAndItsI.pdf; 'A cornerstone of adaptivity - A meta-analysis of the expertise reversal effect' (2025), Learning and Instruction, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959475225000660
- *→ Loop:* This is the single strongest justification that move difficulty MUST track mastery and support must FADE - but fade for the RIGHT reason (redundancy for THIS learner) and primarily to serve transfer/free use. Add invariant: once an item's mastery exceeds a high threshold, suppress high-support moves (explain, explicit correct+rule, worked input) and bias toward low-support moves (chat, free elicit, review). Down-weight 'explain'/'correct' as item/learner mastery rises (explicit meta-instruction is a novice tool). Make this learner-global AND item-local. Because the reversal is weaker for retention, do NOT aggressively strip support from items the learner is still trying to RETAIN (low recent-recall) even if global mastery is high.

**🟢 strong** — Working memory is limited to ~4 chunks when rehearsal/chunking is blocked (Cowan 2001), revising Miller's 7+/-2; a 'chunk' is whatever long-term memory has compressed into one unit. CLT's core claim: difficulty scales with element interactivity (number of simultaneously-interacting novel elements), and exceeding WM capacity blocks schema formation. CAVEAT (confirmed): the exact number is contested - agreement on '4' is not general, estimates depend on what is counted, and the limit is foundational theory, not a clean experimental effect size. Treat '~4' as an order-of-magnitude design heuristic (small, single-digit), not a precise parameter. For language, an unanalyzed multi-word phrase is one chunk; isolated grammar elements are many.
- *Effect:* WM ~4 chunks (Cowan 2001, contested exact value); CLT element-interactivity is a theoretical principle, not a pooled d
- *Sources:* Cowan (2001/2010), 'The Magical Mystery Four', https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2864034/; Modelling Working Memory Capacity (Journal of Cognition), https://journalofcognition.org/articles/10.5334/joc.387; NSW CESE (2017) CLT review, https://education.nsw.gov.au/content/dam/main-education/about-us/educational-data/cese/2017-cognitive-load-theory.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Add a load-aware cap on 'introduce': at most 1 genuinely-novel high-interactivity item per turn, plus invariant 'don't introduce while current item is shaky' (well-justified by element interactivity). Favor teaching multi-word lexical chunks/formulaic sequences as single units early (enter as one chunk, low interactivity); defer analytic grammar 'explain' until the learner has chunked exemplars. The arbiter should track an approximate 'live element' count and refuse moves that stack multiple novel elements on a shaky item. Do not over-engineer to a literal '4'; the actionable content is 'keep simultaneous novel elements very small.'

**🟡 moderate** — Worked-example-to-problem FADING (completion problems: progressively removing steps so the learner fills in more) outperforms abrupt example->problem pairs on near transfer, especially with self-explanation prompts. Evidence suggests it is the TYPE of step faded, not forward-vs-backward order, that determines what is learned (Renkl & Atkinson 2003; Atkinson, Renkl & Merrill 2003). CAVEATS: this is a moderate/small body of mostly lab studies in math/physics; the forward-vs-backward nuance was not independently verified beyond abstracts; and self-explanation's contribution is confounded with fading in several designs (you cannot cleanly attribute the gain to fading alone). Note tension with the scaffolding-fading null below: graded support transitions help in tightly-controlled example-study paradigms, but a generic 'fading helps' claim does NOT hold in the broader scaffolding meta-analysis.
- *Effect:* n/a (consistent direction on near-transfer in small lab studies; smaller/absent on far-transfer; self-explanation confound)
- *Sources:* Renkl & Atkinson (2003), 'Structuring the Transition From Example Study to Problem Solving', https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/B:TRUC.0000021815.74806.f6; Atkinson, Renkl & Merrill (2003), 'Transitioning From Studying Examples to Solving Problems', https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/5057f7decd1e13fc2ab90cf65ca6cc1f79026a6a
- *→ Loop:* Model the example->completion->production transition as a per-item state, not a binary introduced/not-introduced flag. Add a graded 'support level' field (modeled -> cued/partial -> free). Advance support on success, drop on failure (Repair drive). 'recast' is the completion-stage move; 'elicit' is the free-production endpoint. Pair production with a self-explanation/why prompt where feasible. CRUCIAL: make support-level transitions MASTERY-contingent (driven by learner-model evidence), NOT a fixed schedule - the clean transition results come from study paradigms, while schedule-based fading has a null in field scaffolding (Belland 2017).

**🟡 moderate** — Simple-to-complex sequencing helps ONLY insofar as it manages element interactivity; the strong version (always isolate and pre-teach parts before wholes) is not universally supported and can backfire when artificial isolation destroys meaningful context that aids chunking. CLT's own isolated-interacting-elements refinement says part-task isolation helps high-interactivity material for novices but should give way to whole-task practice as expertise grows - mastery-contingent, not fixed. This is theoretical refinement plus indirect evidence, not a direct meta-analysis, so evidence_strength is moderate.
- *Sources:* Kalyuga (2007), expertise reversal and learner-tailored sequencing, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-007-9054-3; NSW CESE (2017) CLT review (isolated/interacting elements), https://education.nsw.gov.au/content/dam/main-education/about-us/educational-data/cese/2017-cognitive-load-theory.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Do not hard-code a fixed simple->complex curriculum spine as the primary controller (argues against pure phase-machine / rigid curriculum for Q1/Q3). Make sequencing emergent from per-item interactivity + mastery: introduce lower-interactivity items first, unlock higher-interactivity ones as prerequisites reach mastery (prerequisite-DAG gate as an invariant: 'never introduce item X before its prerequisite chunks are at mastery m'). Keep the Progress drive subordinate to load: down-weight Progress whenever the current item is shaky.

**🟡 moderate** — Mayer's multimedia design principles replicate, but with an important provenance caveat. The 2025 meta-analysis (Cromley & Chen, Educational Research Review; overall g=0.37, ~181 studies/591 effects) is restricted to a MAYER-AUTHORED corpus (92 articles with Mayer as an author, 1990-2022) - it is a meta-analysis OF ONE LAB'S program, not an independent field-wide synthesis, which limits generalization and is a non-trivial conflict-of-provenance even though the authors checked authorship-order moderation and argued against sample-size-driven bias. Effect sizes vary by principle: coherence/seductive-detail removal (d~0.70-1.00), modality (d~0.72-0.82), redundancy (d~0.87), spatial contiguity (d~0.79), segmenting (d~0.70), signaling (d~0.46), personalization (g~0.70). CRITICAL: modality and redundancy benefits THEMSELVES reverse with expertise, and the meta-analysis reports a small DECLINE in effect size per year (possible early-study inflation / publication-era effects). Most effects are short-duration lab studies measuring immediate post-test learning, not durable acquisition.
- *Effect:* overall g=0.37 (Mayer-authored corpus); redundancy d~0.87; modality d~0.72-0.82; coherence d~0.70-1.00; signaling d~0.46; personalization g~0.70 - effects decline over publication year
- *Sources:* Cromley & Chen (2025), 'A Meta-Analysis of Richard Mayer's Multimedia Learning Research', Educational Research Review, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1747938X25000673; Noetel et al. (2022), 'Multimedia Design for Learning: An Overview of Reviews With Meta-Meta-Analysis', Review of Educational Research, https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/00346543211052329; Ginns (2005) modality meta-analysis; Mayer (2017) e-learning overview, https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jcal.12197
- *→ Loop:* Relevant to Q6 (modality). If multimodal: apply modality (audio narration + minimal redundant on-screen text) and redundancy (no verbatim synced transcript for novices). Apply coherence/personalization to ALL move realizations even text-only: keep move text lean (no seductive tangents), conversational, signal the target +1 item. GATE by expertise: drop redundant scaffolds (transcripts, glosses, signaling) for advanced learners (expertise reversal). Treat the specific d-values as optimistic upper bounds given the single-lab corpus and per-year decline; prefer the independent Noetel et al. meta-meta-analysis for sanity-checking magnitudes. Multimodal redundancy is a fade-able, mastery-contingent setting, not a fixed default.

**🟠 contested** — Scaffolding has a solid positive effect (computer-based scaffolding in STEM: Hedges g=0.46, 144 studies/333 outcomes, Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler 2017). VERIFIED. CRITICAL COUNTER-EVIDENCE (confirmed independently): the same meta-analysis found FADING scaffolding produced NO statistically significant advantage over adding, fading+adding, or never changing scaffolding - directly contradicting the near-universal claim that fading is essential. Only ~16.5% of outcomes implemented fading, and most fading studies were intelligent-tutoring-system contexts (limited generalization). A pilot meta-analysis by the same group even found NON-fading studies had HIGHER effect sizes. This is the strongest single piece of evidence AGAINST a hard, schedule/turn-based fade invariant.
- *Effect:* scaffolding overall g=0.46; fading-vs-no-fading: non-significant (Belland et al. 2017)
- *Sources:* Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017), Review of Educational Research, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5347356/; Meta-analysis of scaffolding effects in online learning (2020), https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1267049.pdf
- *→ Loop:* Caution flag on any scaffolding-fade INVARIANT. Keep scaffolding (it helps), but do NOT add a hard, time/turn-based force-fade invariant - it has no empirical support and risks pulling support from a still-struggling learner. RECONCILE with expertise-reversal: justify fading as mastery-CONTINGENT redundancy (fade because support became redundant for THIS learner, evidenced by the learner model crossing a threshold), implemented per-item/per-learner, never as arc-phase or turn-count. Net rule for Q4/arc design: arc phases may gently bias drive weights but must not trigger support removal on a schedule.

**⚪ weak** — Comprehensible-input / i+1 has empirical backing only in its operationalized form (lexical coverage), NOT Krashen's original vague formulation. Commonly cited thresholds: ~98% known words (reading), ~95% (listening/audiovisual) (Hu & Nation 2000; Schmitt et al. 2011; van Zeeland & Schmitt 2013). SIGNIFICANT DOWNGRADE based on verification: the foundational Hu & Nation (2000) threshold rests on a regression with only 66 NZ university students, and the 2023 replication by Kremmel et al. (Language Learning) did NOT reproduce it - in the 98% condition participants did not reach adequate comprehension, and Larson (2016) argued the coverage-comprehension relationship may be SPURIOUS. These are CORRELATIONAL comprehension thresholds, not causal learning-gain effects: even if true, '95-98% coverage predicts comprehension' does not establish that texts at that coverage maximize ACQUISITION. Krashen's i+1 itself is widely criticized as unfalsifiable; input is necessary-but-not-sufficient (output/interaction also required).
- *Effect:* thresholds ~98% (reading) / ~95% (listening) are contested, did not fully replicate (Kremmel 2023), correlational not causal
- *Sources:* Kremmel et al. (2023), 'Unknown Vocabulary Density and Reading Comprehension: Replicating Hu and Nation (2000)', Language Learning, https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/lang.12622; Hu & Nation (2000) / Schmitt et al. (2011), https://scholarspace.manoa.hawaii.edu/bitstreams/be187723-ba8b-433c-9472-b3b4ac847b86/download; van Zeeland & Schmitt (2013) listening coverage; 'Beyond comprehensible input' critique of Krashen (2025), https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1636777/full
- *→ Loop:* Operationalize i+1 (Q5) as a measurable coverage constraint - this is still the best AVAILABLE handle on input difficulty and far better than Krashen-vibes - but treat the specific 95/98% numbers as TUNABLE DEFAULTS, not invariants, since they did not cleanly replicate. Add a soft constraint for 'input'/'chat': keep most tokens within the known-item set with the unknown '+1' = target/overdue items; expose the coverage target as a parameter the harness can calibrate per-learner from observed comprehension success, rather than hard-coding 98%. Because input is necessary-but-not-sufficient, keep 'elicit'/output drives alive even when input is going well - never let an input-only loop dominate.

**Myths / unsupported flagged here:**
- Backward fading (removing the last solution step first) is the recommended way to fade worked examples. — This is a frequently-repeated instructional-design heuristic, but the cited primary sources (Renkl & Atkinson 2003; Atkinson et al. 2003) indicate it is the TYPE of step faded, not the forward-vs-backward order, that determines learning. The backward-fading prescription is folk wisdom that outran the evidence; the order claim was not independently verifiable beyond abstracts and should not be encoded as a rule.
- Fading scaffolding is essential / scaffolding must be faded on a schedule for learning to occur. — This near-universal theoretical claim is directly contradicted by the Belland et al. (2017) meta-analysis (g=0.46 for scaffolding overall), which found NO significant advantage of fading over adding / fading+adding / never changing scaffolding - and a pilot found non-fading studies had higher effect sizes. Any schedule/turn-count-based force-fade rule is unsupported folk wisdom; fading is only justified as mastery-contingent redundancy removal.
- The working-memory limit is a precise '7±2' (Miller) or exactly '4' (Cowan) items that can be hard-coded as a design constant. — Miller's 7±2 was explicitly a rhetorical estimate, not a capacity law; Cowan's '4' is contested and depends on what is counted. Treating either as an exact, hard-coded parameter is folk wisdom. The defensible content is only the qualitative principle: keep simultaneous novel interacting elements very small.
- Krashen's i+1 / comprehensible input is a validated mechanism for sequencing input. — Krashen's i+1 in its original form is widely criticized as unfalsifiable, and input-alone is necessary-but-not-sufficient (output/interaction independently required). Only its operationalized lexical-coverage proxy has any measurable basis - and even that (Hu & Nation 98%) failed to fully replicate (Kremmel 2023) and is correlational, not causal. Citing 'i+1' as settled pedagogy is folk wisdom.
- Always teach simple/isolated parts before wholes (strong simple-to-complex / part-task-first sequencing). — Not universally supported; artificial part-isolation can destroy the meaningful context that aids chunking, and CLT's own isolated-interacting-elements refinement makes part-task isolation a mastery-contingent tool that should give way to whole-task practice. The blanket 'parts before wholes' curriculum spine is traditional sequencing wisdom without general empirical backing.
- Worked-example and multimedia effect sizes from STEM/lab studies transfer directly to teaching a natural language. — The worked-example (math), scaffolding (STEM), and multimedia (Mayer-authored lab) effect sizes have no demonstrated transfer to second-language acquisition; the explicit-knowledge-to-implicit-acquisition link they presuppose is theoretically defensible but, per instructed-SLA reviews, not empirically demonstrated. Importing these effect sizes as if they were L2 acquisition evidence is an unsupported leap (engagement/immediate-post-test learning vs durable acquisition).
